When we write mathematical proofs,
we often need to use
the definitions of the concepts
that appear in the statement.
In this video,
I will illustrate
how to prove that an example
does NOT satisfy a definition.
I want to prove that a function g,
defined by the equation g(x) = cos(x),
is NOT increasing
on the interval [0, π],
and I want to do it
directly from the definition.
Like in the previous video,
this proof is short
and perhaps not that important,
but I have a
bigger point to make:
I don't need to have learned
how to do a proof of this type 
in advance.
I don't need to memorize any method
as long as I know my definitions.
I know the definition
of "increasing,"
and I can simply use it
to get the definition
of "not increasing."
To say 
that g isn't increasing on an interval I
is to NEGATE 
that g is increasing on the interval I.
So I will write first the definition
of "g is increasing on I":
For every x_1 and x_2 in I,
IF x_1 is less than x_2,
THEN g(x_1) is less than g(x_2).
And then I need
to negate this.
So I want to write
that it is FALSE
that EVERY pair x_1 and x_2
satisfies the implication.
That is the same thing as saying
that there exists
ONE single pair x_1 and x_2
that does NOT satisfy the implication.
And finally,
to say an implication is false
is the same as saying
the "IF" part is TRUE
and the "THEN" part is FALSE.
If this feels a bit foreign,
I invite you to rewatch
the video where I explain
how to negate a conditional.
It is very short,
and I will link it
in the description.
And that's it; I have the definition
of "g isn't increasing on an interval."
If you have a little bit of practice,
perhaps you could have written
the negation right away in one go.
That would be fine!
If you need to break it into steps,
that's also fine!
Either way, now I know what I need to do:
I need to prove THIS statement.
This proof is going to be shorter
than the previous one.
Let's sketch a graph
of the function.
Let's write down the proof.
I need to prove there exists
a PAIR of elements
that satisfy these two inequalities.
The simplest way to do that
is to say WHAT the values
of x_1 and x_2 are,
and then verify the two inequalities.
And there are a lot
of choices I could take;
pretty much any pair
you choose here will work.
I'm going to choose 0 and π/2,
which is here and here,
and then I only need to verify
that 0 is less than π/2 -- Check!
and that g(0) is 1,
which is greater than
or equal to 0,
which is g(π/2) -- Check!
And that completes the proof!
And of course,
if I just wanted to prove
that the cosine
function is NOT increasing,
there are other things I could have done;
it wasn't necessary to do this.
But that wasn't the point;
the lesson learned
is that I can do the same thing
not only for "increasing,"
but for any other
mathematical concept.
