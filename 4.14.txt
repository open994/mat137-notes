In the previous two videos,
I defined the function arcsin,
I explained how to compute with it,
and I found its derivative.
In this video we will do the same thing
for the functions arctan and arccos.
I assume you have watched
the previous videos already,
so this time I will go faster.
First, let’s remember what
we learned about arcsin.
The function sin is not one-to-one,
so it does not have an inverse.
But we can restrict it to a smaller
domain to make it one-to-one.
We keep only the function represented by
this piece of the graph in red,
and then it is one-to-one.
We define arcsin as its inverse:
as the inverse of the restriction of sin
to the interval [-π/2,π/2].
This is the graph of the restriction
of sin, in blue.
And in red we have the graph of
its inverse: arcsin.
These are the two graphs together.
We can also summarize
that the functions are inverses of each
other with the following statement:
x = arcsin(y) IF AND ONLY IF y = sin(x),
as long as x and y are
in the right intervals.
(This is for all x between [-π/2,π/2]
and for all y between [-1,1].)
If this went too fast, I invite you
to watch the previous two videos.
Ok then! Let’s try to do
the same thing for tangent.
Here is the graph of tangent.
Notice that the domain is NOT
all of the reals.
tan is not defined
at any odd multiple of π/2.
Like sin, tan is not one-to-one,
and it does not have an inverse.
Like we did for sin, we can reduce
the domain to make it one-to-one.
It is standard to consider the function
defined by just this piece of the graph,
in red.
Then, it is one-to-one,
and we call its inverse ARCTANGENT.
This is to say,
we define arctan as the inverse of
the RESTRICTION of tan to (-π/2,π/2).
By contrast, remember that we used the
CLOSED interval to define arcsin.
Here is the graph, in blue,
of the restriction of tan.
And here is the graph, in red,
of its inverse arctan.
Here is both of them together.
Since they are inverses of each other,
we can obtain one by reflecting the other
with respect to the diagonal
of the first and third quadrants.
And just like we could summarize
everything we needed to know about arcsin
by this statement,
we can do the same thing for arctan.
We can write that
x = arctan(y) IF AND ONLY IF y = tan(x),
but this time the
intervals are different.
This equivalence is for all x between
-π/2 and π/2 NOT including the endpoints,
and for ALL real numbers y.
We have one more function to go.
Let’s look at cosine.
Here is the graph.
Like the other trig functions,
cos is not one-to-one.
But we can restrict the domain
to make it one-to-one.
In this case we cannot pick
a domain that is symmetric around 0.
It is customary to restrict cos
to the function defined by this
piece of the graph, in red.
And we call the inverse
of this restriction ARCCOSINE.
By now you should be
having a feeling of deja-vu.
So perhaps you can do the rest?
Can you define arccosine?
Can you sketch its graph?
And can you write
the corresponding statement,
like the ones I wrote
for arcsin and arctan?
To conclude the video,
let’s look at derivatives.
In the previous video I already found
the derivative of arcsin.
If you imitate the
exact same derivation,
you will obtain that arccos has
the same derivative as arcsin,
but with an additional MINUS SIGN.
Notice that the + for arcsin,
and the - for arccos,
are due to the specific choices
of restrictions that we made.
If you choose a different restriction
of sin or cos,
you may get a different result.
And finally, the derivative of arctan(x),
with respect to x, is 1/(1 + x²).
I recommend memorizing at least the
derivatives of arcsin and arctan.
They appear often in many situations,
and they are helpful.
For example,
they are useful when computing integrals.
Perhaps you are wondering:
what about the other trig functions?
What about arcsecant,
arccosecant, and arccotangent?
We could define them in a similar way.
However, in practice, these last
three functions are rarely used.
For most practical purposes,
arcsin, arccos, and arctan
are everything we need.
So I won’t say anything else about them.
