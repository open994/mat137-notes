In this video, I will introduce a family
of series (alternating series),
which are easy to work with, and I will
explain the theorem that justifies why.
First, the definition:
a series (an infinite sum)
is ALTERNATING,
when every term times
the next term is negative.
What this simply means is that
if a term is positive,
then the next term is negative,
and if a term is negative,
then the next term is positive,
so the terms I am adding alternate
between being positive and negative.
It doesn't matter
which one I start at.
For example, here is probably the
best-known alternating series:
the sum from n = 1 to ∞,
of (-1)ⁿ⁺¹/n.
For the signs that change
from positive to negative,
we normally represent them
by (-1)ⁿ or (-1)ⁿ⁺¹.
Let's see what the
first few terms here are.
When n = 1, I get 1,
when n = 2, -1/2,
and then + 1/3 - 1/4 + 1/5 - 1/6,
and so on...
I'm going to prove that this series
is convergent, and in the process,
we will actually see that a much larger
family of alternating series
are convergent (all of them).
I'm going to do this directly from the
definition of series (or infinite sum),
which is as a limit of partial sums.
Let's see what the first few
partial sums for the series are.
The first one (S₁) is simply 1,
then S₂ is 1 - 1/2,
then 1 - 1/2 + 1/3 and so on,
so by definition of series,
what I have to do is construct
this sequence of partial sums,
and then see whether it has a limit.
I'm going to argue geometrically,
I will draw a picture for
all of these partial sums,
and see what I get from there.
I start at 0.
First, I add 1,
then I subtract 1/2, (this is not
going to be at scale)
then I add 1/3,
(since 1/3 is smaller than 1/2 I'm going
to end up somewhere between S₂ and S₁)
now I subtract 1/4,
(since 1/4 is smaller than 1/3, I'm going
to end up being between S₃ and S₂),
and I keep going...
Based on the picture,
it looks like this sequence of partial
sums is going to be convergent.
I'm going to end up having
a limit somewhere in there.
What we've gotten, is that these partial
sums are ordered in a specific way.
The even partial sums
are ordered like this;
and they are all smaller
than the odd partial sums,
which are ordered like this.
I obtain this string of
inequalities from the picture,
but I can prove them
algebraically if necessary.
Every even partial sum is
smaller than the next one,
because to go from an even
partial sum to the next,
I have to add something,
and then subtract something smaller.
The same argument tells me why every odd
partial sum is greater than the next one.
Then, every even partial sum is smaller
than the next odd partial sum,
because I go from even to odd
by adding something positive,
and then I go from odd to even
by adding something negative.
So, I can accept this
string of inequalities.
Now can I go from here,
and use it to prove formally,
that the sequence of partial sums,
must have a limit?
I'm going to look at the
sequence of even partial sums,
and the sequence of
odd partial sums, separately.
What we've proven is that the sequence
of even partial sums is increasing,
and also, it's bounded above
(for example, by S₁).
So, it's monotonic and bounded,
and therefore, by the
Monotone Convergence Theorem,
it will be convergent.
But the very same thing applies to
the sequence of odd partial sums!
That one must be decreasing,
and is bounded below by S₂,
so by the Monotone Convergence Theorem,
I conclude that both are convergent.
However, I cannot stop here.
This doesn't guarantee (yet),
that the full sequence of
partial sums is convergent,
because the even terms
may be having one limit,
and the odd terms may be
having a different limit.
I need to justify that those two
limits are going to be the same.
So, let me give names
to those two limits.
How do I prove they are the same?
Well, I know how to write a relation
between the even and odd partial sums.
I can say that any odd partial sum,
is the previous even partial sum,
+ the term I have to add,
which in this case would be 1/(2n + 1).
And now, if I think of this as three
sequences with n as the index,
this has a limit, this has a limit,
and so does this,
so I can use the limit laws!
And I conclude the limit of the
sequence of odd partial sums (that's B)
= the limit of the sequence
of even partial sums (A),
+ the limit of the term, which is 0,
and so, the even and odd partial sums
converge to the same number.
And I want to say from there,
that therefore,
the full sequence of partial sums
also converges to the same number.
To do this, I need to
use a technical lemma.
(A general result that is
true for all sequences.)
If I have an arbitrary sequence,
if I assume that the sequence of even
terms and the sequence of odd terms
(those are two separate sequences),
IF I assume those two are both
convergent, with the same limit,
THEN I can conclude
that the full sequence
must also be convergent
to the same limit.
This can be proven directly from the
formal definition of limit of a sequence.
I won't do that here, but I apply this
result to the sequence of partial sums,
and thus I conclude, that
our original series is convergent.
I have not ONLY proven that the
original series was convergent,
because the same proof is going to apply
to a large family of alternating series.
So in fact, I have proven
a more general theorem.
Let me go back to the proof.
I'm going to look at exactly which
properties of the series I used,
and then I'm going to write
those properties as hypotheses,
and I will have a new theorem,
in which I can conclude,
that under those hypotheses,
the series is convergent.
What have I used?
First, I've used the fact that
the series was alternating,
and just writing it this way
doesn't guarantee it's alternating.
I have to say that the terms
I'm calling bₙ are positive.
I don't care where they start.
An alternating series is the one where I
can separate it as a sign that alternates
times something positive, so this just
means that the series is alternating.
Second, I needed the sequence
of partial sums to look like this.
I add something,
I subtract something smaller,
then I add something smaller, I
subtract something smaller, and so on...
That's what guaranteed that I had
this string of inequalities,
so I need the terms that I am adding and
subtracting to be getting smaller.
I need that sequence to be decreasing.
And third, just those two properties
are not enough.
Those two guaranteed that
I could conclude
the sequence of even partial sums,
and of odd partial sums were convergent,
but I needed to prove
those two limits were the same.
In the final step,
I use the fact that the limit of the
terms I am adding and subtracting is 0
(that that converges to 0),
so that's the third hypothesis.
And that's it!
Whenever I have an alternating series
(a series with those three properties),
I can immediately
conclude it is convergent.
It applies to a lot of series.
For example, I could look at the
sum from n = 2 to ∞, of (-1)ⁿ/ln(n).
And, yes! It satisfies 1, 2, & 3, so
by the Alternating Series Test,
it must be convergent.
