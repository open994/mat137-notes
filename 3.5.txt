In this video, I will prove that every
differentiable function must also be
continuous.
Or, as a formal theorem: given a number c
and a function f defined at and near c,
IF f is differentiable at c, THEN f is
also continuous at c.
The converse is not true. There are some
functions which are continuous without
being differentiable.
I will include a link in the description
to some examples.
As a reminder, saying that f is
differentiable at c, means that
the limit in the definition of derivative
at c, [f(x) - f(c)] ÷ [x - c], exists,
and is therefore a number.
And saying that f is continuous at c
means that the limit of f(x),
the function itself, equals f(c).
Notice that these are two different
limits.
Before doing anything else, it helps to
rewrite the definition of continuity.
Saying that the limit of f(x) is f(c)
is equivalent to saying that the
limit of f(x) - f(c) is 0.
This is because for the purpose of the
limit, the variable is x.
f(c) does not depend on x. It just
behaves like a constant
for the purpose of the limit.
We can justify this more carefully if
needed. IF the limit of f(x) is f(c),
THEN the limit of f(x) - f(c) will be
f(c) - f(c), which is 0.
And reciprocally,
IF the limit of f(x) - f(c) is 0,
THEN we can write f(x) as
(f(x) - f(c)) + f(c).
The first piece has limit 0, the second
piece has limit f(c),
and they add up to f(c).
So the two conditions are equivalent.
I will keep the second condition rather
than the first.
Now that we have agreed on definitions,
let's explore the relationship between 
differentiability and continuity.
IF f is continuous at c, let's see what
happens when I try to compute
the derivative f'(c) as a limit.
In the numerator, I get 0, and in the
denominator, I get 0 as well.
I get 0 ÷ 0, which is an indeterminate
form.
It means I have to do some more work
to figure things out.
In some cases, this limit will exist; in
others, it won't.
So the derivative f'(c) may
or may not exist.
By contrast, if f is not continuous at c,
when I try to compute the derivative f'(c)
as a limit, I get
"not 0" ÷ 0, so to speak.
This limit will never exist.
In the best case I could get a non-zero
number divided by 0,
which may be plus or minus infinity,
but the limit does not exist.
Okay, this was not a rigorous proof, but I
find this argument simple and convincing.
It explains why, if a function is
differentiable, then it must be continuous
as well.
But we can do better. We can write a
formal proof. Let's do it.
Assume f is differentiable at c. Then, I
write the limit of f(x) - f(c).
I multiply and divide by x - c.
Now I have the product of two functions,
so I use the limit law,
and the limit is the product of limits.
The first limit I recognize as the
derivative f'(c),
and the second limit is 0.
So the product is 0.
A very important remark: I am not saying
that 0 times anything is 0.
I am saying that 0 times a number is 0.
To use the limit law, I needed both limits
to exist.
The first limit exists because I am
assuming f is differentiable at c.
Otherwise, I would not be able to use
this argument.
And now, I have proven that the limit of
f(x) - f(c) is 0,
which means f is continuous at c.
And that completes the proof.
