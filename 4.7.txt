What is the derivative of
the natural logarithm?
The goal of this video is to obtain a
formula for the derivative of ln(x).
We already know that
the derivative of eˣ is itself.
But how can this help me?
Exponentials and logarithms
are INVERSES of each other,
so I know that eˡⁿ⁽ˣ⁾ = x,
FOR ALL positive numbers x.
This is helpful because it is a relation
between the two functions.
If I now take the
derivative of both sides,
I will get a relation between
the derivative of the exponential,
and the derivative of the logarithm.
These two expressions are equal, so
their derivatives must be equal as well.
On the left-hand side,
I have the derivative of e
to the power of a function.
By chain rule,
that will be itself, multiplied by
the derivative of the exponent.
And on the right-hand side,
I simply get "1".
Great! Look--this is
the piece I am looking for.
Before solving for it,
I can re-write eˡⁿ⁽ˣ⁾ as "x".
And finally,
I solve for the piece I want.
The derivative of ln(x),
with respect to x, = 1/x,
and that’s my final answer.
Notice that this same trick can be used
to find the derivative of ANY function,
IF we know the derivative of
its INVERSE function first.
