In this video, I will illustrate
with an example
how to find the intervals where
a function is increasing or decreasing.
I want to find the intervals where the
function defined by this equation,
f(x) = 8x^5 + 5x^4 - 20x^3,
is increasing or decreasing.
I invite you to pause the video and try to solve this problem.
Then, continue watching.
This function is a polynomial.
It is defined for all real numbers,
and it is nice!
It is always continuous, and it is
always differentiable.
Let's compute the derivative.
f'(x) = 40x^4 + 20x^3 - 60x^2.
To find out when the function is
increasing or decreasing,
I want to know when the derivative
is positive, negative, or zero.
This will be easier
if I can factor f'.
First, I notice I can factor 20x^2.
I am left with a quadratic.
I can always factor quadratics!
Either guess and check,
or with the quadratic formula.
In this case, it factors as
(2x + 3)(x – 1).
Now, the derivative will be zero
exactly when...
any one of these terms is zero.
So, when x = 0, -3/2, or 1.
In between any pair of
consecutive zeroes,
the derivative will stay either
positive or negative.
At this moment, this is not
a calculus question.
Figuring out the sign of this
product is a pre-calculus question.
So, use your favourite method.
For example, here is
one way to do it.
The derivative is the product
of these three terms
To figure out the
sign of the derivative,
I will figure out the sign of each
piece independently.
I have drawn here a
real number line,
and I have marked the three critical
points, -3/2, 0, and 1.
Let's look at the factors
one-by-one.
20x^2 is zero at x = 0,
and otherwise,
it is always positive.
2x + 3 is zero at x = -3/2.
It is positive for
greater values of x,
and it is negative for
smaller values of x.
Similarly, x – 1 is zero at x = 1,
is positive for greater values,
and is negative for smaller values.
Now, onto the derivative.
I know where the derivative is zero,
Between minus-infinity and -3/2, the
derivative will be
positive times negative times negative.
That's positive.
Between -3/2 and 0, it will be positive
times positive times negative.
That's negative.
And it continues negative...
...and then becomes positive.
That's the derivative. What can we
say about the function now?
Well, to the left of -3/2 (in the open
interval up to -3/2),
the derivative is positive, so the
function must be increasing.
Between -3/2 and 0,
it will be decreasing.
Between 0 and 1, decreasing,
and after 1, increasing.
Let's examine the critical points
more closely.
What happens at x = -3/2?
The derivative is zero, so I am going
to have a horizontal tangent line.
The function is increasing to the left,
and decreasing to the right.
So, that must look
like this.
That must be a
local maximum.
I do not need to compute second
derivatives of anything;
I know this just by looking at the
first derivative on the left and right.
And similarly, at x = 1,
the tangent line is horizontal,
the function is decreasing to the left,
and increasing to the right, so it
must look like a local minimum.
Now, the point x = 0 is the
most interesting one.
At x = 0 I also have a
horizontal tangent line.
But the function is
decreasing to the left,
and decreasing
to the right!
So, it's going to look
something like this.
It is what we will call an inflection
point – it is not a local extremum.
Finally, let's answer the
original question.
In which intervals is this function
increasing and decreasing?
I know that to the left of x = -3/2,
the function is increasing.
But I can actually say the function
is increasing on the interval
all the way to -3/2, INCLUSIVE.
This is because the derivative is
positive on the interior of the interval,
and at the endpoint the
function is continuous.
So, I am allowed to include
the endpoint as well.
Similarly, I can say the function
must be decreasing
from -3/2 to 0, including
both endpoints.
The derivative is negative
on the interior,
and the function is continuous
at the endpoints.
But the function is also decreasing
from 0 to 1.
So I can say it is decreasing all the
way from -3/2 to 1,
including both endpoints.
With the same argument, I can say
the function is increasing
from 1 to infinity, including 1.
And that's my final answer!
Remember that to
include an endpoint
in an interval of monotonicity, I only
need continuity at the endpoint.
Finally, notice that, yes, I am
including 1 in both of these intervals.
That's OK!
It's not a contradiction.
The function cannot be increasing or
decreasing at a point, only an interval.
In this interval, it is decreasing.
On this one, it is increasing.
I have already answered
the original question:
on which intervals is the function
increasing or decreasing?
But with all this information,
we can go one step further, and
sketch the graph of the function.
I have three critical points.
I can evaluate f at those points,
and include them in the graph.
And then, I know what the graph
looks like at each of those points,
and in between them.
If I want a precise graph, I will have
to compute a few more things,
like the intercepts with the axes,
for example.
But as a first approximation,
this is pretty good!
