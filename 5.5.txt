In this video, I will present the statement
of Rolle's Theorem, and its proof.
Rolle's Theorem fits HERE in
our big picture.
It is an intermediate step that will allow us
to prove the Mean Value Theorem.
That is the reason to study it.
The theorem says that if we have
a function f
defined on a closed and bounded
interval [a, b],
and there are certain hypotheses,
we can conclude that there
must be at least
one point in the interior
with derivative zero.
What are the hypotheses?
First, we need the function to be continuous
on the full interval, including the endpoints.
Second, we need the function to be
differentiable on the OPEN interval,
not including the endpoints.
Finally, we ask that f(a) = f(b).
As you know, differentiability implies
continuity, so the first two conditions mean
that f must be differentiable in the
interior of the interval,
but at the endpoints, it only needs
to be continuous.
Perhaps at this moment, you think this
theorem looks a bit dry and uninteresting.
I think I agree.
But remember, Rolle's Theorem will allow
us to prove something more important,
and that is why we care. 
Not because of Rolle's Theorem itself.
In order to understand this theorem
better,
I want to look at a few examples
of graphs of functions.
I think the examples will clarify why
all three hypotheses are there,
and why the theorem makes sense.
After that, I will still write an actual proof.
But a discussion with examples will
probably be more enlightening.
Okay...how can I conclude that a function
must have a point with derivative zero?
The third condition is perhaps the
simplest to understand.
It says that the value of the function at its
two endpoints is the same.
If I try to draw the graph of a
nice function with this condition,
perhaps it goes up first, and then down,
and has a local maximum with
derivative zero...
...or perhaps a local minimum...
...or perhaps both.
By contrast, if the value of the function
at the endpoints is not the same,
it could be increasing everywhere,
and then it may not have
any point with derivative zero,
like this example.
So this condition makes sense.
It is there to try to force the
existence of a local extremum.
But it is not enough by itself.
The function could have a local extremum
at a CORNER.
The derivative would not exist at that point.
In this example, the function is NOT
differentiable on the interior of the interval.
It fails the second hypothesis.
Finally, even if the function is 
differentiable on the interior,
and it takes the same value at the endpoints,
we could have this (silly) example.
This function has a jump at an endpoint –
it is not continuous –
and it does not have any point
with derivative zero.
So there we have it.
This is the theorem.
All three hypotheses are necessary.
Hopefully, it is now clear how the
three of them come together.
You may wonder, "why not require
differentiability on the full closed interval,
including the endpoints, and be done
with it?"
That's a good question.
That would ALSO make the theorem true.
However, in general, it is good
to write our theorems
with hypotheses as WEAK as possible,
because then,
we can use the theorem in more cases,
and so the theorem is more useful.
That is why I will leave the theorem with
these three hypotheses.
Okay. After all that discussion, I still need
to write a proper proof.
It will be relatively short.
Here it is.
First, f is continuous on [a, b],
including the endpoints.
This means I can use the 
Extreme Value Theorem,
and f must have a maximum
and a minimum.
Now, I will consider two cases.
Either at least one of them (the maximum
or the minimum)
occurs at an interior point, or both
of them occur at endpoints.
In the first case, if an extremum happens
at an interior point,
it is also a local extremum.
By the Local Extreme Value Theorem, its
derivative either is zero or does not exist.
But since the function is differentiable on
the interior, the derivative is ZERO.
And in this case, we are done!
We found what we wanted.
Now for the second case.
If the function has both the maximum and 
the minimum at endpoints,
since the value at the two endpoints
is the same,
the function must be constant.
But then the derivative is zero everywhere,
so there is at least one point with
derivative zero. There are many!
And we are done in this case too!
That completes the proof.
I will finish with some remarks.
First, notice the parts I just boxed in red.
I have used all three hypotheses
in the proof.
This is good, because I knew the theorem
would not be true without all of them.
Second, notice the parts I just
boxed in blue.
I have used the Extreme Value Theorem
and the Local Extreme Value Theorem
in the proof.
This agrees with the big plan we have.
We are finally ready to move to the
Mean Value Theorem.
But before we do so, Rolle's Theorem also
has some other,
smaller applications of its own.
I will present one of them
in the next video.
