example how to compute the derivative of
a function directly from the definition.
Here is the exercise.
f is defined by f(x) = 4x - x².

First, I write the definition
of derivative as a limit.
I chose the version as h approaches 0.
I could have chosen the other one.
I have an explicit form for f.
I can substitute it in the function,
then expand some of the terms in the numerator,
then group similar terms and simplify a bit,
and now I can divide the numerator by h.
How convenient!
After all the simplifications,
I am left with a continuous function.
To compute the limit,
I simply evaluate at h = 0.
And so the answer is 2.
This wasn't too bad. However, if the
function is not as simple as a polynomial,
these may get messy. And besides,
it will be tedious if I have to go through
these every time I compute a derivative.
Is there a better way?
Yes there is. The differentiation rules.
They are a set of rules that makes it much faster to compute derivatives.
We still need to prove them from the definition,
but after we prove them once,
we can use them as much as we want,
and then we do not have to come back
to the definition every time.
