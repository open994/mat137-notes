The Big Theorem establishes a hierarchy
among the most common
families of sequences.
It tells us which sequences grow
faster than which ones.
I assume you have already
watched the previous video,
where I introduced the theorem,
I explained the notation,
and I showed how to use it.
(There is a link in the description.)
In this video,
I will present a proof.
As a reminder, the Big Theorem says that
logarithms are much smaller than powers,
which are much smaller
than exponentials,
which are much smaller
than factorials,
which are much smaller
than power-exponentials.
This is actually
four different claims.
In order to prove the theorem,
I will need to prove all four claims.
For example, I need to prove that the
lim_(n → ∞) (a logarithm)/(a power) is 0,
and so on...
The first two claims are easier to prove.
The sequences involved
(logarithms, powers, and exponentials),
can be extended to functions defined with
domain of all the positive reals,
not only the naturals.
Therefore, we can use L’Hôpital’s Rule.
I will leave this as an exercise.
I invite you to try to
write down the proof,
it's just a standard use
of L'Hôpital's Rule.
The last two claims
are more interesting.
They involve n!, which is only defined
when n is a natural number.
Therefore, we cannot prove
them using L’Hôpital’s Rule.
We will have to use other tricks.
Let’s do that.
I will begin with the third claim.
I want to prove that an exponential
is much smaller than a factorial.
Let’s fix a real number c > 1,
and let’s define pₙ = cⁿ/n!.
I want to prove that the
sequence {pₙ} has limit 0.
Here is the key observation.
If I write the (n+1)st term of the
sequence, pₙ₊₁, it is (cⁿ⁺¹)/(n+1)!.
I can factor out a c from the numerator,
and (n+1) from the denominator,
and I am left with cⁿ/n!, which is pₙ.
So, this sequence satisfies a recurrence
relation: pₙ₊₁ = (c/(n+1))⋅pₙ.
And the whole proof is
based on this identity.
I will proceed in 3 steps.
First, notice that the sequence
is eventually decreasing.
For large values of n,
pₙ₊₁ is smaller than pₙ,
because pₙ₊₁ is
a number less than 1 times pₙ.
This is true whenever
n is large enough (n ≥ c),
so the sequence is indeed,
eventually decreasing.
Second, notice that the sequence
is bounded below, by 0.
And now, since the sequence is eventually
decreasing and bounded below,
it must also be convergent.
This is due to the Monotone
Convergence Theorem for sequences.
Great! Now that I know 
the sequence is convergent,
let’s give a name to the limit.  
Let’s call the limit L.
And once we know this limit exists,
it is going to be easy to prove it is 0.
Let’s look back at
the recurrence relation.
I can use the limit
laws and say that:
lim_(n → ∞) pₙ₊₁
= lim_(n → ∞) c/(n+1) ⋅ lim_(n → ∞) pₙ.
I can only do this because I know
all three limits exists.
The first limit is L,
the second limit is 0,
and the third limit is L,
so L = 0⋅L, and L = 0,
which is what I wanted to prove.
Great!
I want to repeat
an important observation.
All the steps were necessary.
The last part of the argument, (proving
that L is 0 from the recurrence relation)
only works if I already know that
the sequence has a limit.
Otherwise, if I only
wrote the last part,
I would not be proving that
the sequence is convergent to 0;
I would only be proving that the sequence
is either convergent to 0, OR divergent.
We have one more claim to prove
before finishing the video.
I want to prove that a factorial is
much smaller than a power-exponential,
or in other words,
that lim_(n → ∞) n!/nⁿ = 0.
This will be short.
I am going to write n!, as 1⋅2⋅3⋅...⋅n,
and nⁿ as the product of n copies of n.
Then, I group each term in the numerator,
with one term in the denominator.
And now, I'm just going to
separate the first term,
so I have 1/n, times a bunch of factors.
Notice that every factor
inside the bracket is ≤ 1,
and therefore,
the whole product is ≤ 1/n.
I have concluded that n!/nⁿ
is sandwiched between 0 and 1/n.
Do you see where this is going?
We know that the
constant sequence {0},
and the sequence {1/n},
both have limit 0;
therefore,
by the Squeeze Theorem,
the sequence sandwiched between
them ALSO has limit 0.
That is what I wanted,
and this completes the proof.
