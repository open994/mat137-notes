In general,
proving that a series is
convergent can be quite difficult.
So far I have found
very few series
whose value could be
computed from the definition.
There is a family of series
that are a bit easier to work with:
positive series.
It may still be hard to find
the exact value
of these infinite sums,
but at least we have
a simpler way
to determine whether they are
convergent or divergent:
whether they add up
to a number or not.
In this video, I will
define what they are,
and I will explain
why we like them.
First, some definitions.
A positive series
is simply a series
where all the terms I am
adding are positive.
That's it.
I can also define negative series
in a similar manner.
And I can also define,
for example,
non-negative series,
where all the terms I am adding
are positive or zero.
And so on.
Why are positive series
easier to work with?
A series is defined as
the limit of a sequence...
the sequence of partial sums.
An arbitrary series may be
convergent or divergent.
If it is divergent,
it may be divergent
for all the reasons
that a limit may not exist.
Perhaps it is divergent
to infinity,
or divergent to
minus infinity,
or divergent "oscillating,"
which just means
divergent in a different way.
That is for an arbitrary series.
But what about a positive series?
For a positive series
there are only two options.
It can only be convergent,
or divergent to infinity.
It cannot be divergent
to minus infinity
because I would need
negative terms for that.
It cannot be divergent "oscillating"
because I would need both
positive and negative terms for that.
I think it's intuitive that
these are the only two options
for a positive series.
If we want to write
a rigorous proof,
we can do that as well,
and it is quite quick.
Here is the proof.
As you know, the value 
of a series is defined
as the limit of the sequence
of partial sums.
What happens if I assume
the series is positive?
In that case, the sequence
of partial sums is increasing,
because I just keep adding
positive terms.
Or in other words,
because the difference between
two consecutive partial sums
is one of the terms I am adding,
which is positive by assumption.
Then we can use the 
Monotone Convergence Theorem.
An increasing sequence is
either bounded or unbounded.
If it is bounded, then
it is convergent.
That is the Monotone
Convergence Theorem.
If it is unbounded, then it is
divergent to infinity.
That's it!
That completes the proof.
As you see the proof consists
of putting into formal language
the intuitive argument
I gave earlier.
This leads us to a new
piece of notation
that is very convenient
for positive series,
because it is simple.
But beware!
We use these for positive
series ONLY!
We are going to write that
a positive series is either
infinity, or less than infinity.
We will write infinity
when it is divergent.
That is the only way for
positive series to diverge.
We will write that a series
is less than infinity
to mean it is convergent.
Again, we only do this
for positive series.
An arbitrary series that
is not infinity
may still be divergent
in a different way.
But a POSITIVE series
that is not infinity
must be convergent...
it must add up to a number.
One more note.
So far I have been talking
about positive series only,
but everything I have said also
works for non-negative series.
If all the terms I am adding
are either positive or zero,
I can still draw the same conclusions.
Even further,
everything also works for
EVENTUALLY non-negative series.
If the first five terms
are negative,
and after that they are all
positive (or zero),
we still draw the same conclusions.
Okay.
So, positive series simplify
things. How is this helpful?
To prove that a positive series
is convergent,
(to prove that it represents
a number)
I only need to show that
it is NOT infinity.
That's all.
Instead of proving that
a limit exists,
I only need to prove a limit
is not infinity.
That is less work!
There are three theorems
that utilize this fact:
the Integral Test,
the Basic Comparison Test,
and the Limit Comparison test.
In the next few videos,
I will introduce these results
and illustrate how to use them
with examples.
They all have something
in common.
They can only be used
on positive series,
and they all rely on the fact
that a positive series can only
be convergent or infinity.
