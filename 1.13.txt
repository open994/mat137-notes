In the past videos,
I have shown how to prove
that something satisfies,
or does not satisfy a definition.
In this video, I will write 
a proof of a theorem a bit more complex
than merely checking a definition.
I want to prove
that the sum of increasing functions 
is increasing.
To do this, I am definitely going to need
to use the definition of "increasing."
There will be no way around it this time.
Before I do anything, I'm going to try
to rewrite this as a formal theorem;
it's going to make proving it easier.
I'm going to be talking
about two functions and their sum.
So let's give them names.   That will help.
I'm going to say, "let f and g
be functions on an interval I,
"and I'm going to call their sum h."
And the theorem says
that the sum of increasing functions
is increasing.
In other words,
IF f and g are increasing on I,
THEN h is ALSO increasing on I.
That is what I want to prove,
written as a formal theorem.
Not every theorem
is explicitly written like this.
However, the majority of theorems,
even though often only in disguise,
are like this; 
they're an if-then statement;
there is some hypothesis 
and a conclusion.
The part that came before the if-then
is what I'm going to call simply
"the set-up";
all I did there
was introduce the notation,
because that makes
stating the theorem easier.
The "IF" part is the hypothesis,
and the "THEN" part is the conclusion.
The conclusion is what I WANT to prove.
Whereas the hypothesis? 
I don't need to PROVE it.
On the contrary,
the hypothesis is something
I may ASSUME is true during my proof.
So, I am going to try
to prove that h is increasing on I,
and in the process,
every time I need it,
I will simply assume
that f and g are increasing on I.
In order to prove
that h is increasing on I,
I will begin by reminding myself
of the definition:
For every x_1, x_2 in I,
IF x_2 is less than x_2,
THEN h(x_1) is less than h(x_2).
And I've done something bad:
This IS what I want to prove,
but I didn't SAY that EXPLICITLY.
My reader doesn't know that;
my reader CAN'T read my mind.
So my reader is going to believe
that I am CLAIMING this to be true,
that I am starting from my conclusion,
which is not allowed.
So how about I SAY explicitly
that this is what I WANT to show?
I've used the abbreviation WTS;
you don't have to use it.
If you prefer, simply write
"my objective is,"
or "I am trying to prove that,"
or ANYTHING to indicate clearly
that this is what you're trying to prove,
NOT that you're claiming
that is true.
Now, let's move on to it.
I need to prove something
for EVERY pair of elements x_1 and x_2,
so we know we write
"let x_1 and x_2 be in I."
This means that I am FIXING them,
and that they are generic;
I didn't chose them.
I don't know what they are exactly.
And now, I want to prove an implication.
I do that by ASSUMING
x_1 is less than x_2;
I assume the "if" part,
and then I want to SHOW the "then" part,
so let's write that explicitly:
"I want to show
that h(x_1) is less than h(x_2)."
What to do next?
I don't know the explicit form of h;
I don't know what h looks like.
All I know is that h is f + g.
But I ALSO know
that f and g are both increasing.
So let's use that.
Since I know that f is increasing on I
and x_1 is less than x_2,
I can conclude
that f(x_1) is less than f(x_2).
Similarly, since I know
that g is increasing on I
and x_1 is less than x_2,
I can conclude
that g(x_1) is less than g(x_2).
Now, I have two inequalities 
with f and g,
I want something about h, 
and h is f + g.
So I can simply ADD both inequalities,
and I get that f(x_1) + g(x_1)
is less than f(x_2) + g(x_2).
But now look what we have!
f(x_1) + g(x_1) is h(x_1),
and f(x_2) + g(x_2) is h(x_2).
And I have proven exactly what I needed,
so this completes the proof!
Notice how I treated VERY differently
f and g on one hand, and h on the other.
For h, I PROVED that it was increasing;
but for f and g, I ASSUMED
and I used throughout my proof
that they were increasing.
That's the difference
between a hypothesis and the conclusion.
