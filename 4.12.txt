In this video,
I will define the function ARCSINE,
and I will explain 
how to compute with it.
Some books, and most calculators,
refer to this function as "SINE INVERSE."
I prefer not to do that.
ARCSINE is NOT the 
inverse function of sine
It is a bit more subtle.
This is the graph of SINE.
As you know,
the domain is all of the reals,
and the range
is the interval from -1 to 1.
However,
this is not a one-to-one function.
For example, sin(0) = sin(π).
Two different inputs 
produce the same output.
Sine is not one-to-one,
and therefore it 
does not have an inverse function.
That’s it.
That is why I prefer the name "arcsine",
rather than "sine inverse."
So, how do we define arcsine if it
isn't just the inverse?
Sine is not one-to-one,
but we can restrict it
to a smaller domain,
hopefully preserving the full range,
so that it becomes one-to-one.
For example,
we can keep just the part of the function
defined by this piece of the graph,
in red.
The restriction would then be one-to-one.
Notice that this is an arbitrary choice. 
Instead of this piece,
I could have chosen this piece,
or this other one,
or many others,
they are all equally good.
Any of those restrictions is 
one-to-one and has an inverse.
Since we do not have a reason
to prefer any of them,
it is common to choose the one with
the domain symmetric around 0,
the one in the graph here.
We give a special name to its inverse:
we call it arcsine.
But again,
this is just a convention,
and any of the other choices could
serve a similar purpose.
And that’s the definition of arcsine!
It is the inverse function of:
the RESTRICTION of sine
to the interval [-π/2,π/2].
NOT merely the inverse function itself!
Here is the graph of
the restriction of sine.
Arcsine is its inverse,
so this is its graph:
it is the reflection
of the previous one,
with respect to the diagonal
of the first quadrant.
And here are the two graphs
on top of each other.
The domain of the
restriction of sine is [-π/2,π/2],
which becomes the range of arcsine,
and the range of sine is [-1,1],
which becomes the domain of arcsine.
Here is a second way to state
exactly the same thing:
that the two functions are
inverses of each other.
x = arcsin(y) is equivalent
to y = sin(x),
as long as x and y
are in the right intervals.
x, the argument of sine, must be
between -π/2 and π/2,
and y, the argument of arcsine,
must be between -1 and 1.
This statement contains
all of the information you could
ever need about arcsine.
Any property of arcsine
will follow from this definition,
as long as you know
the sine function well.
For example,
what is arcsin(1/2)?
By definition,
if I call arcsin(1/2) "t",
this means that sin(t) is 1/2,
and that t must be between
-π/2 and π/2.
Now use your knowledge of trigonometry,
and draw a picture if needed.
There are many angles with a sine of 1/2,
but only one in this region: π/6.
And that’s the answer!
Here is yet a third equivalent way
to write this definition.
If we compose sine and arcsine,
we should get the identity,
(because they are inverses of each other)
but ONLY on the correct intervals.
So, arcsin(sin(x)) will always be x,
as long as x is between -π/2 and π/2,
and sin(arcsin(y)) will always be y,
as long as y is between -1 and 1.
Be careful. These identities are not true
for numbers OUTSIDE of these intervals.
For example, what is sin(arcsin(2))?
It is undefined!
2 is not in the domain of arcsine.
arcsin(2) is not a real number.
But what about arcsin(sin(2))?
This IS well defined.
This IS a number.
But I cannot use the identity above!
The answer is NOT 2.
How do I solve this problem then?
There are a few ways. Here is one.
Think of sin(2) simply as a number.
We know that, by definition,
arcsine of a number = t is equivalent to
asking that sin(t) = that number,
with the extra requirement that
t must be between -π/2 and π/2.
That’s all.
So I have reduced this to
a trigonometric question.
I am trying to find a number t,
between -π/2 and π/2,
which has the same sine as 2.
Now, use any trigonometry you like
to solve this problem:
maybe trigonometric identities,
or look at the unit circle,
or observe the graph of sine...
I will try to solve it by looking
at the graph of sine.
Here in red is a sketch
of the graph of y = sin(x).
I am looking for a number t
which is between -π/2 and π/2,
(so it should be somewhere here)
whose sine is equal to sin(2).
Let’s find sin(2) on the graph.
2 is somewhere between π/2 and π.
Perhaps 2 is here,
and then sin(2) is this y value.
The value t that I am looking for
must be between -π/2 and π/2,
and have the same sine value as 2.
That means it must be exactly here.
That’s it.
Can we write an equation for t?
Looking at the symmetry of the graph,
I think that this distance,
must be the same as this distance.
The first distance is (π/2) - t,
and the second distance is 2 - (π/2).
I can solve for t,
and I get that t = π - 2.
And that’s the answer!
I have proven that
arcsin(sin(2)) is π - 2.
Here is a challenge for you:
what is arcsin(sin(6))?
This is a very good exercise...
...to test whether you have
understood the definition of arcsine.
I will give you a hint:
the answer is neither 6,
nor π - 6.
