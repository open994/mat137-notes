The Substitution Rule is a method
to compute antiderivatives.
It is just an easy-to-use algorithm to
make things a bit simpler,
and a bit more systematic
than guess-and-check.
In the next video, I will present a few
examples of how to use it.
However,
in this video,
I want to explain where the Substitution
Rule comes from and why it works.
As motivation, I will first investigate
some examples by guess-and-check,
then I will derive the general rule,
and finally I will explain
the notation that we commonly use.
To begin, let’s have a look at
these two antiderivatives.
First, I have the antiderivative
of x⁴, with respect to x.
I know this is (1/5)⋅x⁵,
+ an integration constant.
Why? We can check it.
The derivative of (1/5)⋅x⁵ is (1/5)⋅(5x⁴)
which is x⁴, as I wanted.
That’s easy.
Next, what is the
antiderivative of (ln(x))⁴?
Perhaps, it is something
like (1/5)⋅(ln(x))⁵?
We can check it.
The derivative of (1/5)⋅(ln(x))⁵
is (1/5)⋅5(ln(x))⁴...
times (1/x)!
Don’t forget the chain rule!
That’s unfortunate.
So this function is not an
antiderivative of this function.
However, if I had an extra 1/x in the
integrand, then it would be correct!
The 1/x was essential!
(Again, because of the chain rule.)
I can generalize this a bit more:
what is an antiderivative of
(g(x))⁴ for ANY function g?
Well, I don't know,
but IF instead I have (g(x))⁴⋅g'(x),
THEN an antiderivative is
(1/5)⋅(g(x))⁵.
Why? Let’s check it!
The derivative of (1/5)⋅(g(x))⁵,
is (1/5)⋅5(g(x))⁴⋅g'(x).
(Again, chain rule.)
And this will work for any function g,
as long as g is differentiable.
Let’s generalize it even more
in the form of a nice theorem.
IF I know that an
antiderivative of f is F,
THEN, what is an
antiderivative of f(g(x))?
I don’t know, but an antiderivative
of f(g(x))⋅g'(x) is F(g(x)).
Why is that?
The IF part means that
the derivative of F is f.
The THEN part means that the derivative
of F(g(x)) is f(g(x))⋅g'(x).
Notice that the second statement...
...follows directly from the chain rule
PLUS the first statement.
This is sometimes called
the Substitution Rule,
but I think it should be called
the backwards chain rule.
All I did was take the chain rule,
(which is a rule about derivatives),
and reversed it to get
a rule about antiderivatives.
I used the chain rule backwards!
To make this into a proper theorem,
I should introduce my variables:
f, g, and F are functions, and I need to
assume that F and g are differentiable.
And that’s it.
That's the Substitution Rule.
This is the theory.
In practice, we try to use this theorem
in an efficient and algorithmic way...
...that simplifies computations,
rather than just guess-and-check.
I will explain this next.
Assume we know that an
antiderivative of f is F,
and we want to compute
∫f(g(x))⋅g'(x)dx.
I want to think of g(x)
(the argument of f), as the new variable.
For that purpose, I will give it a name:
I will call it "u";
because I want to think that I am
actually integrating f(u),
with respect to u,
so I need to write "du" here.
If I do this, it seems that I am
re-writing g'(x)⋅dx as du.
This is what we call a substitution:
u = g(x),
and du = g'(x)⋅dx.
For now, this is just notation.
There is a way to give a precise
meaning to the differential
and to make this rigorous,
using so-called differential forms,
but that is beyond the scope of
this course, so I won’t go there.
I will just think of it as notation.
Ok then, if I do this substitution,
the integral becomes ∫f(u)du.
Now I find an antiderivative,
which by assumption
is F(u) + an integration constant.
And after integrating,
I UNDO the substitution,
and I replace u back with g(x).
The justification for this notation is
that it gives us the right result.
(This is the same result
I derived earlier.)
And the point of the notation is
that it streamlines the process,
so we don't have to use
so much guess-and-check.
This will become clearer with an example.
Let's go back to the original example.
I want to compute ∫(ln(x))⁴⋅(1/x)dx,
but this time I will
do it using substitution.
First, I notice that my function is
written in terms of ln(x),
and that its derivative
(1/x) is also here.
This suggests that I try the
substitution: u = ln(x).
If I do that, I also need to
substitute du as (1/x)dx.
Great! This works because
I already have 1/x here!
Then, this becomes ∫u⁴du.
I know how to do this.
The answer is (1/5)⋅u⁵,
plus an integration constant.
And now that I have completed the
integration, I undo the substitution:
and I am back to (1/5)⋅(ln(x))⁵,
plus the integration constant.
In general,
every time the integrand is written in
terms of a function (like ln(x) here),
and the derivative is already
in the integrand (1/x in this case),
using a substitution like this
is a good idea.
And after doing the substitution,
we hope to find an easier integral
that we can complete.
As to why the method works, that is the
whole explanation I gave earlier.
It all boils down to the chain rule.
