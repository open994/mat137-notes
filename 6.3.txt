An optimization problem is an application
of calculus to a physical situation
where I want to make a certain quantity
as large as possible
or as as small as possible.
Here is an example. I want to build an
open box that will have four side walls
and a bottom but no top,
with a square base.
The volume of the box must be 500cm³,
and I want to spend as little as possible
on the materials.
What dimensions should I choose for
the box?
At this moment I invite you to pause the
video and try to solve it
before you continue watching.
Even if you cannot fully solve it, my
explanation will be much more useful if
you spend some time trying to understand
the statement and setting it up.
First, we need to model the problem.
That is, we want to convert the word
problem
into a precise mathematical question.
In this case, I'm looking for a question
of the form,
I want to find the maximum or the minimum
of this specific function
on this specific domain,
and figuring out what that function is
and what the domain is
is the modelling part of the problem.
This is what the box may look like.
I'll begin by giving names to the
dimensions of the box,
since that is what the problem
is asking about.
I'm going to call x this side of the base,
and since the base is a square,
x is also the length of this side.
The height is a different dimension, so I'll call it y.
In this question I want to spend as
little as possible on the materials.
What that means is that I want the total
surface of all the five walls I'm building
to be as as small as possible, 
because that will use
the smallest possible amount of material.
So I want to minimize the total area.
I'm going to call the total area s for
surface. So let's figure out what it is.
First, we have the bottom of the box, and
the bottom of the box is a square,
and it is a square with side length x,
therefore the area here is x².
And then I also have the other four walls.
Now let's look at the right wall, for
example.
Here the base is x, but the height is y, 
so this wall has area xy,
and the same is true of the wall on the 
back, and the one on the left,
and the one on the front.
So this adds 4xy 
to the total surface area.
That's the quantity I want to minimize.
However, x and y are not independent
variables; they are related.
There is an extra constraint because we
want the total volume of the box
to be a fixed quantity.
We want the total volume to be 500cm³.
Now the volume of a box is the product of
the three dimensions,
so it's going to be x·x·y or x²y.
I think I should also say that x must be
a positive number
and y must be a positive number,
otherwise the problem 
doesn't make any sense physically.
And that's it. Now I have a function with
two variables but with a constraint.
I find it easier to calculate 
the maximum or the minimum
of a function with a single variable, without constraints,
and one way to attack this 
is I can use this constraint
to write one of the variables 
in terms of the other.
In this case I think it's a bit easier to
write y as 500/x²,
and then I can use this and plug it in the
original expression for the surface area
to get s in terms of x and x only.
If I do that, I get that s is going to be
x² + 4x·500/x²,
or we can simplify that as
x² + 2000/x.
And there is my function. My goal is to
find the minimum of this function,
and this is in the domain when x is any
positive number.
That's the only constraint I have,
now that I have removed y.
Now that we have set up the problem,
the second part is the easier part:
use calculus to find the minimum
of the function on the correct domain.
This function is differentiable on the
domain, so to find the minimum
I look at when the derivative is
zero or positive or negative.
The derivative of s with respect to x is
going to be 2x - 2000/x².
Now, I'm interested in when this is zero
or positive or negative,
and I find it easier if I
reduce to common denominator.
I can also factor a 2,
and in the top I get x³ - 1000.
And once I put it this way it's clear:
the derivative is zero exactly when x³ is

That's the only critical point.
At this point, I know many students like

This is the only critical point, but we're
looking for the minimum,

Perhaps it is the minimum,

Perhaps the function

So we have to do a little bit more
justification to say
why the minimum occurs there.
What I'm going to do is look at how the
function behaves to the left
and to the right of 10.
I know that at 10, the derivative is 0.
Now when x is greater than 10,
the derivative is positive,
and when x is smaller than 10,
the derivative is negative.
Therefore, the function s is decreasing
all the way to 10
and increasing all the way after 10.
And now yes, with this information I know

Not a local minimum only, just the global

So after doing this little justification

And therefore my answer is x = 10cm, and

y was 500/x² which comes out to be 5cm.
And these are the dimensions that I need
to use in my box
