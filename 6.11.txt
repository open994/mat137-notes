Of all the limit
indeterminate forms,
1^infinity is probably the most
controversial one.
At least, it is the one I have seen
students argue about the most.
In this video, I want to explain why
1^infinity really is an indeterminate form,
and why our intuition about it
may be a bit off.
First, let's remember what it
means to say that 1^infinity
is an indeterminate form.
It means that if I am trying to 
calculate a limit
like the one here at the bottom,
the limit of a function f(x) raised to
the power of another function g(x),
and if I know that separately,
the limit of the base, f, is 1,
and the limit of the exponent
g is infinity,
if I know those two things, what
can I say about the full power?
Well, then I cannot draw any
conclusion about the full power.
The limit of f(x)^g(x) under these
assumptions could be anything.
That's what it means to say that
1^infinity is an indeterminate form.
No more, no less.
Notice that at no point am I actually
raising the number 1 to any power.
It's going to be numbers CLOSE to 1
raised an exponent that is large.
That's all.
Probably the best way to prove this,
the best way to prove that
under these hypotheses,
I cannot draw a conclusion,
is to offer the battery of examples
where the hypotheses are true,
but the outcome (the final limit)
takes many different values.
Let's do exactly that.
I'm going to construct a family
of examples the following way.
Let c be any real number.
I'm going to look at
the following limit.
The limit as x approaches 0
on the right
of (e^x) raised to (c/x).
I think of this as a function
raised to another function.
So let me look at the
two functions separately.
As x approaches 0, e^x
is approaching 1.
As x approaches 0 on the right,
c/x is going to be approaching...
well, if c is positive,
it will be infinity.
If c is negative,
it will be minus infinity.
I'm going to exclude
the case when c = 0.
So all together, for every value
of c I have a different example,
and all those examples
are of type
1 raised to either
plus or minus infinity.
So, what's the value of this limit?
The nice thing about this example
is that we can compute it very easily.
I can use the properties
of exponentials
and rewrite these
functions as follows.
e^x raised to c/x
is simply e^(x * c/x).
Now, I can simplify this.
Turns out that this is just
a constant function.
So the limit is e^c.
There you have it. By choosing
different values of c,
I can construct examples of
limits of type 1^infinity
that have many different values.
In fact, I can make the final limit
be any positive number I want.
So, this justifies saying that
1^infinity is an indeterminate form.
Just from knowing I have 1^infinity,
I can't know what the
final answer will be.
So, why is it hard to accept that
1^infinity is an indeterminate form?
I believe the answer comes from
taking 1^infinity a little bit too literally.
It's not ONE to the infinity,
it's numbers CLOSE to 1 raised
to very large exponents.
Look at these five examples
here on the top.
If we were computing
the limit as x goes to infinity
of 1^x, this one
definitely would be 1.
This is just the constant
function 1 in disguise.
Now, let's compare with the others.
If I am computing the limit as
x approaches infinity of 2^x,
we know that's going
to be infinity.
The same is true for any other
constant base that is greater than 1.
1.01^x as x approaches infinity
goes to infinity.
It just goes there much more slowly.
It takes a long time to get large.
On the other hand, the limit as
x goes to infinity of 0.1^x,
well, that is 0.
The same is true for any other
base smaller than 1.
0.99^x as x goes to infinity
also goes to 0.
It just takes a long time
to get close to 0.
However, in practice,
when I look at 1^infinity,
I don't have a constant base.
What I have now is a
function that changes,
and is approaching 1, perhaps
above, perhaps below, perhaps both.
I have another function g(x) that is
changing and is getting large.
So, what's happening
to f(x)^g(x)?
On the one hand, since f(x) is
approaching 1,
and 1 raised to anything is 1,
we could say that the base, f, is
trying to make the power get to 1.
But on the other hand, 
since g(x) is changing,
and g(x) is approaching infinity,
if f is slightly greater than 1,
g(x) is saying:
"anything slightly greater than 1
raised to infinity is infinity."
So, g is trying to make
the answer infinity.
Or, if f(x) is slightly smaller than 1,
g is saying:
"anything smaller than 1
to infinity is 0."
So, g(x) is trying to make
the answer 0.
There lies the tension.
The base wants the
answer to be 1,
the exponent wants the answer
to be either infinity or 0.
That's why we get an
indeterminate form,
and that's why the actual outcome,
the actual value of the limit,
depends on the specific example.
