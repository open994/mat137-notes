The fundamental theorem of calculus
establishes some connections
between definite integrals and
antiderivatives,
two concepts that are defined separately
and in principle are different,
but they relate through this theorem.
And it has two parts.
The first part answers the question
what can we say
about any function defined as an integral,
and the second part answers the question
how do we compute
a definite integral quickly.
I'm going to focus for now
just on the first part.
Here is the precise
statement of this theorem:
I begin with an interval I
and a real number a on I that I fix.
And then I also have a function f
with domain I.
Then I define a new function F
by this equation:
F(x) is the integral
from a to x of f(t)dt.
So F is a function
that is defined as an integral.
If this notation is confusing,
I suggest you rewatch the previous video
in which I explain
how to define functions as integrals.
Now the theorem says that if
f is continuous -
and notice that f being continuous
guarantees that it will be integrable,
and therefore this expression makes sense
and F is well-defined,
so this is a valid way
to define a function.
If f is continuous,
then F is differentiable,
and the derivative of F is f. In other
words, F is an antiderivative of f.
We can summarize this with words
by saying that
a function defined as an integral
is an antiderivative, and that is it.
This is the connection between the notion
of antiderivative and integral,
which again were defined separately,
differently,
but this theorem tells us
that they are related.
We can summarize this theorem with an
equation. It's often written like this:
I can say that for any real number x
on the domain I, F'(x) is equal to f(x).
Or equivalently, the derivative
with respect to x of F(x) is f(x).
And now I can replace F(x) with its
definition, and I get this equation,
which is the common way to summarize it.
The derivative with respect to x of the
integral from a to x of f(t)dt
equals f(x).
And it is important to notice how we use
notation in this equation.
Both the left side and the right side
are functions of x, not of t.
t is just the variable of integration,
it's not relevant and disappears.
Both sides are functions of x.
On the left hand side I have a function of
x, and the derivative with respect to x,
still a function of x.
And so on the right hand side,
I also need to have a function of x.
If you attempt to change an x into a
t or vice versa on one side or the other,
not only will it be wrong,
but it will make no sense.
