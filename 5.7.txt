As a reminder, this is what
Rolle's Theorem says.
I begin with a function that is defined
on an interval [a, b]
(closed and bounded,
including the endpoints).
If I assume three conditions:
I assume that the function is continous
on the full interval (including endpoints),
is differentiable on the open interval
(not including the endpoints),
and that the value of the function at the
endpoints is the same.
Then, necessarily, there must be a point
in the interior
where the derivative is zero.
For the mean value theorem, I want to
get weaker hypotheses.
I'm going to keep the first two.
They are true for most common functions, so they won't cause trouble.
But I want to remove the third hypothesis,
and I want to know: if I only have the first
and second ones,
what kind of conclusion can I draw?
The conclusion will, necessarily, be different.
My first task is going to be figuring out:
if I only have those two,
what can I possibly conclude?
Once I figure it out and prove it,
that is what we are going to be calling
the Mean Value Theorem.
Let's begin with an example.
I'm going to look at the function defined
by this graph.
This function certainly satisfies
the first two hypotheses.
It is continuous on the interval [a, b],
including the endpoints,
and differentiable on the interior.
But the values at the endpoints
are different.
So, I cannot use Rolle's Theorem
directly on it.
The trick is going to be to look
at the line that goes

I am going to look at this red line,

In fact, it is going to give us the statement
of the Mean Value Theorem.
This is what the Mean Value Theorem
says.
I begin with a function defined on an
interval [a, b]
(closed and bounded, including the endpoints).
If I assume the function is continuous
on the full interval,
and differentiable only on the interior,
and nothing else,
just those two assumptions,
then I can conclude that there must be
a point on the interior
where the derivative has exactly the
expression I got on the previous page.
And that is it.
That concludes the statement.
I have not proven it yet.

But before that, a few comments.
The Mean Value Theorem doesn't
say that this equation is true.
The Mean Value Theorem says that
if these hypotheses are true,
then there is a point in this domain
that makes the equation true.
So, it's not merely an equation.
It is ALL of this.
IF these hypotheses are true,
THEN there exists a value in this domain
that makes this equation true.
