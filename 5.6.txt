In this video, I will present an application
of Rolle's Theorem.
It can help us answer the question:
How many zeroes does a function have?
As a reminder, the main reason we have
learned of Rolle's Theorem
is as an intermediate step
to get to the Mean Value Theorem.
Nevertheless, it has some other 
applications of its own,
and this is one of them.
First, let's make sure we use
notation correctly.
I say that the number c is a ZERO
of a function f when f(c) = 0.
In other words, we talk about a zero
of the function f,
and a SOLUTION of the equation
f(x) = 0.
We never talk about a solution of
a function;
a function by itself is not an equation.
Now that we have the right terminology,
how do we find the number of zeroes
of a function?
A common strategy uses two steps.
First, use the Intermediate Value Theorem
to prove that the function
has AT LEAST a certain number of zeroes.
Second, use Rolle's Theorem to prove that
the function has
AT MOST a certain number of zeroes.
And if the two bounds agree, we're done.
For example...
Consider the function g defined by the
equation: g(x) = x^6 + x^2 + x – 2
How many zeroes does it have?
First, I will use the Intermediate Value
Theorem.
This should be familiar.
I already presented a similar example
when I introduced the IVT.
The function g is a polynomial, so it is
continuous on all the reals.
I can plug in a few values,
and I notice that...
...g(-2) is positive...
...g(0) is negative...
...and g(1) is positive.
A continuous function that switches
from positive, to negative, to positive
must have at least TWO zeroes.
By the Intermediate Value Theorem, there
must be at least one zero between -2 and 0,
and there must be at least one zero
between 0 and 1.
But are there more?
The IVT can never answer this.
It can only tell us that there are at least
a certain number of zeroes.
Rolle's Theorem will help us with the rest.
Let's pause the example for a moment,
and think more generally.
Consider a function f.
Assume for simplicity that f is continuous
and differentiable everywhere.
Now, assume f has two zeroes:
x_1, and x_2.
Then, I can use Rolle's Theorem for the
function f, on the interval [x_1, x_2],
because the values of f at the endpoints
of the interval are the same.
Rolle's Theorem says that
the derivative f'
must have a zero between x_1 and x_2.
We have drawn an important conclusion.
Between any two zeroes of a function,
there must be at least one zero
in the derivative.
Now, let's take it one step further.
Assume f has three zeroes instead.
x_1 smaller than x_2, smaller than x_3.
Then, there must exist a zero in the
derivative between x_1 and x_2,
and another one, between x_2 and x_3.
Similarly, if f has four zeroes,
then the derivative must have at least
three zeroes...
one between each pair of consecutive
zeroes of f.
We have drawn a second
important conclusion.
The derivative f' must have at least as
many zeroes
as the function f does, minus one.
Or, equivalently, the number of zeroes
of the function is AT MOST
the number of zeroes of its derivative,
plus one.
This is the same inequality
as conclusion 2.
This is important enough that I am going
to call it a theorem.
The only hypothesis we had assumed
was that
f must be continuous and differentiable
on an interval.
This is exactly what I needed!
I now have a way to say that the number
of zeroes of a function is
at most SOMETHING.
Let's go back to the original example,
and we can see if we can finish it.
Remember, I was trying to estimate
the number of zeroes of this polynomial.
I already knew it had at least two zeroes.
Now I would like to prove
it has at most two zeroes.
The theorem I obtained suggests
that I should look at its derivative.
The derivative is...
g'(x) = 6x^5 + 2x + 1.
...and I don't know how many
zeroes g' has.
Look instead at the
second derivative.
The second derivative is x equals
30x^4 + 2.
Aha!
x^4 is always non-negative.
So, the second derivative
is always strictly positive.
So the second derivative has no zeroes.
How does this happen?
The theorem I just proved said the
number of zeroes of a function
is at most the number of zeroes of its
derivative, plus one.
Let's use it on g' as the function!
The number of zeroes of g'
is at most the number of zeroes of its
derivative g'', plus one.
That is zero plus one,
which equals one.
Now, let's use the theorem again
with g as the function.
The number of zeroes of g
is at most the number of zeroes of its
derivative g', plus one,
which is less than or equal to
one plus one...
...which is two.
Perfect!
To summarize, the IVT says this function
has at least two zeroes.
Rolle's Theorem says it has at most
two zeroes.
So, it has exactly two zeroes.
