In this video, I will explain the meaning
of the mathematical quantifiers,
which are these two symbols.
This symbol, an upside down "A,"
means "for all," or "for every."
And this symbol, a backwards "E,"
means "there exists," or "there is."
And a quick note: in mathematics,
whenever we say "there exists,"
or "there is,"
we ALWAYS mean AT LEAST one;
we don't mean exactly one.
This is in all contexts.
For example, if I say
that an equation "has a solution,"
it means "has AT LEAST one solution."
If you want to say something different,
say so explicitly.
Let's look at some examples
of these quantifiers in statements.
The first statement says:
for every real number x,
x^2 is greater than or equal to 0.
Or in other words,
every real number
satisfies that its square 
is greater than or equal to 0.
And we know that that is true;
that's a property of squares.
The second says that for every integer x,
x is greater than pi (π).
And that is false.
Some integers are greater than π;
some integers are smaller than π.
For example, 1 is smaller
and that's enough to say 
that this statement is false.
Okay next, let's look at some examples
of statements with "there exists."
The first statement says:
there exists a real number x,
x^2 equals 5.
And I'm going to pause here for a moment.
This statement reads really weird.
As an English sentence,
it's not quite grammatically correct.
We kind of need a connector in between; 
we cannot just say it as I've read it.
And the connector 
we normally use in math here
is "such that."
Now, this didn't add anything
mathematically;
it didn't change the meaning.
It just made it make sense grammatically.
And I think it makes
the sentence easier to read.
You don't have to write it.
Logicians never write it;
they write a comma.
But I think it helps make the sentence
easier to read.
Now let's look at the meaning:
there exists a real number 
such that x^2 = 5.
And yes;
there's one such real number x.
This is true. In fact, there are two.
But one is enough.
So for example, we can say that
x = -√5.
That's a number that satisfies that.
Now, the second statement says:
there exists a real number x
such that x^2 = -1.
That is false;
no real number satisfies that.
To finish, I want to compare
these two statements with something else.
If I simply write "x^2 = 5" 
and nothing else,
is this true or false?
Well I want to say: neither.
The answer is that this is meaningless.
In logic, we could say 
that this is not a statement;
it's a predicate.
But don't worry about that.
It just means that, 
as it stands; with nothing else,
this is neither true nor false.
Unless we have previously agreed
on the meaning of x
(for example, perhaps we fixed x
to be a certain value),
this means nothing.
Because what are we saying?
Are we saying this about
ALL values of x somewhere?
Or that there
is a specific value of x in some set?
Or are we saying,
"pick your favourite value of x
that satisfies that"?
We need context.
So if you are ever tempted
to begin your definition,
or your theorem, or your proof
with a statement like this
without having first 
FIXED the value of x
or explained what you mean by that?
Don't do that, because that 
will be neither true nor false;
it will be meaningless,
which is MUCH worse.
