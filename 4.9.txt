In this video, I will introduce the
method of logarithmic differentiation.
We use this to take
the derivative of a function,
that is raised to the power
of another function.
I will illustrate logarithmic
differentiation with an example.
We consider this function f, defined by:
f(x) = (cos(x))ˢᶦⁿ⁽ˣ⁾,
and I want to take its derivative.
The first thing to notice,
is that this function is NOT
merely a power or an exponential.
What do I mean by that?
A power would be a function that
depends on x only in the base,
and the exponent is a number.
For example, x³,
and I know how to take its derivative:
that's what the power rule tells me.
I could also have a power composed
with a different function,
for example: cos³(x),
which is simply (cos(x))³.
I know how to take the derivative
of this function as well:
I just combine the power rule
with the chain rule.
On the other hand,
an exponential would be a function
that depends on x in the exponent,
but the base is a constant (is a number),
for example: 2ˣ.
Again, I have a formula to take
derivatives of functions like this:
I know the derivative of 2ˣ is 2ˣ⋅ln(2).
And again, I could have a function like
this composed with something else,
for example: 2ˢᶦⁿ⁽ˣ⁾.
To take its derivative, I would use
this rule, together with chain rule.
The problem with my original function is
that it isn't any one of those.
Neither the base nor the exponent is a
constant, they BOTH depend on x.
So, none of these rules
(by itself) tell me what to do.
I am going to present two methods
to take care of this,
which are actually
the SAME method in disguise.
The first method consists of
using this identity,
which allows me to re-write
any power exponential,
as an exponential with base "e".
Why is this useful?
Because I know how to take the derivative
of an exponential with base e!
In our case, this function
can be re-written as: eˢᶦⁿ⁽ˣ⁾⋅ˡⁿ⁽ᶜᵒˢ⁽ˣ⁾⁾.
And once I have it this way,
I know how to take the derivative:
this is an exponential of a function.
So, the derivative will be itself,
times the derivative of the exponent.
And now, I can re-write this
as it was originally.
And to take this derivative,
I will use the product rule.
And that's it!
Perhaps we can simplify it a little bit,
but that was the first method.
The second method is the one we call
"logarithmic differentiation."
Here is the function whose
derivative I want to take.
The difficulty is in having this exponent
here; so, before I do anything,
I'm going to begin by taking
logarithms of both sides.
This is a good idea because logarithms
simplify many operations.
They turn products into sums,
quotients into differences,
and powers into products.
So, using the properties of logarithms,
I can re-write this as:
ln(f(x)) = (sin(x))⋅ln(cos(x)).
And look,
the exponent has disappeared entirely!
Now that the exponent is gone,
I can take derivatives of
both sides much more easily.
On the left-hand side,
I no longer have just "f", I have ln(f).
But, I can take the derivative
using implicit differentiation.
And on the right-hand side,
I have to take the derivative of this
product, so just use the product rule.
If this feels familiar,
it's not an accident!
It's exactly the same derivative that
I computed in method one.
And finally, I can solve for f'(x),
which is what I wanted.
And that's it!
The answer is the same as in method one.
