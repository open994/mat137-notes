I derived an explicit formula
for the Taylor polynomials and
the Taylor series of a function.
In this video, I will illustrate
them with some examples.
As a reminder,
this is what we know.
The n-th Taylor polynomial
of a function f at the point a
is given by this sum:
It is a polynomial,
of degree at most n.
The Taylor series for
the function f at a
is given by this sum.
It looks similar, but instead
of a finite sum,
it is an infinite sum,
or power series.
You can think of it as a
polynomial of infinite degree,
if you prefer.
We know that Taylor polynomials
are good approximations
for f, near a.
The higher n, the better
the approximation,
and the Taylor series is an
even better approximation.
There is one special,
important case.
When the Taylor series is
centred at zero,
we call it a
MACLAURIN series.
That's just a name.
Finally, we say that
a function is ANALYTIC
when it is equal to its
Taylor series.
For analytic functions,
the Taylor series is not
an approximation.
It is exactly the function.
But we do not know yet
which functions are analytic.
The actual definition of analytic
is a bit longer.
I will explain it in the next video.
I need to specify the domain
where this must be true.
But, for now, I will just go
with this imprecise idea.
When I talk about
analytic functions,
I am talking about functions that
can be written as power series.
Here's a spoiler:
Most common functions,
including exponential
and trigonometric functions,
are analytic!
They can be written as power series.
It will take us a bit of work
to get there.
For now, in this video,
I will just concentrate on
finding the Maclaurin series
for some functions,
without proving yet that the
function equals the series.
As a first example,
let's compute the
Maclaurin series
of the exponential
with base e.
I am going to define
f(x) as e^x.
We know that the derivative
of this exponential is itself.
Therefore, all the derivatives
are the same function,
and when I evaluate
any derivative at zero,
I get 1.
Now, here is the general formula
for the Maclaurin series
of a function.
In this case, all derivatives
at zero are 1,
so I get the sum
from k = 0 to infinity
of x^k/k!
The first few terms are
1, plus x, plus x^2/2!, plus x^3/3!
...and so on.
But, there is more!
This function IS analytic.
I am postponing the proof
until later.
So, the function equals
the Maclaurin series.
e^x equals this infinite sum,
for ALL values of x.
What if...instead of
the Maclaurin series,
I wanted the Taylor series
centred at any other point c?
Well, we could repeat the calculation,
and evaluate the derivatives
at c rather than at 0.
In this case it would
be quite similar.
But there is also an
alternative trick
that is worth knowing.
If I want to write e^x in terms
of powers of (x – c)...
(that is what a Taylor series
centred at c will do)
...I can use the subtitution
u = x – c.
Then, I just need to write
the function
in terms of powers of u instead.
What do we mean by this?
Under this substitution,
e^x becomes e^{c+u},
or e^c (which is a constant)
times e^u,
and then I can use the
Maclaurin series for e^u.
After that, I simply undo
the substitution,
and I get that e^x
equals this power series
centred at c.
This trick is quite convenient,
and it works for
many functions.
It is the reason why
most of the time,
we only work with
Maclaurin series.
If we ever need a Taylor series
centred at another point,
we can re-interpret it
as a Maclaurin series
with a substitution.
As a second example,
I want to find
the Maclaurin series
for sine.
In this case, not all
derivatives are the same,
but there is still a pattern!
I begin with sin.
The first derivative is cos,
the second derivative is -sin,
then -cos, and the fourth
derivative is sin again.
We are back where we started.
The four derivatives repeat
in a cycle.
I need to evaluate these
functions at zero.
sin(0) = 0.
cos(0) = 1.
...and I see the pattern now.
The derivatives at zero
are going to be zero,
one, zero, minus one,
and then they repeat.
Let's use this information
and try to write
the Maclaurin series for sin.
We know this is
the general formula.
All the even derivatives
disappear.
For the odd derivatives,
the coefficients
alternate between 1 and -1.
So, I get  x/1! –x^3/3!
+ x^5/5! – x^7/7!, and so on.
There is a nice way
to rewrite this sum.
Since I only get terms
with odd degree,
I can rewrite the exponent
of x as 2m + 1,
summed from m = 0 to infinity,
and the coefficient is 
(-1)^m/(2m + 1)!
But, there is more.
sin is also analytic.
Again, I am postponing the proof.
So, this is not just the
Maclaurin series for sin...
it is sin itself!
sin(x) equals this power series,
for all values of x.
As an exercise, I invite you
to repeat
the same calculation for cosine,
instead of for sine.
It's very similar, and you will
obtain a new Maclaurin series.

three Maclaurin series
we know so far.
