I remind you that the fundamental theorem
of calculus provides us with connections
between definite integrals
and antiderivatives,
two concepts that are defined separately
but that are related through this theorem,
and it has two parts,
it is actually two theorems.
The first part,
which we already know,
tells us what we can say
about any function defined as an integral,
and the answer is
that it's going to be an antiderivative.
The second part of the theorem,
which is the point of this video,
tells us how can we
quickly compute a definite integral,
and it's going to be
through antiderivatives.
So let's focus on this second part.
Here is the precise statement
of the theorem.
Let a and b be real numbers,
a less than b, and I take a function f
that is continuous on the interval [a, b]
(it must be continuous).
The theorem tells me how to compute
the definite integral from a to b of f.
And what it specifically says is that
first, take any antiderivative G of f,
and then the definite integral
will simply be
the antiderivative evaluated at b minus
the antiderivative evaluated at a.
That's it.
This theorem doesn't tell me
how to find an antiderivative,
and for some functions that will be easy,
for some functions that will be hard.
That's a different problem.
But if we have a way
to find an antiderivative,
this will allow us to compute
the definite integral very quickly.
It certainly is much faster
than computing the definite integral
from the formal definition,
in terms of upper sums and lower sums
and so on.
There is a piece of notation associated
with this theorem that we use a lot,
and it is the following.
Since we will often be writing
something like this, G(b)-G(a),
we can rewrite it
or abbreviate it this way:
G(x), a vertical bar,
on top x=b, on bottom x=a.
And this just means take the function G
evaluated at b
minus the same function evaluated at a.
And sometimes we also write it
simply like this:
G(x), vertical bar,
on top b, on bottom a.
This final notation
is a little bit ambiguous,
but we tend to use it a lot if
there is no ambiguity in specific cases.
