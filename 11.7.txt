The “Big Theorem” is probably the most
important theorem about sequences
that you will learn
in a calculus course.
It helps us compute
many limits very quickly.
In this video, I will explain the
statement of the theorem,
and how to use it.
In the next video,
I will write a proof.
As motivation, let’s look at a type of
limits that we understand very well:
rational sequences.
For example, let’s say I need to compute
lim_(n → ∞) (n³+2n+1)/(5n³+3n²).
You probably know how to do this.
When I have a quotient of polynomials,
the only thing that
matters is the biggest power.
In the numerator,
the biggest power is n³,
in the denominator,
the biggest power is also n³,
so this is the same as the
lim_(n → ∞) n³/5n³, which is 1/5.
That is the quick calculation, but let’s
remind ourselves of WHY this works.
To justify this method, the trick is
always to factor the biggest power.
Let’s factor n³ in both the
numerator and denominator.
I can cancel these terms,
and let’s see what we have left...
As (n → ∞), 1/n² has limit 0,
And so does 1/n³ and 1/n.
Now use the limit laws,
And that is how we get 1/5.
The key observation here is that the
lim_(n → ∞) n/n³,
is the limit of 1/n²,
which is 0.
(And the same is true for the
limits of the other quotients.)
And that is why, when I have a sum
of an n³ term and an n term,
the n term is irrelevant, and
the n³ term determines the answer.
(The same happens with the
other “irrelevant” terms.)
This is useful.
Can I generalize it?
I would like to do the same thing with
other sequences, not just powers.
I want to compare other terms and decide
which ones are more important,
and which ones can, so-to-speak,
be ignored when added together?
That’s what the Big Theorem is about.
First I need a new definition.
We will use this new concept
only for POSITIVE sequences.
I am going to write that
a sequence aₙ is << a sequence bₙ,
To mean that the lim_(n → ∞) aₙ/bₙ = 0.
This is just new notation.
And when this happens, I am going to say
that “aₙ is much smaller than bₙ”,
or that “bₙ is much larger than aₙ”.
For example, with this
new notation and definition,
I can say that n is much smaller than n³,
because the limit of the quotient is 0.
Now I have the tools
to state the Big Theorem.
The Big Theorem establishes
a hierarchy among sequences.
It says that a logarithm is much smaller
than any power with a positive exponent,
which is much smaller than any
exponential with base > 1,
which is much smaller than n!,
which is much smaller than nⁿ (which
I will call a “power-exponential”).
That’s the Big Theorem.
Why is this useful? We can use it
to compute many limits quickly.
Let’s look at an example.
I want to compute the
lim_(n → ∞) (eⁿ +2⋅n¹⁰⁰)/(ln(n) + 5⋅eⁿ).
According to the Big Theorem,
n¹⁰⁰ is much smaller than eⁿ,
and ln(n) is much smaller than eⁿ,
so the only terms that
matter are eⁿ and 5⋅eⁿ.
And this limit is 1/5.
That’s it!
Why is this?
We can justify this rigorously
from the Big Theorem,
like I did in the example
at the beginning of the video.
To justify this, I will factor the
biggest term (in this case eⁿ),
in both the numerator and denominator.
And let’s look at what I have left.
n¹⁰⁰/eⁿ has limit 0 as n → ∞.
That is exactly what
the Big Theorem says!
The same applies to ln(n)/eⁿ.
Now use the limit laws, and we get that
the full limit is 1/5. That’s it!
I hope you can see from this example
that there are many other limits
that we can compute very quickly,
using this theorem.
Of course, we still need to prove it.
The proof will be in the next video.
