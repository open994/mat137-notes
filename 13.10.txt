In this video, I will
explain the Integral Test;
a theorem that gives us a
relation between series and integrals.
It is motivated by this question.
I begin with a function f that is
continuous between 1 and ∞,
and I'm going to compare a series
and an improper integral.
Specifically, the sum
from n = 1 to ∞, of f(n),
and the improper integral from
1 to ∞, of f(x), with respect to x.
I want to know if there is
a relation between them.
This is a very natural question,
because series and improper integrals
are defined in a very similar manner.
A series (or infinite sum) is defined as
a limit of partial (or finite) sums.
Specifically,
I compute the sum of the same terms
but I stop at a certain cutoff k,
and then take the limit when k → ∞.
And the improper integral is
defined exactly the same way:
as a limit of proper integrals.
I calculate the integral
of the same function,
but I stop at a certain cutoff b, and
then I take the limit when this b → ∞.
And the similarities continue:
many of the theorems,
results, and properties,
of series and improper integrals
are very much the same,
so it seems natural to ask if there is
a relation between these two notions.
Finding a relation would be useful,
because there is a tool for integrals
that we don't have for series:
we can calculate the value
of an improper integral
if we have an expression for
the antiderivative of f.
So once we find
this relation,
we'll be able to use that tool to
also say something about series.
I'm going to make two assumptions
to simplify things.
The question can be asked
for any continuous function f,
but I'm going to assume
the function f is positive,
because in that case,
I know this guarantees that both the
series and the improper integral
can only be either convergent,
or divergent to ∞.
There is not a possibility
of oscillation,
and that simplifies things a lot,
because to prove one
of them is convergent,
all I have to prove
is that it is not ∞.
So if it is bounded above,
it's necessarily convergent.
Second, I'm also going to assume
the function is decreasing.
Most of the interesting functions
we use for series or integrals
are actually decreasing, or at least
eventually decreasing,
which will be enough.
That's going to simplify the calculation
in a way I will explain soon.
Here is the strategy.
I will first find a relation between
proper integrals and finite sums,
and then I will take the limit.
I'm going to do this, geometrically.
I've chosen here the graph
of a generic function
that is positive, decreasing
and continuous;
and in green I have represented
the area under the curve,
which is the integral
from 1 to 5 of this function.
I want to find a relation between
this integral and a finite sum.
We know that we can bound
this area by bigger rectangles,
which I'm going to draw in blue.
Specifically,
I can say that the integral
is ≤ the area of these blue rectangles.
Every rectangle has width 1,
the height of the first
rectangle is f(1),
the height of the second is f(2),
and then f(3), and f(4),
so the area of all the rectangles is the
sum of f(n) when n goes from 1 to 4.
So, this picture summarizes that
the integral from 1 to 5,
is ≤ the sum of f(n),
when n goes from 1 to 4.
Notice that in order
to be able to do this,
I needed the function
to be decreasing,
so that the values of the
functions at the left endpoint
of every small interval,
would be the maximum of the
function on that interval.
I can also do the very same thing to
bound the area below instead of above.
Instead of using these blue rectangles,
I can use these red rectangles,
and then say that the green area is ≥
the area of these four red rectangles.
Again, every rectangle has width 1,
the first one has height f(2),
then f(3), f(4), and f(5),
so the sum of the area
of the red rectangles,
is the sum of f(n),
when n goes from 2 to 5.
So now this picture summarizes,
that the integral from 1 to 5
of the function f,
is ≥ the sum of f(n),
when it goes from 2 to 5.
When I put both things together,
I get that this partial sum,
(which is the area of the red rectangles)
is ≤ this proper integral, in green,
which is ≤ this partial sum, which is
the area of the blue rectangles.
So let's take those two inequalities
and see what we can do with them.
First, I chose "5",
but I could have chosen any other integer
and done the same calculation,
so let's replace 5 with "N" that
represents an arbitrary integer.
Next, I am going to take
the limit as N → ∞, and I get this:
that the series, from 2 to ∞,
is ≤ the integral from 1 to ∞,
which is ≤ the series from 1 to ∞.
I can do this because
the function f is positive,
and therefore,
for these three limits,
each one of them either exists
(is convergent), or is ∞.
That's what allows me
to pass to the limit,
with the understanding that some
of these things could be ∞.
What I got here,
is that the improper integral
is sandwiched between two series.
Now the two series (the red and
blue series) are different,
but the red series is
a tail of the blue series,
(they only differ on the first term)
so even though they
have different values,
what we know is that if one of them is
convergent, then so is the other.
At this point,
I can now use a comparison between
improper integrals and infinite sums,
and get the result I'm looking for.
If the series (if the blue
series) is convergent,
then the improper integral
in green is bounded above,
and therefore must be
convergent as well.
And if the improper integral in green is
convergent (is a finite number),
then the red series is bounded above, and
therefore must be convergent as well.
I'm using the fact (again)
that since the function is positive,
this has to be convergent
to a finite number, or ∞.
I have concluded that one is convergent
IF AND ONLY IF the other is convergent,
and that is the theorem.
The theorem, called the Integral Test,
says the following:
I have been working with
functions defined starting at 1,
but I could have defined
them starting at any point,
so let's say a is a real number,
and let's say f is a function that is
continuous, positive, and decreasing,
from a to ∞.
We need continuous so that
the integral is always defined,
positive, so that we know everything
is either convergent or ∞,
and decreasing, so that I could write a
comparison between the partial sums,
and the proper integrals.
In that case,
the theorem says
that the improper integral is convergent,
IF AND ONLY IF the series is convergent.
(And yes, to say convergent, I don't
need to say where the series starts.)
We're going to use the following notation.
We're going to write a tilde between
the integral and the series,
to indicate they're either both
convergent, or they're both divergent.
(Meaning they both behave the same way.)
This does not mean that they are equal.
If they are convergent, they are both
numbers, but they are different numbers.
It just means they are either
both convergent (both a number),
or none of them are,
and that's the theorem.
