In this video, I will write a proof of the
Local Extreme Value Theorem.
In the previous video (linked in the description),
I already introduced the theorem.
I explained the statement and I gave
some motivation.
In this video I will ONLY write the proof.
As a reminder, this is the theorem.
It says that IF a function f has a local
extremum at a point c,
which is an interior point to the domain,
not an endpoint,
THEN the derivative f'(c) is zero or
does not exist.
This is exactly the statement I wrote in
the previous video.
Time for a proof.
There are actually two proofs!
One if f has a local maximum, and one if
it has a local minimum.
I will write a proof only for a local 
maximum. The other case is very similar.
We know that the derivative f'(c)
is defined as 
lim_(x → c) [f(x) – f(c)]/(x – c)
I want to prove that EITHER this limit
does not exist or equals zero.
To do so, I am going to assume
the limit exists,
and then I will prove that it must be zero.
That will be enough.
I will examine what happens to this limit 
as x approaches c
on the right and on the left separately.
First, the denominator.
When x approaches c on the right,
(x – c) is positive.
When x approaches c on the left,
(x – c) is negative.
Then, the numerator.
The numerator has the same sign
in both cases.
[f(x) – f(c)] must be negative or zero
as x approaches c on both sides.
This is my definition of local maximum.
f(c) must be greater than or equal to f(x)
when x is close to c.
As a consequence, on the right of c, the
QUOTIENT is negative or zero,
and on the left of c, the quotient is
positive or zero.
What can we say about the side limits?
The limit on the right must be negative or zero.
A priori, it could also not exist, but we
are assuming the full limit exists,
so the two side limits must also exist,
and be equal.
...and the limit on the left must be
positive or zero.
For the two side limits to exist and be
equal, they must both equal zero.
That is it!
That completes the proof.
I want to add some comments.
First, notice that we needed c to be an
interior point,
because we needed to examine the limit
on both sides of c.
If c had been an endpoint of the domain,
the proof would not have worked.
Second, this was a proof only for a
local maximum.
I invite you to think about how to prove
the result for a local minimum.
It is very similar.
You only need to change the direction of
some of the inequalities.
Everything else works the same way.
Finally, I remind you that I have proven
this theorem as a first step
in our long journey towards
the Mean Value Theorem.
But this theorem is useful on its own.
In the next video, as an application,
I will explain, with an example, how to use
this result to find
both local and global maxima and
minima of certain functions.
