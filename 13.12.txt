This is a short video about
the comparison tests
for convergence of
series (or infinite sums).
And it is short,
because everything I want to say about
comparison tests for series
can be summarized by
this one sentence:
comparison tests for series,
and comparison tests for improper
integrals, work exactly the same way.
In various previous videos,
I introduced the comparison tests
for improper integrals, slowly.
I explained the statement of the
theorems, the proofs, why they work,
and some examples of the
calculations we normally do with them.
I will add a link in the
description of this video
to the videos where I did all
that for improper integrals.
Well everything works exactly
the same way for series,
so I guess I could end it here.
But just a quick summary
of what these theorems say.
First, the main result these
theorems are based on is the following:
if I have a positive series, it may only
be convergent or divergent to ∞,
other types of divergence
are not possible.
And as a consequence, if I want to prove
that a positive series is convergent,
all I have to do is prove
that it is not ∞.
This is at the basis of all comparison.
This is what allows us
to write comparison tests.
So, here is the statement
of the two theorems.
First: the Basic Comparison Test.
For this test, I begin with two series
(the sum of aₙ and the sum of bₙ),
and I assume that one
is bounded by the other.
I assume that the bₙs are ≥ the aₙs,
which are ≥ 0.
I'm saying for every natural number,
but as we know,
it could be enough if
this was true eventually,
(if this was only true
for large values of n)
because for the
purpose of convergence,
only what happens to the tail
of the series matters.
So I assume that bₙ bounds the aₙ.
And then, if the sum of the aₙs (the
smaller series) is divergent to ∞,
then the sum of the bₙs (the bigger
series), must also be divergent.
On the other hand,
if the sum of the bₙs (the bigger series)
is convergent (is a number),
then the sum of the aₙs must
also be convergent (also a number).
As you see, it's exactly the same
theorem as for improper integrals.
Here is the second theorem:
The Limit-Comparison Test, for series.
In this case, I begin again with two
positive series (sum of aₙ, sum of bₙ),
but instead of trying to
bound one with the other,
I compute the limit of
the quotient of the general terms.
So I calculate the lim_(n → ∞) aₙ/bₙ,
and I need to assume this
limit exists (is a number),
and that this number is positive.
In that case,
the conclusion is that the two series
must either be both convergent,
or both divergent, that's it!
Again, it's the same theorem.
For the proof, I invite you
to rewatch the proof
of the Limit-Comparison Test
for improper integrals,
and convince yourself that you
could take the very same proof,
replace every integral with a series,
and it will work exactly the same way.
As for examples of how to use the two
theorems, again,
I invite you to rewatch the examples
I explained for improper integrals,
and convince yourself that every example
would have worked equally well,
if we had been talking about series,
rather than improper integrals.
