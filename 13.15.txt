Is the sum of sin(n)/n^2

This series is not positive.
sin takes both positive

But it is also not

The pattern of positive and

Then...I'm out of ideas!
What can I do?
Would it help to first look

Maybe there is a relation

This second series is positive,

But, will it help me answer

More generally, I want

It would be great if we could
reduce the problem

series to ONLY positive series.
There is a theorem that
helps us with this,
although only half of the time.
This is called the
Absolute Convergence Test.
Given an arbitrary series,
IF the same series with
absolute values is convergent,
THEN the original series
is convergent as well.
I am going to represent this
in a little table.
An arbitrary series may be
convergent or divergent.
The same series with
absolute values,
since it's a positive series,
can only be convergent
or infinity.
A priori, this combines into
four possible cases.
The theorem says that one of
these four cases is impossible.
We cannot have a
divergent series
that becomes convergent
with absolute values.
I will postpone the proof

For now, I want to explain:
how is this theorem useful?
How do we use it in practice?
Let's say we are studying
an arbitrary series.
We want to know
if it is convergent.
In general, the series won't be
positive, negative or alternating,
so we do not have many tools
that we can use.
We can first look at the same
series with absolute values,
and try to find out whether
this one IS convergent.
If the series with absolute values
is convergent,
then so is the original series.
Problem solved!
However, if it isn't,
then we cannot draw
any conclusions
about the original series yet.
We have to try something else.
Let's illustrate this
with some examples.
As example #1, let's go back
to the motivating question.
Is the sum of sin(n)/n^2
a convergent series?
First, I will look at the same
series with absolute values.
It is a positive series, so
I can use comparison tests.
|sin(n)|/n^2 is bounded above
by 1/n^2.
And we know that the sum
of 1/n^2 is convergent.
By the basic comparison test,
so is |sin(n)|/n^2.
Great! This is the good case.
The Absolute Convergence Test
tells us the original series
is also convergent.
Done.
Notice that I would not have 
been able
to use comparison tests directly
on the original series.
The comparison tests can only
be used on positive series.
For a second example, let's look

For each positive integer n,

1/n, if n is prime,

I want to know:

Think about it for a moment.
This is similar to the
harmonic series,

The patterns of which terms

To attack this problem, I do not

In this case, that's easy.
It is exactly the sum of 1/n.

I do not know YET whether

It is definitely one or the other,

There are two different cells

These two.
Let's give them names.
This is just a definition.
A convergent series is called
"absolutely convergent"

with absolute vales is also convergent,
and is called
"conditionally convergent"

These are just names for

Here are the names

These is one fourth case, but

Let's illustrate these
two concepts

First, the alternating
harmonic series:
the sum of (-1)^{n+1}/n.
This series is
conditionally convergent.
Why? The series itself
is convergent,
thanks to the Alternating
Series Test,

all terms positive,

series, which I know to be divergent.
It is a p-series with p = 1.
By contrast, if I look at
the same series
with denominator n^2

then this new series is
ABSOLUTELY convergent.
Why?
The series itself is
still convergent,
thanks to the Alternating
Series Test,
and the series without signs
is also convergent.
In this case it is a p-series
with p = 2.
Okay. All I have done so far is
give a name to these two types

Absolutely convergent,
and conditionally convergent.
That's all.
I have not stated any
new results.
You may wonder:
why do we bother
distinguishing them?
Aren't both types just
"convergent?"
Aren't they both equally good?
After all, every convergent

a number, whether it is absolutely

Well...not quite.
It turns out that absolutely
convergent series are nice.
In many ways, they behave
just like finite sums.
You can only do to them things that
you'd like to do to series.
There are some theorems

to absolutely convergent series.
By contrast, conditionally
convergent series are a TRAP.
They look nice,
but they are nasty.
They have some pathological
properties. For example...
Conditionally convergent series
are NOT commutative.
This may come as a surprise.
It goes against everything we
know about finite sums.
