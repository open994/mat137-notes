In this video, I will introduce
indeterminate forms for limits.
I want to explain what it means, and
what it DOESN'T mean,
to have an indeterminate form.
In the later videos,
linked in the description,
I will explain how to resolve them;
how to actually compute these limits.
For now, I only want to explain
what an indeterminate form is.
As motivation, 
consider this limit.
The function is a quotient.
The numerator and denominator are
both nice, continuous functions.
So, I may be tempted to evaluate it.
But if I simply evaluate, I get...
zero divided by zero?!
That is not the answer!
That is not a number!
That makes no sense.
Instead, we have an
indeterminate form.
This indeterminate form appears
when I try to compute
certain types of quotients.
Let's say I want to compute
the limit of f divided by g,
and I know something about f and g.
IF I know the limit of f is two,
and the limit of g is three,
What can I conclude about
the limit of the quotient?
That is easy. The limit of the 
quotient is 2/3.
That is a simple use
of limit laws.
Another example.
IF I know the limit of f is 1,
and the limit of g is infinity,
what can I conclude about the
limit of the quotient?
In this case, the limit of
the quotient is zero.
We can prove this from the
definition of the limit, if necessary.
However...
IF I know the limit of f is zero,
and the limit of g is also zero,
what can I conclude about the
limit of the quotient?
In this case, I cannot
draw any conclusions
about the limit of the quotient.
Just based on the information I have,
I do not know what the limit will be.
The way to prove this
is with examples.
I can construct various examples
that are all of this type,
zero divided by zero,
but where the final answer,
the limit of the quotient,
is different in each case.
Let's do that.
All of these limits will be taken
as x approaches zero.
The limit of 2x/x...is 2.
Just simplify the function
before computing the limit.
The limit of x^2 / x...
is 0.
Same argument here – simplify the
function before computing the limit.
And the limit of x / x^3...
is infinity.
That's it. That proves my claim.
In these three examples, the limit of
the numerator is always zero,
and the limit of the denominator
is always zero,
but the limit of the quotient
is different in each case.
We say that 0/0 is a limit
indeterminate form.
This means what I have at the top.
Nothing more, nothing less.
Indeterminate form means that
whenever we encounter this
type of limit – 0/0 – 
just from knowing that it is 0/0,
I cannot draw any conclusions.
I have some important
remarks to make.
First, we are talking about limits.
We are never ACTUALLY
dividing zero by zero.
For any value of x, I will have
something CLOSE to zero,
divided by something
CLOSE to zero.
Second, indeterminate form does
not mean that the limit of the quotient
is undefined or that it does not exist.
Each particular case may be different.
Some may exist and some may not.
It only means that we do not know
what the answer is yet,
and we have to do more work.
And third, indeterminate
forms normally arise
because there is a tension between
two pieces of the function.
In this case, the numerator thinks:
"zero divided by anything is zero!"
So the numerator wants to make
the quotient have limit zero.
The denominator thinks:
"anything divided by zero is ±infinity!"
So, the denominator wants to
make the quotient
have a limit of ±infinity.
This becomes a competition
between f and g,
to see which one approaches zero
faster...so, which one "pulls" faster.
All other indeterminate forms
can also be understood
as this kind of tension between
two pieces of the function.
The most common
indeterminate forms are:
zero over zero,
plus or minus infinity
over plus or minus infinity,
zero times infinity,
infinity minus infinity,
and the exponential types:
zero to the power of zero,
infinity to the zero,
and one to the power of ±infinity.
A very useful tool to solve some
indeterminate forms is L'Hôpital's Rule.
In the next sequence of videos,
I will explain what L'Hôpital's Rule says,
present examples of how to use it,
explain how L'Hôpital's Rule
may fail if misused,
and go over the different types of
indeterminate forms
to learn tricks to handle each one.
Before I do that, I want to comment
on one more type of limit:
one divided by zero.
Why didn't I include it in the list?
If the limit of the numerator is one,
and the limit of the denominator is zero,
what can we conclude about
the limit of the quotient?
We do not normally consider this
an indeterminate form,
because we CAN conclude something.
But, we have to be careful.
In this situation, I may be tempted to
say that the limit of the quotient
is going to be infinity.
But I can ONLY conclude that the limit
of the absolute value of the quotient
is going to be infinity,
because the denominator may be
positive or negative!
If I know that, in addition, near the point
the denominator stays positive,
then I conclude the limit
of the quotient is infinity.
But if I know that, in addition, near the
point, the denominator stays negative,
then I conclude the limit
of the quotient is negative infinity.
For example, let's say I want to compute
the limit as x approach 3 on the right
of 1/(x – 3)(2 – x).
This is one divided by zero.
Let's tally the sign of the denominator.
As x approaches 3 from the right
(x is greater than 3),
x – 3 is...positive.
And 2 – x is negative.
So the full denominator is negative.
So, the limit is minus infinity.
So we have to be careful,
but we can say something,
and that is why I did not include 1/0
on the list of indeterminate forms.
