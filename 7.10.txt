Riemann sums are
an alternative way
to calculate the
integral of a function.
They are not very convenient
to prove theorems,
but sometimes they are
useful for computations.

For example,
If the function is

As you know,

Then, I can estimate the area
of each slice with a rectangle.
I can under-estimate with

If I want to use this
in the calculation,

The height of each rectangle

of the function
on that sub-interval.
Unless the function
is monotonic,
that will be a pain
to calculate.
So, what if, instead,
I choose a random point
on the graph of f
over every sub-interval,
and I use the value of the
function at that point
as the height of
the rectangle?
That gives me a new set
of rectangles
to estimate the area.
Notice that these rectangles
are always between the
lower rectangles
and the upper rectangles,
so they are just an estimate.
We do not know if they are
an under- or over-estimate.
These rectangles are
going to depend
on the points I choose
over each sub-interval.
Let's give a name to the
x-coordinates of those points.
If the points in the partition

x_1*, x_2*, and so on.
I'm not a big fan of this notation,
but it is very standard,
so I will use it.

With this notation, what is the
area of these new rectangles,
pictured in black?
The width of each rectangle
is the same as before,
and the height of each rectangle

at the point x_i*.
So the area is
the sum from i = 1 to n
of f(x_i*) time ∆x_i.
Notice that I had many options
for the points x_i*, and
each one of them
would give me

I can make things simpler
by always picking
the left endpoint of
each sub-interval,
or the right-endpoint,
or perhaps the middle point,

Whatever choice I make,
I will call this sum

Here's a more formal definition.
Let f be a bounded function
on the interval [a, b],
and let P be a partition of [a, b],
consisting of the point
x_0, x_1, x_2 and so on,
until x_n.
For each i, I define ∆x_i
to be the length
of the i-th sub-interval,
and I choose a number
x_i* on the i-th sub-interval.
This means that x_i* is
between x_i and x_{i-1}.
Then, the sum I wrote on
the previous picture

"Riemann-sum for f and P."
Notice that I said
A Riemann sum,
not THE Riemann sum.
This Riemann sum depends
not only on the function,
the interval and the partition,
it also depends on
the number x_i*
we choose on each sub-interval.
So, even after fixing the function
and the partition,
there are lots of other
Riemann sums.
I am going to denote this
Riemann sum as S*_p(f).
The star in the name indicates
that the Riemann sum
depends on the choice of

How is this helpful???
Here is the theorem that justifies
caring about Riemann sums.
Let's fix a bounded function f
on the interval [a, b],
and assume f is integrable
on [a, b].
As I said before, for example,
if f is continuous on [a, b],
then f is integrable on [a, b].
Let's pick a sequence
of partitions
P_1, P_2, P_3 and so on,
whose norms approach 0
as n approaches infinity.
For example, the simplest choice
is to take P_n to be the
partition that breaks [a, b]
into n sub-intervals
of equal length.
Then, on each sub-interval
of each partition,
we pick a point x_i*.
For example, the simplest choice
is to just take the right
endpoint of each sub-interval.
Then, the integral
from a to b of f
equals the limit of
these Riemann sums.
I want to emphasize that
this is simpler than
computing the integral
directly from the definition.
For, I do not need to worry
about ALL partitions.
I can use just one
sequence of partitions,
and I will pick
simple-enough partitions
to simplify the calculations.
Second, I do not need to
calculate suprema
and infima on each sub-interval.
I just have to pick a point
on each sub-interval,
and I will make a choice that
makes the calculation simpler.
I will explain this with an example
at the end of the video.
Here's another way to write
the same formula,
that is quite suggestive.
It suggests that in the limit,
the sum turns into an integral,
and the width of each
sub-interval, ∆x,
turns into dx.
That's why some people
like to say that dx
represents an infinitesimally
small width.
There is some value
to that interpretation,
but I won't go into it just yet.
I also want to mention that
this notation is not perfect.
Strictly speaking, 
the numbers x_i and x_i*
should have two indices:
i and n,
because they depend on
the partition P_n.
However, this notation
is quite standard,
so I will continue using it.
The proof of this theorem
turns out to be quite simple,
if we use everything
we know so far.
We know what happens
when we take limits
of lower or upper sums.
The limit as n approaches infinity
of the P_n-lower sum of f
is the lower integral of f.
And the limit of the P_n-upper sum
is the upper integral.
That is always true for
any bounded function,
given the choice of
partitions we used.
But since the function
is also integrable,
we know the lower
and upper integrals
are both THE integral.
In addition, we know that
any Riemann sum is sandwiched
between the corresponding
lower and upper sums.
Therefore, using
the Squeeze Theorem,
the limit of the Riemann sums
is also the integral.
That's it.
That is why we needed the
function f to be integrable
to begin with.
On the positive side, this
calculation is easier.
On the negative side,
I needed to know the function f
was integrable before
I even started.
If I try to use Riemann sums
to compute the integral
of an arbitrary bounded function,
without knowing whether
it is integrable or not,
this method won't work.
The limit may or may not exist,
and it may depend on
the choices we made.
Even if the limit exists,
it does not tell me whether
the function is integrable.
That is why in practice,
we tend to use Riemann sums
only for continuous functions,
because those are guaranteed
to be integrable.

To summarize, Riemann sums
may look like a simpler way
to compute some integrals
than the original definition.
But they only work if we know
in advance that the function is integrable.
For example, if it is continuous.
To develop the theory
of integration
and to prove theorems
and properties,
the original definition
is still more useful.
