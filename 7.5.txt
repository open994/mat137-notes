STEP 3:
This is the hard one.
How do I define integral
out of all of this?
What I know so far is that
the integral must be a number,
and it must be between
all the lower sums
and all the upper sums.
Let's draw a picture.
This is the real line.
I choose one partition and
compute its lower sum;
it is a number.
Then I choose another partition
and I get another lower sum.
And another, and another,
and so on, until I get
all possible lower sums.
Similarly, I could compute
all possible upper sums.
The way I drew this picture, I am
making an implicit assumption:
that EVERY lower lower sum
is smaller than or equal to
EVERY upper sum.
This makes sense geometrically,
and it turns out to be true.
But it is a bit subtle,
and it needs to be proven.
I am calling it a lemma.
I will postpone the proof

For now, let's see what I can
do with this assumption.
Let's focus on the lower sums.
Out of all of them, how do
I define the integral?
Notice that every lower sum

But, if I take better partitions,

Similarly, better partitions will
give me smaller upper sums.
...then what?
Better partitions give me
larger lower sums,

Or, so it seems

This suggests I should

But what limit exactly?
The limit as the partitions

Partitions are not numbers,

There IS a way to

But there is also a
simpler, more elegant solution.
I am going to define

One from the lower sums,

I define the lower integral

Each lower sum

All the lower sums together

It may be tempting to

Therefore, it must

Alternatively, I can use

I define the upper integral

Now I have two
definitions of integral.
Which one do I choose?
Since every lower sum is
less than or equal to
every upper sum,
I can conclude that the
lower integral is
less than or equal to
the upper integral.
And now, I have
two cases.
The good case is when
they are equal.
In that case, I say that
f is "integrable" on [a, b],
and I define the integral
to be the common value.
The bad case is when
they are different.
Then, I say that f is
"non-integrable" on [a, b],
and the integral
is undefined.
You may wonder how

Every continuous function
