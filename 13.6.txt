Series (or infinite sums) are linear.
This refers to these two properties.
If I have a series whose general term
is the sum of two separate terms,
then I can split it as
the sum of two series,
and if I have a series where the general
term is a constant times something else,
then I can take the constant out.
Looking at these two properties,
perhaps your reaction is:
"Well that's obviously true!
Do we have to waste any time on this?
Do we have to bother proving it?"
Yes. To justify why these
properties are not obvious,
and why we cannot take them for granted,
I want to first look
at a different property
that surprisingly
happens to be false.
I'm going to do that with an example.
Let's look at this infinite sum:
it's 1 - 1/2 + 1/3 - 1/4, and so on,
so it's the sum of the reciprocal
of all the positive integers,
but the odd ones I add,
and the even ones I subtract.
This infinite sum happens to be
convergent, and the value is ln(2).
This is not obvious at all, and
I'm going to prove it now.
For now, just trust me on it.
Now I'm going to look at a second sum,
which is very similar.
I'm going to take the very same terms
and I'm going to reorder them.
So now I add two positive ones,
then one negative,
then two positive,
then one negative, and so on,
but ultimately in the infinite sum,
it's the very same terms, reordered.
We would probably expect that if the
first sum is converging,
so is the second,
and it has the same value;
and indeed the second sum is convergent,
but the value of the sum is different.
This should be surprising.
This would make you perhaps a
bit uncomfortable,
and in fact, perhaps
you do not believe me.
While I'm not going
to prove it yet,
later in this series of
videos (no pun intended),
I will justify why this is true.
I will explain it.
But the reason I'm bringing it up now
is to explain that just because a certain
property of finite sums
seems intuitive and natural, and we
believe it's going to be true,
doesn't mean it will necessarily carry
forward for infinite sums...
...and still be true.
Finite sums can be reordered at will,
but infinite sums can not be reordered
at will, even when they are convergent.
It may change the outcome.
By contrast, linearity refers
to two important properties
that are true for finite sums,
and are still true for infinite sums.
Let's have a look at them.
Linearity of series is
summarized by two theorems:
what happens when I add two series, and
when I multiply a series by a constant.
The first theorem says:
IF I begin with two series,
(the sum of aₙ and the sum of bₙ),
and I assume they're both convergent,
THEN the combined series
(the sum of aₙ + bₙ),
must also be convergent, and the value of 
the sum is exactly what you would expect.
(It's the sum of the other two series.)
So for this specific rearrangement,
it works as we expected.
I need to assume the two initial series
are convergent, otherwise it's not true.
But if they're convergent,
then the rearrangement works.
The second theorem is about
multiplying a series by a constant.
IF c is a real number, and I begin with a 
series (sum of aₙ), which is convergent,
THEN the sum of c⋅aₙ is also convergent,
and it's value is again, exactly what
you would expect it to be.
So this kind of property of finite sums
also extends to infinite sums.
But again, I need to assume that
this series I begin with is convergent.
As an example, I'm just going
to prove the first theorem.
The proof of the second theorem
works very similarly.
The proof is going
to be quite straightforward.
For these properties (when they're true),
the proof tends to be very short.
We need to use the definition
of infinite sum.
An infinite sum is defined as a
limit of finite sums, so let's do that.
I say that the infinite sum of all
the aₙs is the limit of partial sums,
and I'm going to call
the partial sum Sₖ,
where Sₖ is the finite sum of
the aₙs only up to the term k.
Similarly, the other two series can also
be written as limits of partial sums,
but I'm going to give different
names to those partial sums.
So, I'm going to say the sum of all the
bₙs, is the limit of the partial sum Tₖ,
where Tₖ is a finite sum of bₙs.
And the sum of aₙ + bₙ is
the limit of the partial sum Rₖ,
where Rₖ is a finite sum of aₙ + bₙ.
And once I have written the
infinite sum as a limit of finite sums,
I can now manipulate the finite sums.
And for finite sums I
don't have to be careful!
I know that for finite sums, any of
the usual manipulations are true,
so I can reorder the terms.
So it is true that Rₖ = Sₖ + Tₖ.
Now, the hypothesis of the theorem is
that the first two series are convergent.
What that means is that the first
two limits exist. That's my hypothesis.
So, if Rₖ = Sₖ + Tₖ, and I am assuming the
first two limits exist,
then simply by the limit laws,
the third limit also exists,
and is the sum of the other two,
and that's it!
That completes the proof.
This is kind of a template of
all the common properties of series,
and this is how they are all proven.
First, write the infinite sum as a
limit of partial sums (finite sums).
Second, use the property you
know is true for finite sums.
And then third, pass to the limit.
(Assuming we are allowed
to pass to the limit.)
