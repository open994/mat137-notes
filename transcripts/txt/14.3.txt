In this video,
I will define Taylor polynomials.
If you look at different books,
you will find many equivalent ways
to introduce Taylor polynomials.
The definition I've chosen
illustrates very well where
they come from,
and why we care about them,
although it is not the most
helpful for computations.
In later videos,
I will transform this definition
into other equivalent versions
that are better suited
for computations,
including an explicit formula.
My goal is to approximate functions 
with polynomials.
Why? Because polynomials
are simple,
and they make everything easy!
Let's say I have a function f,
and a is a number in its domain.
I want to find a polynomial P
such that P(x) is close to f(x)
when x is near a.
In other words, the polynomial
is a good approximation
for the function near a.
We already know an
example of this:
the tangent line!
For example, here is the
graph of a function,
and its tangent line at a.
We know that the graph
of the function
and the graph of the tangent line
are close to each other
as long as I stay near a.
Of all the lines that intersect
the graph of the function at x = a,
the tangent line is the best
approximation near a.
But, what does that mean?
I would like to know what
being a good approximation
near a actually means
so that I can construct
other polynomials
that are even better approximations
than the tangent line.
For this purpose, I am going to
define the "remainder", or "error",
as the difference between
the function and the polynomial.
This difference is literally
the error I make
when I use the polynomial
instead of the function.
I want this error to be small...
but I will need something
more precise
than merely saying "small"!
Let's look at the tangent line again.
Here is a value of x, and
here is the remainder at x.
It is this difference.
Let's explore.
What's special about the
tangent line?
I said I wanted an approximation
near a,
so perhaps the condition is
that the remainder at a is 0?
No, that's not enough.
There are many other lines
that also give error 0 at a,
and some of them are
very bad approximations
so that's not it.
I need something else.
Well, I want the approximation
to be good when x is NEAR a,
not only AT a.
Perhaps what I need is for
the limit as x approaches a
of the remainder at x to be zero.
No, that's not enough either.
All these other lines also
satisfy that the limit
of the remainder is zero.
Then, what's special about
the tangent line?
What I need is for the remainder
to approach zero...
...FAST.
All of these lines satisfy that
as x approaches a,
the remainder approaches 0,
but the remainder for
the tangent line
approaches 0 faster than
for any other line.
That's the key.
But of course, this immediately
leads to another question:
what does it mean to
approach a limit "fast"?
How do I measure
how fast a function
is approaching its limit?
I need a scale.
Fortunately, I have one.
For simplicity, first let's
focus on the case a = 0.
This is the graph of y = x.
As x approaches 0,
y approaches 0.
Now, compare it with y = x^2,
and with y = x^6.
They all approach 0
as x approaches 0,
but some do it faster
than others.
Look how flat the graph
of x^6 is!
As x approaches 0,
every positive power of x
approaches 0,
but the larger the exponent,
the faster it approaches 0.
Excellent!
I am going to use this idea.
I want the remainder
to approach 0 "fast".
To determine how fast,
I am going to compare it
with powers of x.
This leads to the
following definition.
Let f and g be two
continuous functions at 0
and let n be a natural number.
We say that g is an
approximation for f
near 0 of order n when...
Well, that's a lot of variables.
Let's unpack them first.
f is my original function,
g is the function I am using as
an approximation for f,
0 is the point near which
I want an approximation,
and n measures how good
the approximation is.
Okay. As I was saying,
g is an approximation for f
near 0 of order n when
the limit as x approaches 0
of the difference of f and g
over x^n is 0.
What does this mean?
If I write the function f
as g plus a remainder,
this means that
as x approaches 0,
the remainder approaches 0
faster than x^n approaches 0.
Why? Look at the limit
here in the box.
In general, I have an
indeterminate form
of type 0/0.
The numerator is the remainder,
which has limit 0,
and the denominator is x^n,
which has limit 0.
For this indeterminate form
to resolve into being 0,
the numerator must win
over the denominator.
The numerator must approach 0
faster than the denominator.
That is why this is
a sensible definition.
Great!
This works when a = 0,
but what if I am looking for
approximations
near a different value of a,
not just 0?
All I need to do is SHIFT the scale.
Here is the graph of (x – a),
which approaches 0
as x approaches a,
and here is (x – a)^2,
and (x – a)^6.
They look the same as before.
To study approximations near a,
I will compare the remainder
with powers of (x – a)
instead of with powers of x.
That is all.
Other than that, the definition
works the same way.
This time, I begin with
functions f and g,
which are continuous at a,
and I say that g is an
approximation for f
near a of order n
when the limit
as x approaches a
of the difference
over (x – a)^n is 0.
That is all.
The interpretation is
the same as before.
This means I can write
f as g plus a remainder,
and as x approaches a,
the remainder approaches 0
faster than (x – a)^n.
Excellent.
We are finally ready
for the first definition
of Taylor polynomials.
Let a be a real number.
Let f be a continuous function
defined at and near a.
Let n be a natural number.
The n-th Taylor polynomial
for f at a
is a polynomial,
which I will call P_n,
that is an approximation
for f near a of order n.
...And now we know what that means.
It means that as x approaches a,
the function minus the polynomial
approaches 0
faster than (x – a)^n.
I am also going to request
that the polynomial
has degree at most n.
This requires some explanation.
At the moment, the degree
seems unimportant.
In later videos I will prove that
as long as f has enough derivatives,
we can always construct
this polynomial,
and we can do so while keeping
the degree at most n.
We can also get other polynomials
with the same property,
and with higher degrees.
But smaller degree is simpler,
so we prefer it.
That's the reason for
this requirement.
But again, right now this is
the least important part
of the definition.
