of how to prove

In the previous video, in Example 1,
I chose a function that was simple

the logic and structure of the proof.
In Example 2, the function

It is important that you have watched

Here is the problem:
I want to prove
that lim_(x → 4) (x^2 + 1) = 17,
and I want to do it directly
from the definition of limit.
So, let's recall the definition of limit.
What I need to show
is that for every ε positive,
there exists a δ positive
such that IF the distance between x and 4
is between 0 and δ,
THEN the distance between x^2 + 1 and 17
is smaller than ε.
The statement that I want to prove
is very similar
to the same statement in Example 1,
so the structure of the proof
will ALSO be the SAME.
Let's quickly recall the structure.
In my proof, I'm going to need
to first FIX an arbitrary value of ε.
After I do that,
I will say what I take as δ,
and δ may be a function of ε.
And after that, I will assume
the hypothesis in the conditional;
I fix an arbitrary real number,
and I assume the distance between x and 4
is between 0 and δ.
And after doing some math,
hopefully I can conclude
that the distance between x^2 + 1 and 17
is smaller than ε.
Like before, the most difficult step
is to decide WHAT I take as δ.
So before I start writing the proof
-- I can't quite do that yet, --
let's do some rough work to see
if we can come up
with a good expression for δ.
I'll focus for now on the implication.
I want to end up CONCLUDING
that the distance between x^2 + 1 and 17
is smaller than ε.
Being with some algebra,
I can rewrite this distance
as |x^2 - 16|.
This factors as |x + 4| ⋅ |x - 4|.
And in the middle of the proof,
I will be assuming
that |x - 4| is less than δ,
so the product is less than δ ⋅ |x + 4|.
Now, I want to conclude
that the left-hand side
is smaller than ε,
and I know it's smaller than δ ⋅ |x + 4|.
So perhaps I may be tempted
to take δ as ε ÷ |x + 4|.
This is a bad idea;
this doesn't work.
This is very wrong.
But I am mentioning it
because this is a common error
that I have seen often
in students papers,
so I want to explain why it's a bad idea.
Let's take a step back;
let's look back
at the structure of the proof.
We were supposed to FIRST FIX ε,
THEN SAY what we take as δ
as a function of ε,
THEN FIX a generic x,
ASSUME the hypothesis, etc.
Notice that δ
is allowed to depend on ε,
but NOT on x.
In fact, it makes no sense
to allow δ to depend on x.
If you think about it a bit further,
you will notice
that if we allow δ to depend on x,
we change the meaning entirely;
this doesn't mean limit.
So δ can ONLY depend on ε.
So, that idea didn't work
and I seem to be a bit stuck.
There are two tricks
that can help me at the moment.
First: since the part that bothers me
is |x + 4|:
if I can somehow manage to prove
that |x + 4| is less
than some constant C,
for example if I can prove
|x + 4| is less than 100 by any means,
then by taking δ as ε / 100,
I think I'll be able to finish the proof.
So I don't know how I'm gonna do this,
I don't know yet if I can do this,
but if I can prove
that |x + 4| is bounded by a constant,
then I have a way to finish the proof.
Is this reasonable?
Yes it is.
Since δ is small,
x is going to be close to 4.
And if x is close to 4,
|x + 4| cannot be arbitrarily large.
So yeah, I'm pretty sure
it's gonna be smaller than 100.
But I have to prove it.
So here comes the second trick.
The second trick is that I am going
to choose δ at most 1.
Because that will guarantee
that x and 4 are "close."
There are two important remarks
to make here.
First, I AM ALLOWED to do this.
Remember that in the proof,
I get to choose δ;
I have to say what I am taking as δ,
so I CAN PUT constraints on δ.
I'm just going to keep track
of all the conditions
that I would need δ to satisfy,
and at the end, if I can find one δ
with all those conditions,
then that's the one I will take
and the proof will work.
So it is okay for me
to put extra constraints on δ
as long as I check at the end
that there is such a δ.
Second, I've put here 1 for simplicity,
but it's an arbitrary choice;
I could have chosen any other number,
and this could link to another proof
that ALSO works;
it looks different
but also works.
I just kept it as 1 for simplicity.
So with those two remarks,
how does it help me
to choose δ at most 1?
If I do that,
|x - 4|, which is less than δ,
will ALSO be less than 1.
And therefore, x will be between 3 and 5,
x + 4 will be between 7 and 9,
and therefore,
|x + 4| will be less than 9.
Hooray! That's what I needed.
Choosing δ at most 1
guarantees that I can bound |x + 4| by 9,
and therefore I only need
to take δ at most ε/9,
and that will make the proof work!
So, I need two conditions:
I need a δ that is at most 1,
and that is at most ε/9.
The easiest way to accomplish that
is by choosing δ
to be the smallest of those two numbers;
whichever one is smallest.
That will be a positive number
that satisfies both inequalities.
So I've done it;
I have found a value of δ that works.
That means I am ready to write the proof.
Remember that what I've done so far
does NOT count as a proof;
I haven't even BEGUN the proof.
I now need to write the proof properly
in the logical order,
making sure that every step
does follow from the previous ones,
and that I haven't confused myself
while doing the rough work.
So let's do that.
To prove this claim,
first I fix an arbitrary positive ε,
then I take δ
to be the minimum of 1 and ε/9.
How do I know this?
Because of ALL the rough work
I did in the previous slide;
otherwise, I wouldn't know it.
Choosing this δ guarantees that δ ≤ 1,
and is also less than or equal to ε/9.
Next, I fix an arbitrary real number x,
and I assume the distance between x and 4
is between 0 and δ.
This implies that this distance
is less than ε/9, and also less than 1.
Since it is less than 1,
x must be between 3 and 5
and therefore x + 4
must be between 7 and 9.
And now, let's take
the expression I care about.
|(x^2 + 1) - 17| = |x^2 - 16|.
It factors as |x - 4| ⋅ |x + 4|,
the first term is less than ε/9,
and the second term is less than 9.
So the product is at most ε.
And that's it!
I have proven
that the distance between x^2 + 1 and 17
is smaller than ε, as needed.
So this completes the proof.
