I want to explain what the theorem says,
and when it does and doesn't apply.
Then, I will work out some examples

L'Hôpital's Rule is a tool that
helps us compute limits.
Specifically, let's look at these four
examples on the screen.
They are all limits of quotients,
and they are all indeterminate forms,
of types zero/zero
or infinity/infinity.
They are all examples where, to compute
the limit, you cannot just look
at the numerator and
denominator separately,
because we always get
an indeterminate form.
L'Hôpital's Rule provides use with
something we can do to attack all these,
so that we are not stuck.
So, what does this theorem say?
I'm going to begin with two functions,
f and g, and a real number a.
I want to compute that the limit
as x approaches a
of f(x) divided by g(x).
Well, the theorem actually is more
general than that.
The theorem also works for limits
at infinity, and limits at minus infinity,
and also for side limits.
All those types.
For simplicity, I'm going to write
the statement only for limits
as x approaches a number a.
But, the theorem is still true
for all those other types of limits.
This theorem says that under certain
conditions, which I will go into in a moment,
the limit of f divided by g
is the same as the limit of f' divided by g'.
How would this be helpful?
Normally, the situation is that I want to
compute the first limit,
and that's a difficult limit
which we don't know how to compute.
So, instead we compute the second limit,
which hopefully is easier.
And that happens often, because when
we take derivatives of functions,
they simplify a little bit.
Now, what are the conditions for
this theorem to work?
The most important one,
(I wouldn't put it first, but it's
the most important one)
is that this only works when the limit
I am trying to compute,
the limit of of f/g,
is an indeterminate form of type
zero/zero or ±infinity/±infinity.
What that means is that
for this to work,
either the limit of the numerator and
the limit of the denominator
are both zero, separately,
OR each one of them in separately
either plus or minus infinity.
Notice that those are the only
indeterminate forms of quotients.
So, if I am in any other situation, then
I am not allowed to use L'Hôpital's Rule.
But luckily, if I am in any other situation,
I don't need L'Hôpital's Rule,
because I wouldn't have an indeterminate
form, and then I would be done.
I would have no doubt about
what the limit was.
So, most importantly, this rule only applies
when I get an indeterminate form:
zero/zero or ±infinity/±infinity.
Apart from this, there are two
technical conditions
for the rule to even make sense.
First, I need to require f and g to be
differentiable as x approaches a.
Otherwise, it makes no sense
to take their derivatives.
When I say "as x approaches a,"
I mean when x is close to a, 
but not necessarily at a.
It doesn't matter if they
are not differentiable at a.
I am also going to require that g and g'
are never zero as x approaches a.
So again, when x is close to a,
but not necessarily a.
Otherwise, I cannot even write
the function f/g, or f'/g',
when x is close to a, because
it would be dividing by zero.
If I cannot write those functions, then
I cannot make sense of the limits.
So those are technical conditions,
without which the question is not
even worth positing.
But, there is a fourth condition,
which we often forget
because it doesn't come into play
often, but which is very important.
For this theorem to be true,
I also need to request
that the second limit,
the limit of f'/g'
either exists, is infinity,
or is minus infinity.
Otherwise, the theorem
does not apply.
Let me elaborate on this. As I said,
this is something we often forget.
This means that if the second limit,
the limit of f'/g', exists,
then I can conclude that the limit of
f/g also exists, and is equal.
If the limit of f'/g' is infinity,
then I can conclude that the
limit of f/g is infinity as well.
If the limit of f'/g' is minus infinity,
then I can conclude that the
limit of f/g is minus infinity.
But!
If the limit of f'/g' does not exist
for a different reason,
say, it is some function that
oscillates in a weird way,
and that's why the limit doesn't exist,
then I can draw no conclusion
about the original limit!
The original limit may well exist, and
be anything...or perhaps it doesn't exist.
So, for the theorem to apply, I need
the second limit to either exist,
or be plus or minus infinity.
That's it! That's the theorem.
What about the proof?
Well, the proof is rather long.
This is not just one theorem...
it is a lot of theorems.
It's a separate theorem for 0/0
and for infinity/infinity,
and there is also a separate theorem
for limits at a point a (a real number),
and at infinity, or minus infinity,
or side limits...there are a lot of them.
We normally prove
one of them from scratch
and then reduce the rest
of the versions to it.
The proof of this theorem relies
on the Mean Value Theorem,
or rather, on a variant of the
Mean Value Theorem.
