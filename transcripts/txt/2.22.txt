Specifically, the theorem says this:
I'm looking at a function
defined on an interval,
and I want to know:
when can I conclude for sure

will take the value of 0?
And what we saw is

is negative on one endpoint,
positive on the other,
AND continuous.
That guarantees it.
That's all!
This is what
the Intermediate Value Theorem says.
Perhap, instead of looking

I may be looking
at when it takes another value.
So if I look at the real number M

that f takes value M,
I have to request
that it's continuous,
and that takes a value less than M

Or in other words,
we can always conclude
that the function f
will take all the values
between f(a) and f(b).
And that's ANOTHER equivalent way
to state this:
IF the function is continuous on [a, b],
THEN it takes EVERY value
between f(a) and f(b).
