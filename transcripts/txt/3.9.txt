In this video, I will present some
examples of functions that are continuous,
but not differentiable.
How may a function fail
to be differentiable?
We know that if a function is
differentiable,
then it must be continuous.
I proved this in an earlier video which
I will link in the description.
So any non-continuous function will also
be non-differentiable.
However, it is more interesting to look
for examples of functions that
ARE continuous,
but are NOT differentiable.
As a reminder, saying that a function f
is continuous at a number a means that
lim_(x → a) f(x) = f(a).
Saying that f is differentiable at a
means that a different limit exists:
lim_(x → a) [f(x) - f(a)] ÷ (x - a).
This is the limit we use to define the
derivative f'(a).
So, saying that f is NOT
differentiable at a
means that the second limit
does not exist.
This second limit, like any other limit,
may not exist for a variety of reasons.
I am going to focus on two specific ways
in which this limit may not exist.
Because the two side limits are
different (we will call this a corner),
or because the limit is +/- infinity (we
will call this a vertical tangent line).
But of course, there could be other
reasons why this limit does not exist,
and any way in which this limit
fails to exist
still gives us a non-differentiable
function.
To understand these two special cases,
I will look at one example of each.
For my first example, consider the
function f defined by f(x) = |x|.
I want to know whether f is
differentiable at 0.
Let's try to compute the derivative at 0,
as lim_(x → 0) [f(x) - f(0)] ÷ (x - 0).
Using the definition of f, 
this is the limit of |x| over x.
Since we have |x|,
it is easiest to break it into two cases.
When x is positive,
as x approaches 0 on the right,
|x| is simply x, and the limit is 1.
On the other hand, when x is negative, as
x is approaching 0 on the left,
|x| is -x, and the limit is -1.
In conclusion, the derivative at 0 does
not exist,
because the two side limits in the limit
definition of derivative are different.
Remember that derivative represents slope.
I can interpret this by saying that at 0,
there is a slope on right and a different
slope on the left, so to speak.
And yes, that is what
the graph looks like.
The slope at 0 seems to be 1 if I look at
0 from the right,
and -1 if I look at 0 from the left.
The graph has a sharp edge: a corner.
And in general, a function f will have a
corner at x = a when f is continuous at a,
but the limit in the definition of
derivative f'(a) does not exist
because the side limits are different.
For the second example, I will look at
the function g, defined as g(x) = x¹ᐟ³.
I want to know whether g is
differentiable at 0.
Again, I try to compute the derivative
g'(0) from its definition as a limit.
Using the expression I have for g, this
is lim_(x → 0) x¹ᐟ³ ÷ x,
which is the limit of 1 ÷ x²ᐟ³.
This limit is infinity,
because the denominator has limit 0
and is always positive.
Now, remember that derivative means slope.
What does infinite slope mean?
A line with infinite slope is a vertical
line,
so this suggests the graph has a
vertical tangent line.
And indeed, this is what
the graph looks like.
More generally, I will say that a function
has a vertical tangent line at a point a
when the function is continuous at a,
and the derivative at a,
computed from its definition as a limit,
is plus or minus infinity.
In the example it was plus infinity,
but minus infinity would also produce a
vertical tangent line.
We are not done yet.
There is a little bit more to add to the
concept of vertical tangent line.
Let's go back to the example.
I can look at it in a different way.
Whenever x is not 0,
I have a formula for the derivative g'(x).
Using the power rule, it is 1 ÷ 3x²ᐟ³.
And then I can calculate
lim_(x → 0) g'(x),
and this limit is also infinity.
Perhaps you find that this second
calculation
is easier than the earlier one.
However, a priori, these two quantities -
the derivative at 0 and the limit as x
approaches 0 of the derivative at x -
could have been different.
They turn out to give the same result for
this example.
What's the relation between these two
conditions in general?
It is possible to prove that the second
condition
always implies the first condition.
I won't write a proof right now; it is
an application of L'Hôpital's rule.
To be fair, in practice, for almost all
the functions we commonly work with,
these two conditions are the same.
But if we really insist,
we can construct some unusual functions
that satisfy one but not the other.
So what do we do with the situation?
The correct definition of vertical
tangent line
requires the DERIVATIVE to be plus or
minus infinity.
Requiring instead the LIMIT of the
derivative to be plus or minus infinity
is slightly stronger.
But that is helpful -
if you're working with an example and you
prove it satisfies the second condition,
then you have also proven it satisfies
the first condition,
so you have a vertical
tangent line anyway.
And that's what we do in calculations
most of the time in practice,
because it tends to be easier.
All right - that was probably a longer
explanation than you care for,
so I will just finish
with a quick word of caution.
A vertical tangent line is not
the same as a vertical asymptote.
I do not know where this error comes from,
but I have seen it often in
students' papers.
In this graph, the function h has the
vertical asymptote x = 0.
Notice that the function itself is not
defined at 0;
the limit of the function h itself, as x
approaches 0, is infinity.
By contrast, in this graph the function g
has a vertical tangent line at x = 0.
The function itself is perfectly well
defined and continuous at 0.
It is the derivative, g', which has limit
infinity as x approaches 0.
So don't confuse vertical asymptote
and vertical tangent line.
