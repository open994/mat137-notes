This seems, in principle, clear, but it

More specifically, here is the theorem
I want to prove.
I begin with a function f, defined on
an interval [a, b] (including the endpoints).
I assume f's derivative is zero.
I want to conclude that f is constant.
Now, I am only going to assume the
derivative is zero on the interior,
because often times functions are
not differentiable at the endpoints.
But in that case, in order to be able to
conclude that f is constant everywhere,
I just need to request continuity
(on the endpoints).
So if its derivative is zero, which is only
needed on the interior,
but it is continuous everywhere on [a, b],
then the function must be constant.
Once again, this is a theorem
that seems intuitive...
...seems...obvious?
But it isn't!
Geometrically it makes sense, but it
needs to be proven.
And the only way we know how to prove
it is with the Mean Value Theorem.
And now, we have the
Mean Value Theorem.
So, let's write down a proof.
First: what do I need to prove?
I want to show that for every x_1 and x_2
on the interval [a, b],
f(x_1) is equal to f(x_2)
This is simply the definition of
being constant.
For every two points in the domain,
the value of f at them is the same.
Since this begins with a for-all,
I am going to begin my proof
by fixing arbitrary values of x_1 and x_2
in the domain.
So I fix them for the rest of the proof,
and they are generic (arbitrary).
This is the same way we begin any
epsilon-delta proof for limits.
I am going to make an
assumption: for simplicity,
I will assume that x_1 is less than x_2.
I can do this because if x_1 is equal
to x_2, there is nothing to prove.
And, if x_2 were smaller than x_1,
the proof would work exactly the same.
So, whichever is smaller is
the one I am calling x_1.
Now, I want to use the
Mean Value Theorem
for the function f
on the interval [x_1, x_2]
Whenever you use the
Mean Value Theorem,
you must say for which function,
and on what interval.
How is this going to help me?
I need to verify this is OK,
but once I verify it is OK,
by the Mean Value Theorem I will be
able to say that there must exist
a value of c between
x_1 and x_2 such that
the derivative of f at c
is this quantity:
(f(x_2) – f(x_1))/(x_2 – x_1)
However, we know that the derivative
is always zero, so f'(c) is also zero.
Therefore, the numerator must be zero.
So, f(x_1) is equal to f(x_2), as we wanted,
and we are done!
Now, what is missing?
Before I use the Mean Value Theorem,
I have to verify that is OK.
I have to verify the hypotheses
of the Mean Value Theorem,
of which there are two.
f must be continuous
on the closed interval,
and differentiable on the open interval.
Well, this works.
We know f is continuous on the closed
interval [a, b] by our hypothesis,
so it is also continuous on the smaller
interval [x_1, x_2] (closed).
We know f is differentiable on
the open interval (a, b)
because we know the derivative is zero.
It exists, so f is differentiable.
Therefore, it must also be differentiable
on the smaller open interval (x_1, x_2).
That's it.
The hypotheses of the
Mean Value Theorem are true,
