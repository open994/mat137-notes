In this video, I will explain the
definition of limit of a sequence,
and some notation related to it.
I assume you are already familiar with
the definition of limit of a function,
and therefore you have seen other
similar statements with quantifiers.
I will begin with an example.
Let’s look at the sequence {n/(n + 1)},
with n starting at 0.
The first few terms are
0, 1/2, 2/3, 3/4, and so on...
We can draw them on a real line.
Here is the zeroth term, the first term,
the second term, the third, and so on...
As n increases,
these terms get closer and closer to 1,
and they end up crowding around 1.
If n is very large, the terms
are all very close to 1.
We say that the limit, as n approaches ∞,
of n/(n + 1), is 1.
I would like to write a
formal definition of this concept.
If you are already familiar with the
definition of limit of a FUNCTION,
as the variable approaches ∞,
you can just use the same definition:
change the domain from the reals
to the naturals, and that’s all.
That will be correct.
However, I want to get to the same
definition from a different perspective,
using something unique to sequences.
What does it mean that
the limit as n approaches ∞,
of a sequence {aₙ}, is a real number L?
In this picture,
I see that the terms of the sequence
get closer and closer to the limit.
I want to be more precise
than just saying “close to L”.
I will draw a small open
interval centred at L.
For this interval I drew,
I see that the first 3 terms
of the sequence are outside,
and after that,
all of the other terms are inside.
If I take a SMALLER interval, perhaps
instead of missing the first 3,
I will miss the first 10,
or the first 20,
but after that, the rest of the sequence
stays inside the open interval.
I am going to say that I want:
a TAIL of the sequence to stay
inside of the open interval.
A TAIL means “all of the terms of the
sequence after the first few ones”.
It could be the first 3, the first 20,
the first 10,000, or any other number.
The point is that after missing
a finite number of terms,
the rest of the sequence stays
inside of the open interval.
And I want this to be true
FOR ALL open intervals centred at L.
Great. I have a good idea.
Now I need to formalize it.
First, to construct an open interval
centred at L, I need to give its radius.
I will call the radius "ε",
and so, this is the interval from
L - ε to L + ε. (L - ε,L + ε)
Therefore, I want aₙ to be
between L - ε and L + ε.
Now to the difficult part:
I want these inequalities...
for which values of n?
I am tempted to say
“for every natural number n”...
But not quite! Remember, I said
I can miss the first few ones.
Instead, I will say that
there is a cut-off: n₀,
and the terms of the sequence
will be in the interval,
but only starting at n₀.
Finally, what am I saying about ε?
I want this to be true FOR ALL
open intervals centred at L,
so FOR ALL positive values of ε.
Then if I change the interval,
(if I make ε smaller)
the cut-off n₀ will also change.
(Perhaps I will miss the first
10 terms instead of the first 3.)
And there we have it! This is the
definition of the limit of a sequence.
Here is the same statement again.
This is what it means for the sequence {aₙ}
to converge to the real number L.
This is the formal, rigorous definition,
but I do like very much
the one sentence summary, with words.
A sequence {aₙ}
has limit a real number L,
when every open interval centred at L
contains a tail of the sequence.
(Where a tail means all of the elements
after the first few ones.)
This one sentence contains
ALL of the same information
as the rigorous statement
above with 3 quantifiers,
but I find it easier
to parse and to remember,
it helps gain intuition on what's going
on, and it gives us a geometric picture.
It also ties in with one of the most
important principles to keep in mind
when working with sequences:
to determine whether a sequence
has a limit or not,
it only matters what happens
to the tails of the sequence.
Changing the first 3 terms, the
first 12 terms, or the first 60 terms,
could never change whether
the sequence has a limit or not.
Next, a bit of notation.
In this situation,
we write that the
lim_(n → ∞) aₙ = the real number L.
(And there is the implicit convention
that n only takes natural values.)
Alternatively,
we may write this as aₙ → L.
And we say that
the limit of the sequence is L,
or that the sequence CONVERGES to L.
(as I wrote above)
One more note:
when I wrote that aₙ
is between L-ε and L+ε,
this is equivalent to saying that the
distance between L and aₙ is less than ε.
Perhaps you are more used to using this
second version with absolute values,
instead of the two original inequalities.
That is fine. The two expressions
are entirely equivalent.
To conclude, two more definitions.
When we say a sequence is CONVERGENT,
and nothing else,
we mean that it has a limit.
(and the limit must be a number)
“Convergent” is just a way
to say that the sequence has a limit,
without saying what the limit is.
Otherwise, we say that
the sequence is DIVERGENT.
So, a sequence may be convergent,
(for example, the sequence {1/n},
which is convergent to 0)
or it may be divergent.
And if it is divergent,
it may be divergent to ∞,
(for example, the sequence {n²})
or to -∞.
(for example, the sequence {-n})
But it could be divergent
in a different way,
for lack of a better term,
I will call this divergent "oscillating".
For example, the sequence {(-1)ⁿ},
(the first few terms are:
1, -1, 1 -1, 1, -1, and so on...)
is divergent. But it is not divergent
to ∞, and it is not divergent to -∞.
I could also write a precise
definition of divergent to ∞,
but I will leave that as an exercise.
