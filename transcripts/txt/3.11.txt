In this video, I will write the statement
of the chain rule for arbitrary functions,
explain the notation, and try to prove it.
Here is the equation for arbitrary
functions f and g.
The derivative of the composition
of f and g, evaluated at x,
equals the derivative of f
evaluated at g(x),
times the derivative of g evaluated at x.
I know that some students find
calculations with specific functions easy,
but get confused when I write the chain
rule this way.
I will try to unpack this formula
slowly with an example.
Let's say I want to take the derivative
of √(x³ + 1) with respect to x.
This should be familiar if you have
watched the previous video.
I can write this quantity as f(g(x)),
where f(x) = √x, and g(x) = x³ + 1.
f(g(x)) is the same as (f∘g)(x).
I know not everyone is comfortable with
this notation for composition,
so let's clarify it first.
For this video,
I want to be very strict and distinguish
between the name of a function,
and the value of that function at x,
which should be a number.
For example, I may call a function f,
and then its value at x is f(x).
Notice that if x is a number,
f(x) is also a number.
f(x) is not the name of the function.
The name of the function is just f.
For example, there is a function
called sine.
Sine by itself is not a number.
It is the name of the function.
But sin(x) is the value of this function
at x, which will be a number.
Now let's get to composition.
For functions f and g, f(g(x)) will be a
number (as long as the domains are right).
But what is the name of this function?
We normally write it f ∘ g,
and we read this f composed with g.
So the name of the function is f ∘ g,
the value of the function at x is f(g(x)).
You may think I am being overly pedantic
in making this distinction,
and I admit that it is common to abuse
notation and confuse f and f(x),
but there are a few situations where we
have to be very careful
with this distinction, and this is one of
them, as I will explain in a moment.
Okay, back to what I was doing.
In this example, what is each one
of these three quantities?
First, I have the derivative of the
composition.
The composition at x is this quantity.
When I take the derivative of the full
function,
it is the derivative with respect to x.
So this first term is the derivative
with respect to x of √(x³ + 1).
Second, I have the derivative of f,
evaluated at g(x).
f(x) is √(x,) so f'(x) is 1/2√x,
but instead of at x, I need to evaluate
it at g(x), which is x³ + 1.
So I get 1/2√(x³ + 1).
And the third piece is the easiest.
g(x) is x³ + 1, so g'(x) is 3x².
That's it. This is the chain rule.
Notice that I do not have any way to
write this first term nicely,
other than using the notation for
composition.
And that's why I was being strict
with the notation.
This formula summarizes the chain rule,
but if I want to think of it as a theorem
and prove it,
I need to be yet a little bit
more careful, as usual.
Here is the chain rule as a theorem.
I begin by introducing the notation.
a is a real number, 
f and g are functions.
At the bottom, I wrote the equation for
the chain rule.
I wrote it at a,
because we can study differentiability 
at a single point at a time.
The theorem, like most theorems, is an
if-then statement.
What are the hypotheses? In order for
this formula to be true, I need to assume
that each one of these derivatives exists.
So I assume that g is
differentiable at a,
and I assume that f is
differentiable at g(a).
Then I conclude that f ∘ g is
differentiable at a
and that the equation is true.
But notice that if f or g are not
differentiable,
then the composition may or may not
be differentiable.
We cannot conclude anything in that case,
and we cannot use the
chain rule in that case.
We have a theorem.
Shall we try to prove it?
I can write the derivative
of the composition at a
using the definition of
derivative as a limit.
I am going to multiply numerator and
denominator by g(x) - g(a),
which shouldn't change anything.
And then I can use a limit law to write
this limit of a product,
as a product of limits,
assuming the two individual limits exist.
Look at the second limit first.
This is g'(a).
And I can say so because we are assuming
g is differentiable at a.
The first limit will be a bit more
interesting.
I am hoping that this is f'(g(a))?
Let's write f'g(a)) as a limit.
I will use a different variable, u,
so as not to get confused.
It is lim_(u → g(a))
[f(u) - f(g(a))] ÷ [u - g(a)].
It looks similar enough.
Notice that g(a) is just a number.
In this new limit, the variable is u. In
the original limit, I instead have g(x).
I am tempted to say that u = g(x),
and as x → a, u → g(a),
because g is continuous.
Strictly speaking, 
what I would be doing is
composing the function in this limit 
with the function g.
Limits of compositions are a bit delicate,
but before making sure that this works,
I should pay attention to an
even bigger problem.
Right here in the second step, 
I multiplied and divided by g(x) - g(a).
What if I divided by zero?
We know when x → a that x is not a,
but g(x) may still be g(a).
For this proof to work,
I need an additional condition.
I need that when x is close to a
but not a, g(x) is not g(a).
And unfortunately,
this is not always true.
For example, for this function, on every
small open interval centered at 0,
there are plenty of values x
where g(x) equals g(0).
The proof would not work
for this function at all.
Sadly, this is not a proof. I still find
it an interesting argument to consider,
because it is such a natural thing to try.
And for most of the functions we usually
work with, it would be a valid proof.
This argument can be fixed into

To finish the video, I want to rewrite
the chain rule using Leibniz notation.
This will come in handy
in physical applications.
Here is the formula again.
This time,
let's assume we are working with physical
quantities, and let's give them names.
I will say u = g(x), and y = f(u),
which is also f(g(x)).
With this notation, g'(x) is du/dx.
What is f'(g(x))? It is dy/du.
And finally, the derivative of the
composition is dy/dx.
And that's the chain rule
in Leibniz notation.
This notation is very suggestive;
it suggests "just cancel the du's!"
But of course, it is not that simple.
du/dx is notation.
It does not mean that I divide a number
du over another number dx.
The notation has to be understood
in terms of limits.
Still, it is a convenient way to remember
the statement of the chain rule.
