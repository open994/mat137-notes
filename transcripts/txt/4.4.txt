In this video,
I want to explore the relation between
the derivative of a function,
and the derivative of its inverse.
I want to answer this question:
if a function is differentiable,
and it has an inverse,
must the inverse function
also be differentiable?
In other words, if f has a derivative,
must f⁻¹ have a derivative as well?
Moreover,
Can I write (f⁻¹)' in terms of f'?
To the first question the answer is:
not necessarily,
unless we add an extra condition.
I can explain it geometrically.
Consider as an example the function f
described by this graph, in blue.
This function is differentiable:
it has a derivative everywhere.
It is also one-to-one
and it has an inverse.
As you know, we can obtain the graph of
its inverse by reflecting the graph of f
with respect to the diagonal
of the first and third quadrants.
Here is the graph of the inverse, in red.
Now, if the graph in blue has a
well-defined tangent line at every point,
then the graph in red,
which is a reflection,
will also have a well-defined
tangent line at every point?
So, f⁻¹ should be differentiable
everywhere, right?
Not quite.
Consider the point I am labelling P
in the graph of f.
At that point, the tangent line is
horizontal, and the derivative is 0.
I am labelling Q the corresponding
point for the inverse.
Think about what happens when we reflect
a horizontal tangent line:
f⁻¹ must have a vertical tangent line
at Q, with infinite slope.
And thus, f⁻¹ is not differentiable at
Q.
Therefore, it is possible for f to have
a derivative while f⁻¹ does NOT.
But this is the only thing
that may go wrong.
We get a nice theorem.
Consider a function f that is
defined on an interval I:
IF:
f has an inverse,
f is differentiable,
and the derivative f’ is never 0.
THEN:
we can conclude that f⁻¹
is also differentiable.
Side note:
this little result should not be confused
with its “big brother” so-to-speak.
There is another theorem called the
Inverse Function Theorem.
The Inverse Function Theorem
is much more complicated,
and much more important as well.
It belongs in a
multivariable calculus course,
so it is beyond the scope of
this sequence of videos.
But just in case you have heard of the
Inverse function Theorem:
that is not what I am talking about.
The little result I am presenting
here is much less ambitious.
Back to what I was doing:
once we know that both f and f⁻¹
are differentiable,
how can I write the derivative of f⁻¹
in terms of the derivative of f?
We already know
a relation between f and f⁻¹:
f(f⁻¹(y)) = y,
FOR ALL y in some interval.
(I am being careless with domains.)
Then, if I take the
derivative of both sides,
I will get a relation between the
derivative of f
and the derivative of f⁻¹.
Let’s do that!
The derivative of the left equals
the derivative of the right.
On the left I need to use the chain rule:
I get f'(f⁻¹(y))⋅(f⁻¹)'(y).
And on the right, I simply get "1".
This is promising,
but this equation looks more complicated
than it actually is.
I can make it look nicer:
if I call f⁻¹(y): "x",
or equivalently, I set y = f(x),
then the equation says that
f'(x)⋅(f⁻¹)'(y) is 1.
And from here I can solve:
(f⁻¹)'(y) is 1/(f'(x)).
I just have to remember that f and f⁻¹
are evaluated at DIFFERENT points!
And that’s the equation
I was looking for!
