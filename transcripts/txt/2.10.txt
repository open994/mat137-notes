I want to explain what they say

Before I tell you

I want to begin

I want to prove

For example, if I want to calculate

Can I do this easily?
Yes, I can. Here is the plan:

The ONLY limits I have to prove

That's it.
Why these two?
Because they are the building blocks

every polynomial can be written

So after proving this basic limit,
I will prove that sum and product
behave well with respect to limits,
and that's what the limit laws say.
Specifically, the limit laws tell me
that IF I have two functions f and g
and I assume they have a limit
at the same point a;
the limit of f is L
and the limit of g is M,
THEN the sum must also have a limit;
it will be the sum L + M,
and similarly,
the product must also have a limit;
it will be the product L ⋅ M.
And there is a third limit law
about quotient
that I won't use in this particular case,

The limit law for the quotient requires
that we do NOT divide by 0.
And that's it! That's all I need.
I need to prove the value of two limits
from the definition,
and I need to prove
this theorem about sums,
and this theorem about products.
How will this help me?
How can I go from just THIS

rigorously?
Well, let's look at an example.
Let's say I want to calculate
lim_(x → 2) (x^4 - 3x).
This function is the sum
of two smaller functions: x^4, and -3x.
So using the limit law for sums,
I can write this limit
as the SUM of TWO limits.
And now, EVERY piece
is the product of functions.
-3x is -3 ⋅ x,
and x^4 is x ⋅ x ⋅ x ⋅ x.
So using the limit law for product,
I can write each of these limits
as a product of limits.
And look! I am now down
to the basic limits:
the limit of x,
and the limit of a constant.
So I can use that result to plug it in,
and end up with the value of the limit.
That's it! And the exact same process
is going to work for ANY polynomial.
