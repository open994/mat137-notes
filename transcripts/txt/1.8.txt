The negation of a conditional statement
is NOT a conditional statement.
I will explain why in this video.
Let us look at this example.
A is a subset of the real numbers,
and I'm going to consider
this statement:
"if x is an element of A,
then x must be positive."
My objective is to write the NEGATION
of this statement.
Before I do, that let's remember EXACTLY
what the conditional statement means.
This is an "if P, then Q."
So remember: that means
that whenever the "if" part is true,
the "then" part must be true as well.
But whenever the "IF" part is FALSE,
we don't care whether the "THEN" part
is true or false.
Perhaps x is in this situation,
OR perhaps x is in THIS situation,
OR perhaps x is in THIS situation.
Now, an important point:
which values of x are we talking about?
When I say this statement,
do I mean it's true for... which x?
And the answer is: it's true for ALL x.
Saying that
if x is in A then x is positive
means that ALL real numbers x
must be in one of these three cases.
There is a HIDDEN quantifier
that is not written explicitly
in conditional statements like this one,
but once again, if I say
"if x is in A then x is positive,"
what I actually mean
is that ALL real numbers satisfy
that if they are in A
then they are positive.
And now that I have written it this way,
it is easier to write a negation.
What's the negation
of "every real number
is in one of those three cases"?
Well that would be
that there exists
at least one real number
that is NOT in ANY of them.
But "not in any of them"
means it must be in the fourth case.
And that's it!
We have written the negation
of the original statement.
There are two important
lessons to learn from this.
First: the negation of an if-then
is NEVER another if-then.
Notice that
the negation of an if-then
is always going to refer
to the fourth case:
the "IF" part is TRUE,
and the "THEN" part is FALSE.
And second: beware!
Many conditional statements
like this one
have a HIDDEN, IMPLICIT quantifier!
And when you write the negation,
you ABSOLUTELY MUST write
the quantifier explicitly.
