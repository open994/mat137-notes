In this video,
I will list the five most important
properties of definite integrals.
Here they are all at once.
In a moment, I'll go through
them one by one.
Once I explain them,
these properties are all
pretty intuitive.
They make sense geometrically,
and they are easy to believe.
Of course, to be rigorous,
we would need to prove them,
and we should do it from
the definition of integral.
I will skip the proofs
in this video.
Let's begin.
The first two properties say
that definite integrals are linear.
What does this mean?
The integral of the sum
of two functions, f and g,
equals the integral of f
plus the integral of g.
And the integral of a constant C
times a function f
equals the constant C
times the integral of f.
Linearity means these
two properties.
You probably already assumed
they would be true, and we are
going to use them anyway.
I should clarify that what I
wrote by itself is a bit sloppy.
To be more rigorous,
I should write these properties
as theorems,
and I need to specify which functions
must be integrable, where.
For example,
the rigorous way to write
the first property is this theorem.
Let f an g be bounded functions
on the interval [a, b].
IF both f and g are integrable on [a, b],
THEN f + g is also integrable on [a, b],
and I get the equation from above.
But if f and/or g are
non-integrable,
then the sum may or may not
be integrable
and the equation does not apply.
This is the same as the limit laws,
or the differentiation rules.
I will leave it as an exercise
for you to write the second
property as a theorem.
In any case, the equations alone
are a good way to summarize
the properties,
and to remember them.
Let's move onto the third property.
It is about comparing integrals
of the same function over
different intervals.
This one is best explained
with a picture.
Here I have the graph of a function f,
and three points a, b and c,
in that order, in the horizontal axis.
In this picture, the integral
from a to c represents this area.
The integral from a to b
represents THIS area.
And the integral from b to c
represents THIS area.
This suggests that the
integral from a to c
equals the integral from a to b
plus the integral from b to c.
This makes sense geometrically,
and indeed, that is the third property.
Like before, if I want to be rigorous,
I can write this as a theorem.
I have to pay attention to what
must be integrable, where.
The theorem says:
I begin with a bounded function f,
on the interval [a, c],
where a < b < c.
IF f is integrable on the two
smaller sub-intervals, [a, b] and [b, c],
THEN we can conclude
that f is also integrable on the
bigger interval, [a, c].
And, we get the equation above.
And that's the third property.
But we can expand this a little bit.
So far we have only defined
a definite integral from a to b
when a is less than b.
Let's go further.
Here is a new definition.
I am going to declare that
the integral from b to a
is –(the integral from a to b).
I can do this.
Up to this point we had
never integrated backwards.
This is a new concept.
So, I get a new definition for it.
From now on, this is what
a backwards integral will mean.
And in addition,
the integral from a to a
will always be zero by definition.
This is extremely useful.
With these new definitions,
the identity I had,
that the integral from a to c
is the integral from a to b
plus the integral from b to c,
is true NO MATTER the order
of the numbers a, b, and c!
For example, if c is between
a and b, or anything else,
even if some of them are equal!
I will leave the proof as an exercise.
It is a proof by cases,
depending on the order of a, b, and c.
Many cases.
Let's look at one case as an example,
to show that this notation
is reasonable.
Let's consider the case
when a equals c.
Then, I have the integral
from a to b
plus the integral from b to a,
equals the integral from a to a,
which should be zero.
In other words, this equation says
that the integral from b to a
equals –(the integral from a to b).
Great!
This example is not a proof.
This example suggests that
the new definitions are reasonable.
They make sense;
they are consistent.
So once again, these identity is true
for all value of a, b and c
as long as the function is
integrable where it needs to be.
The fourth property deals with
the geometric interpretation
of integral as area.
That was our original motivation
to define integral,
but this time I want
to go a bit further.
As you know,
if I have a function f like this one,
over an interval [a, b],
the integral of f from a to b
is the area of the region between
the graph of f and the x-axis,
shaded in light blue in this picture.
However...
in this interpretation, we had
always been assuming,
perhaps implicitly,
that the function f was positive.
What if f isn't positive?
Think back to the definition of integral.
Upper and lower sums,
upper and lower integrals...
At no point in the definition
did we need f to be positive.
The definition is exactly the same
for positive or negative functions.
In other words,
we have already defined
integrability for bounded functions
regardless of whether they
are positive or negative.
However, we only had a geometric
interpretation of the integral as area
for positive functions...
so far!
So, what does the integral represent
for a negative function?
For example, this one?
In this case,
the integral is MINUS the area between
the graph and the x-axis,
pictured in orange.
Why?
Think back to the definition of integral.
The heights of the rectangles
in the upper and lower sums
are values of f,
and are therefore negative.
But other than for the sign,
the upper and lower sums
are still measured in area.
If necessary, pause the video
to convince yourself of this.
What if I have a function that takes both
positive and negative values?
Putting together what I just said,
plus the previous property,
we can split the region between
the graph of f and the x-axis
into two pieces.
The integral will be:
the area of the piece of
the region above the x-axis,
MINUS the area of the piece of
the region below the x-axis.
There are many other types of regions
whose areas we can write
as integrals,
but I will leave it at this for now.
Just to make it very clear:
areas are always positive,
but definite integrals may equal
plus the area, or minus the area.
Finally, the last property deals
with inequalities and integrals.
IF I have two functions f and g,
and f(x) is less than or equal to g(x)
for all x in the interval,
THEN the integral of f is less than
or equal to the integral of g.
This one also makes sense geometrically.
Just compare one area
with the other area.
Unlike the other properties,
I should write it more rigorously
as a theorem,
paying attention to what must
be integrable on what.
But I will leave that as an exercise.
And that's it!
These are the five most important
properties of definite integrals.
I have a final comment.
Pretty much everything
you will ever need to do
with definite integrals
relates to these five properties,
and nothing else!
To a certain extent,
if you don't care about
rigorous definition or proofs,
you could forget the formal
definition of integral
