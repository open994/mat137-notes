This is my second example of how to find
the asymptotes of a function,
given its equation.
I'm going to look at the function g
defined by:
g(x) = x + 2 + 1/(e^(x²) + 1).
This function has one asymptote but
it is neither vertical nor horizontal.
I invite you to pause the video
and try to find it.
Think about: what does the graph of this
function look like as x approaches ∞?
After that, continue watching.
The first thing I notice
for this function,
is that the domain is all of the reals.
(It's defined for all real numbers.)
I have a quotient,
but exponentials are always positive,
so this + 1 is also always
positive and never vanishes,
so this is always defined.
and also this function is continuous
on its domain, which is the reals.
Since it's defined and continuous
on all of ℝ,
it can't possibly have
vertical asymptotes.
Next, let's see if
it has horizontal asymptotes.
So I need to calculate the limit
as x approaches ±∞.
Well let's look at this part here:
as x approaches ∞ or -∞, x² approaches ∞,
e to the power of that also approaches ∞
and + 1 doesn't change it,
so what I have here is 1/∞, which is 0.
So this whole term approaches 0
as x approaches ∞,
and the same thing happens for -∞.
So for large values of x
or small values of x,
the function is pretty much
just like x + 2, and nothing else.
In other words, the limit of this function
as x goes to ∞,
is just the limit of this piece,
which is ∞.
And at -∞, it's also the limit
of this piece, which is -∞.
So there are no horizontal asymptotes.
However, in doing this,
I have discovered something.
I just said that as x goes to ±∞
this goes to 0,
so my function is pretty much
just like x + 2,
and x + 2 (or y = x + 2)
is the equation of a line.
So what I'm saying here, is that my
function is pretty much just a line,
and something that gets very small at ∞.
Another way to write this...
is that, as x approaches ∞,
the limit of [g(x) - (x + 2)]
is just this piece,
and we said that that limit was 0.
(and the same thing happens at ±∞)
And now, this is the condition
to have a slant asymptote.
That the difference between the function
and the line, goes to 0.
Specifically, let's think about what
this means for the graph.
In these axes, I have pre-drawn the
line y = x + 2, and what I know,
is that as x goes to ∞,
the difference between g and x + 2,
goes to 0.
So, the graph of g should stay very close
to the line for large values of x,
and also at -∞, but I know more.
I notice as x goes to ∞,
the difference is always positive.
(We argue it was always positive.)
So the difference is going
to be small and positive.
So I know g stays very close
to x + 2 and is slightly above,
and here also g stays very
close to x + 2 and slightly above.
So, definitely the distance between the
graph of the function,
and the graph of the line, has limit 0,
as x goes to ∞.
So by definition,
we have found a slant asymptote.
Finally, what will the rest
of the graph look like?
Well, I know that the graph is always
this line, + something positive.
So, if I had to guess, I would say maybe
it's going to look something like this,
but I can't quite be certain.
It could also do something more exotic,
and go up and then down and up and down,
or something like that.
If I wanted to be sure, I should look at
the first and second derivative,
and see what the monotonicity
and concavity is,
and then with that,
and the asymptote,
I will be able to
graph it perfectly well.
But what I know for sure,
is that for large values of x,
and for small values of x,
the graph is staying
very close to the line y = x + 2.
