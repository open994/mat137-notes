What is a function?
There are a few concepts related
to the notion of function...
...that can get confusing.
Strictly speaking,
they all have a very precise definition.
But in some contexts,
we relax those definitions,
we abuse notation,
and we use the terms
to mean something slightly different.
Sometimes students may learn
the same theorem...
...in two apparently contradictory ways,
depending on whether it is in a calculus
class, or in a linear algebra class.
But there is no contradiction if
we understand the conventions.
In this sequence of videos,
I will do two things:
first, I will explain the proper use of
these terms when we WANT to be strict.
Second,
I will explain the conventions used
in calculus to relax those definitions.
If you have taken a logic course,
perhaps you have learned
the definiton of function as:
"a subset of a cartesian product."
What I will write here is EQUIVALENT,
but in plainer English.
A function consists of three pieces:
first, a set of INPUTS called the DOMAIN.
In this example, the DOMAIN is the set A,
with elements 1, 2, and 3.
Second, a set of POTENTIAL OUTPUTS
called the CODOMAIN.
In this example, the CODOMAIN is the
set B, with elements 4, 5, and 6.
And third, a RULE that matches
each INPUT in the DOMAIN,
to exactly one OUTPUT in the CODOMAIN.
In the picture below, I am representing
the matches with arrows.
Notice the notation that I am using:
I am giving a NAME to the function,
in this case: "f".
Then, for each element "x" in the DOMAIN,
I am calling f of x (f(x)) the
corresponding element in the CODOMAIN.
So, the NAME of the function is "f",
NOT "f(x)".
f(x) is an element in the CODOMAIN,
NOT the NAME of the function.
Back to the example, there are only
three elements in this domain,
so I can just list all of the matches:
f(1) is 4, f(2) is 4, and f(3) is 6.
That is the RULE.
In most examples; however,
I will have an infinite domain,
and I will not be able to
list all of the matches.
Instead, I will probably describe the
RULE with an equation.
Why did I describe the elements in B
as POTENTIAL outputs?
I want to distinguish between
CODOMAIN, and RANGE.
The CODOMAIN is the set
of POTENTIAL outputs.
In this example, it is the set {4,5,6}.
The RANGE is the set of ACTUAL ouputs.
In this example,
it only has two elements: 4 and 6.
The CODOMAIN is a set
we determine beforehand,
from which we are allowed
to choose outputs.
And then, once we define the RULE,
we find out that we are only using
some of the outputs,
and those form the RANGE.
Notice that to define a function,
I only need to give the DOMAIN,
the CODOMAIN, and the RULE.
I do not need to explain
what the RANGE is.
The RANGE can be calculated
from the rest of the information.
Finally, one piece of notation.
If you read an expression like this:
f, colon, A, arrow, B (f: A → B),
it means that "f" is the NAME of a
function, A is the DOMAIN,
and B is the CODOMAIN.
It is just shorthand for all of that.
For example, 
I could define a function f,
with a DOMAIN of all the reals,
and a CODOMAIN of all the reals.
With the RULE given by the equation:
f(x) = 3 + x² + sin(x).
So far, I have explained the STRICT way
to define a function,
and to refer to its components.
Next, I will explain some conventions.
You may be thinking:
in calculus, or in pre-calculus,
I am used to defining functions in a much
easier way: just by giving the equation.
For example,
I may say the function g is defined by
g(x) = 1/(x^2), and say nothing else.
We do this all of the time.
Is it okay?
There is a convention that we use
in single-variable calculus.
We agree that it is okay to define
a function by just giving the RULE.
(normally as an equation)
And then, by default,
we assume that the domain is the
LARGEST SUBSET of the reals,
on which the RULE makes sense.
And we assume that the
codomain is all of the reals.
For example, when I define the function g
by the equation g(x) = 1/(x^2),
if I do not say anything else;
implicitly, I mean:
for which values of x does
this equation make sense?
I can divide by any non-zero number, so
x can be any real number EXCEPT for zero.
So that's what I mean.
The domain is all of the real numbers
EXCEPT for zero;
and I also imply that the codomain is ℝ.
This convention is useful,
because we are only talking about
this SPECIFIC type of function,
it saves a lot of time.
But once you move to other fields
of math, like abstract algebra,
where the domain and codomain may not
even be sets of numbers,
then you will have to be careful,
and specify domains and codomains
in order to define a function.
There is a second convention
worth mentioning.
Look at these two functions.
Are they the SAME function?
STRICTLY speaking,
they are NOT the same.
They have the same domain, and
the same rule, even the same range,
but they have DIFFERENT codomains.
They differ by the element "5".
In practice, particularly in calculus,
we often abuse notation and PRETEND that
these two functions ARE the same function,
and we use the same name for both.
Effectively,
we treat the codomain as irrelevant,
and we only pay attention to the range.
If you had never heard the word
“codomain” before, this may be why.
I will talk more about this
abuse of notation in a later video...
...about SURJECTIVE functions.
But remember that
in other areas of mathematics,
this distinction may be important,
and in that case,
we will stick to saying
that these two functions are DIFFERENT.
To make matters worse,
this notation is not universal!
The names I have introduced here are
the most commonly used names in math.
But sometimes people use different names,
at least in some areas
of computer science.
They still use the word DOMAIN
in the same way that I have.
But what I have called the CODOMAIN,
they call the RANGE,
and what I have called the RANGE,
they call the IMAGE.
This makes it confusing,
because now RANGE may mean
two different things,
depending on who you ask;
but don’t blame me!
The moral is,
that if you are working
on math with somebody else,
you should make sure that
you agree on notation...
...so that you can understand each other.
