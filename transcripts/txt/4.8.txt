We know that the derivative
of eˣ is itself,
but what is the derivative of an
exponential with a DIFFERENT base?
That is the goal of this video.
The base "a" could be
any positive real number,
it has to be positive, otherwise the
exponential does not make sense.
I will use a trick
that is very common in mathematics.
I want to solve the general problem
FOR ALL values of a,
and I already know the answer
for one value: "e".
I am going to try to write
the general case (FOR ALL a),
in terms of the one case I can solve.
So, write aˣ in terms of eˣ.
And here is how to do it:
any positive number "a", can be
written as eˡⁿ⁽ᵃ⁾.
(This is by definition of ln.)
Then aˣ, can be written as (eˡⁿ⁽ᵃ⁾)ˣ.
And now I can use
the properties of powers.
(And perhaps it is more common
to write this as: eˣˡⁿ⁽ᵃ⁾.)
And that's all I need!
I have written aˣ in terms of eˣ.
That's going to allow me
to take the derivative.
So now, the derivative of aˣ is...
Instead of as aˣ,
I will think of it as eˣˡⁿ⁽ᵃ⁾.
I know the derivative of eˣ is itself,
so the derivative of e to a function,
by chain rule,
will be itself, multiplied by
the derivative of the exponent.
And now that I have taken the derivative,
I can re-write this
as it was originally (as aˣ).
And here I have x multiplied by a number,
so the derivative is just
the multiplying number: ln(a).
And that's my final answer!
The derivative of an
arbitrary exponential is itself,
multiplied by ln of the base.
Notice that when a = e,
I get ln(e), which is 1.
So this term disappears, and I recover
the identity that I already knew.
This strategy,
reducing a bigger problem to a
smaller problem we have already solved,
is very common in mathematics.
