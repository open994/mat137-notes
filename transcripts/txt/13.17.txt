Infinite sums are not commutative.
This is perhaps one of the most
surprising results about series,
and it is at the heart of the difference
between absolutely convergent and
conditionally convergent series.
That is the topic of this video.
I'm going to start with an example.
Let's look at this series:
the alternating harmonic series
1 - 1/2 + 1/3 - 1/4, and so on...
This series is convergent, thanks to
the alternating series test.
Now I'm going to compare with this
other one, which is a reordering.
I am adding the very same terms,
but I change the order.
Now I am adding two positive terms,
then one negative,
then two positive, then one negative,
and so on...
But the terms I am
adding are the same.
If these were finite sums,
they would be equal.
But they're infinite sums!
I'm not going to prove it,
but it is possible to show that
the first sum adds to ln(2),
and the second one
adds to (3/2)⋅ln(2).
This is a surprise. This definitely
doesn't happen with finite sums,
and I wish it didn't
happen for infinite sums...
So why is this?
The theorem says, that whether we can
reorder the terms of a series or not,
depends on what type
of convergent it is.
If a series is absolutely convergent,
then what I just showed will not happen,
Then it can definitely be reordered, and
this won't change the value of the sum.
If the original series was convergent,
after I change the order, it is still
convergent, with the same sum.
But if the series
is conditionally convergent,
then, well, reordering it
may well change the value.
And it is actually worse!
If a series is conditionally convergent,
then it is always possible to reorder it
to make it convergent
to ANY number we want.
Choose what you want the
value of the sum to be,
and there's a way to order it
so that that's the value of the sum.
And there is also a way to reorder it to
make it divergent in any way we want.
(Perhaps divergent to ∞,
or oscillating.)
In other words, conditionally
convergent series are bad.
This theorem is quite shocking.
It seems very powerful,
and very surprising,
and it looks like it's going
to be difficult to prove.
But surprisingly, it
is not that difficult.
I'm going to try to explain what's bad
about conditionally convergent series
first with an example,
and then we'll see that that works
for all conditionally convergent series.
So I'm going to look more
closely at this example:
the alternating harmonic series
1 - 1/2 + 1/3 - 1/4, and so on...
I'm going to point out that this example
has three important properties.
First, if I look at the terms that I am
adding and subtracting without the signs,
the limit of those terms is 0.
This will be true for any
conditionally convergent series,
because it must be true
for any convergent series.
Second, I'm going to look separately at
the negative and positive terms.
If I look just at the negative terms:
-1/2 - 1/4 - 1/6, and so on,
then they add up to -∞.
In this case,
one way to see that is if I take a common
factor of -1/2 from these terms,
they give me the harmonic series
itself, which I know to be ∞.
And separately, if I look just at the
positive terms 1 + 1/3 + 1/5, and so on,
they add up to ∞.
One way to justify
that is by comparing
the positive terms with the negative
terms in absolute values;
and the positive terms are greater than
the negative terms in absolute value,
so they also diverge to ∞.
And these three properties
turn out to be true for
any conditionally convergent series.
Well it turns out that
whenever I have a series
where the limit of the
terms I'm adding is 0,
the positive terms add to ∞,
and the negative terms add to -∞,
then I'm going to be able to reorder it
to get any value of the sum I want.
And here is how!
As an example, I'm going to try to
reorder the alternating harmonic series,
so that the sum is 7.
But there's nothing special about 7.
It's a number I chose at random.
The same will work
with any other number.
To do this, first,
I'm going to add positive terms
(just positive terms,
ignoring the negatives)
until a partial sum goes above 7.
I know this is possible because all
the positive terms add up to ∞;
therefore, this would be possible
to go as far as I need.
Then, I'm going to add negative terms
until a partial sum goes below 7.
Again, that's possible because all the
negative terms together add up to -∞.
And then, I go back to Step 1,
and keep doing that
(adding positive terms
until I go beyond 7,
and adding negative terms
until I go below 7).
If I do this, it will definitely give me
a reordering of the original series.
Why will this new series
be converging to 7?
Now is when I use the third property.
Notice that the limit of the terms I am
adding and subtracting is 0,
so every time I get to a partial sum
that goes above or below 7,
they will be staying closer to 7,
because they will only
go above or below by 1/n,
so they will be staying closer
and closer to 7,
and thus, the limit of the sequence
of partial sums is 7, that's it!
I love this theorem because
it's shocking and surprising,
and yet the proof
is delightfully simple.
In summary, absolutely convergent series
are the good type of convergent.
They behave nicely (they have similar
properties to finite sums),
and we can do with them many of the
things we can do with finite sums.
But conditionally convergent
series are treacherous.
They may be convergent,
but don't trust them!
They misbehave, and they don't have the
properties we would like them to have.
