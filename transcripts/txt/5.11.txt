This video is about monotonicity
of functions.
When is a function 
increasing or decreasing,
and what does this have to do
with derivatives?
This will be our third application
of the Mean Value Theorem.
Before I begin,
an important clarification.
Saying that a function is increasing is
NOT the same thing
as saying it has positive derivative.
These two concepts are related,
but they are NOT THE SAME.
Positive derivative is NOT the definition
of a function increasing.
Here is why.
This is an example of a function
that is increasing everywhere,
but it has derivative zero at a POINT.
This graph represents another function
that is also increasing everywhere,
but it has a corner at a point, so it is
not differentiable at one point.
It doesn't have a derivative there.
This is a function that is
increasing everywhere,
and yet, at infinitely many points, it is not
even continuous, let alone differentiable.
So, a function can be increasing
without having positive derivative.
We should then define
"increasing function" formally.
Here is the definition.
I begin with a function defined
on an interval I.
I want to say what it means
to be increasing on I.
It is always "a function is increasing
ON A DOMAIN."
That means this condition.
That, for every two points
x_1 and x_2 in I,
if x_1 is less than x_2,
THEN f(x_1) is less than f(x_2).
So that means, if I take a bigger input,
then it produces a bigger output.
That is going to mean that
the graph is always going up.
I am using this definition, where I say
that f(x_1) is LESS than f(x_2),
but there is a related concept, which
I am going to call NON-DECREASING,
in which case I say that x_1 < x_2 implies
that f(x_1) is less than or equal to f(x_2).
So, increasing just means the graph
always "goes up."
And non-decreasing means that the
graph always goes up or stays constant.
Of course, there are also the notions
of decreasing, and non-increasing,
which will be similar.
I assume you can write the definitions.
Just reverse the second inequality.
Unfortunately, this notation
is not universal.
While some books use this convention
and call these two things
"increasing" and "non-decreasing,"
other books use "strictly increasing"
and "increasing" instead,
which is unfortunate, because
it means "increasing"
could mean either one of the two,
depending on which math book
or math paper you are reading.
There is no way around it.
Both are used.
So, if you are reading a paper
or a book, make sure you know
which definition it is using. And if you
are doing math with somebody else,
agree on a definition before starting.
So, that is the definition.
The three graphs of functions
I looked at before satisfy this property.
They are increasing.
This is an increasing function.
This is an increasing function.
And this also satisfies the definition,
so it is an increasing function.
Okay! Now that we know
what increasing means,
what is the relation between
increasing and positive derivative?
Here is the theorem.
Let's say I begin with a function f,
defined on an open interval (a, b).
IF I know that the derivative
of the function is positive,
THEN I can conclude that the function
is increasing on that interval.
That is the theorem. Positive derivative
implies increasing function.
But, a function could be increasing
without having positive derivative.
That is the theorem for
an open interval.
There is a companion to this one.
If instead, I am looking at
a function defined
on a closed and bounded
interval [a, b],
if I want to conclude that the function
is increasing on [a, b],
then I only need to require that the
derivative is positive
on the OPEN interval – on the interior.
But, on the endpoints,
I must require continuity.
So, positive derivative on the interior
and continuity everywhere,
implies that the function is increasing
on that whole interval.
These two theorems need proofs.
I invite you to write them.
A couple of videos ago, a wrote a proof
for a similar result,
that if the derivative of a function is zero,
then the function must be constant.
That was a direct application of the
Mean Value Theorem.
This works in exactly the same way.
So I invite you to rewatch that video –
– I will add a link in the description –
– and try to imitate the proof.
You only have to modify it slightly
to prove these two theorems.
That is a good exercise to make sure
you understand the Mean Value Theorem,
and how to write these kinds of proofs.
So, after seeing the theorems
and their proofs,
here is a summary of
what we know so far.
On an open interval, IF
the derivative is zero,
THEN the function must be constant.
IF the derivative is positive,
THEN the function must be increasing.
IF the derivative is negative,
THEN the function must be decreasing.
I have not written the theorem
for this last case,
but it works exactly the
same as the others.
But, if I just have a point, where the
derivative is zero or does not exist,
then in principle, if could be anything.
It could be part of a bigger interval where
the function is increasing,
or we may have a local extremum
at that point, or something else.

to determine the intervals where
