The Mean Value Theorem is perhaps the most
interesting result in calculus.
And yet, when you look at the statement, at
first sight it appears not very interesting,
technical, boring...
Honestly, it appears useless.
So why do we care? Or, should we care?
In this video I want to try to explain why
this theorem, despite its appearances,
is very important.
The goal of the Mean Value Theorem is
to help us obtain information about a function
using something about its derivative.
When we know something about the derivative,
what can we say about the function?
For example, here is a question related to
it.
We know that constant functions have zero
derivative.
Are those the only ones?
Are there other functions with zero derivative?
It appears not.
So as a theorem, are constant functions the
only ones with zero derivative?
Here it is written as a theorem.
If I take an open interval and a function
defined on it...
IF I assume the derivative is always zero
on it, THEN the function must be constant.
This is an example - 
the easiest example I can think of -
of a theorem where we use something 
about the derivative
to conclude something
about the function.
This is the simplest such example.
Since it is the simplest, to begin with
let's prove this little theorem.
.... Hmmm ...
Surprisingly, I do not even how to start!
It turns out that despite looking very simple,
this is not easy to prove.
Pause the video if you need to and try to
do it.
There is no clear way to prove this.
Actually, what is happening is that we
do not know ANY way
to prove this simple theorem 
without using the Mean Value Theorem.
And if we do not know any way to prove this,
then we do not know any way to prove many
more things that are much more complicated.
Any interesting theorem in calculus
about applications of the derivatives
are not as easy to prove as they look.
In fact, they ALL rely on the Mean Value Theorem.
In fact, EVERYTHING relies on the Mean Value
Theorem.
Here is a list of just some of the consequences
of the Mean Value Theorem.
To prove that if a function has zero derivative,
it must be constant: that's the MVT.
To prove that if a function has positive derivative,
then it must be increasing: that's the MVT.
All integration methods rely on the
MVT.
L'Hopital's Rule, if you are familiar with
it, relies on a variant of the MVT.
The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus...
...Taylor's theorem, for writing functions as
Taylor series with the right remainder...
...or the multivariable calculus result about 
cross-double derivatives being equal...
...and pretty much anything important
in calculus, whether it's big or small,
requires the MVT to prove it.
So, it is worth learning and proving the Mean
Value Theorem.
But that is quite an investment, because
to get to the Mean Value Theorem,
we have to take a few steps. I am not
going to state what this theorem says yet.
Before we are ready to state and prove the
Mean Value Theorem we need a little lemma:
a simplified version that is called
Rolle's Theorem.
It is interesting on its own, but the main
reason we'll study Rolle's Theorem
is as a stepping stone to get to the MVT.
And in order to prove Rolle's Theorem we need
two other theorems:
One that we already know, the Extreme Value Theorem, and another one that,
for lack of a better name, I am going to call the Local Extreme Value Theorem.
So this is going to be the path that we take
with the sole goal of eventually getting to
the Mean Value Theorem... and then there will
be profit.
All the applications we want will come out
of the Mean Value Theorem.
So, what about you?
Should you care about the Mean Value Theorem?
To be honest, it depends. Perhaps yes, perhaps
not.
What is your goal when you study calculus?
If you just want to use calculus tools, I
suggest you skip the MVT.
You do not need it, you will never use it.
It's not worth it.
But if you want to learn to prove the theorems,
and to know why they are true
and prove them rigorously, then 
you absolutely need the MVT,
since there is little or nothing that you
can prove without it.
