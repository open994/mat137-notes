They will make the rest

So far, I have been casually
saying that a function is analytic
when it equals its Taylor series.
But, that is not precise enough.
I need to specify
WHICH Taylor series,
and I need to specify
on which domain.
So, to begin, let's write
a more precise definition.
I start with a C^infinity function f,
on an open interval I.
I require C^infinity so that
we can at least write
its Taylor series.
Now, let's fix a point a
in the domain,
and let us call S_a the
Taylor series of f
centred at a.
I will say that f is analytic at a when...
...I want to say when f(x) = S_a(x),
but for which values of x?
For all values of x in some
open interval centred at a.
In other words, this means
that the function
and the Taylor series of the
function centred at a
are equal NEAR a.
That was the definition
of "analytic at a".
If instead I say that
"f is analytic"
and I do not specify a point,
it means that f is analytic
at all points in its domain.
That's it.
That is the precise definition
of analytic,
which we often
carelessly summarize as:
"the function equals its Taylor series."
Here are some results
about analytic functions,
which I am not going to prove.
First, polynomials
are analytic.
They are finite power series.
Second, sums, products, 
quotients
(as long we we don't
divide by zero)
and compositions of analytic
functions are all analytic.
Moreover, the derivatives
and antiderivatives
of analytic functions
are also analytic.
In short:
All common operations
with analytic functions
produce analytic functions.
But, there is more.
An analytic function is
written as a power series.
We know that in the interior
of the interval of convergence,
a power series can be manipulated
like a polynomial.
So, all the operations above
can be performed with Taylor series
the same way we would perform
them with polynomials.
And finally, we know that the
Taylor series of a function
at a point is unique.
Thanks to all these properties, 
my goal now is very humble.
I just need to prove
that the exponential and the
sin function are analytic.
That's all.
Why only these two functions?
Because all other
elementary functions
can be written in terms
of these two functions,
polynomials, and the
operations I listed.
Moreover, once I know
these two are analytic,
getting the Taylor series of
new functions will be easy
by manipulating the
Taylor series I know.
I will illustrate how to do this
with examples
in a video linked in the description.

How do I prove that a
function IS analytic?
Let's review the notation
and make a plan.
Let f be a function defined
on an open interval I,
and let a be a point on I.
If the function is C^n,
then we can write the n-th
Taylor polynomial.
As you know, it is given
by this sum.
We can always write the function
as the n-th Taylor polynomial
plus the n-th remainder.
The remainder is just the difference:
the error we make
when we approximate a function
by the polynomial.
If the function is C^infinity,
then we can write
the Taylor series.
As you know, it is given by
this infinite sum.
We also know that the Taylor
series is the limit
of the n-th Taylor polynomials
as n approaches infinity.
This is because the
Taylor polynomials
are the partial sums corresponding
to the Taylor series.
Finally, the function is
anaytic when, in addition,
the function equals
the Taylor series.
In other words, analytic means
that the error made when using
the Taylor series
as an approximation is 0.
Now, the Taylor series is the limit
of the Taylor polynomials.
Therefore the error made when
using the Taylor series
as an approximation is
the limit of the remainder
of the Taylor polynomials.
In other words, being analytic
is equivalent to saying
that the limit of the n-th
remainders,
as n approaches infinity,
must be zero.
This is what we need to prove
in order to show that
a function is analytic.
You may be thinking:
"haven't we already established this?"
"Don't we already know that the
limit of the remainders is zero?"
Let's be careful.
There are two important limits
involved in the remainders.
What we know, from the very
definition of Taylor polynomials,
is that if I fix n, the order
of the Taylor polynomial,
and then I make x approach a,
then the error approaches zero,
and it approaches zero FAST.
That is what we knew.
But in order to prove that
a function is analytic,
I need to look at a different limit.
I need to fix x, and then
I need to take the limit
as n approaches infinity
of the remainder,
and I need that limit to be zero.
Okay then.
To prove the limit of
the remainders is zero,
it will help to estimate
the remainders.
There is a perfect tool
for this purpose:
the remainder theorems.
I say "theorems" in plural because
they are a family of theorems.
They are all very similar.
A typical remainder theorem
has this form.
IF [some hypotheses are true],
THEN we get [some formula
for the remainder].
There are at least three common
versions of the remainder theorem:
Lagrange's form, Cauchy's form,
and the Integral form.
They each give a different
looking formula,
but for our purposes, they
are interchangeable.
They all allow us to
estimate the remainders,
and to prove that functions
are analytic.
I won't write a proof for any of them,
but I will mention
that all their proofs use the
Mean Value Theorem,
or perhaps Rolle's Theorem.
This should not be a surprise.
Every important theorem in calculus
relies on the Mean Value Theorem.

precise statement

Once again, this is just one

This is not more or less

Let n be a natural number,

Notice that we only

The theorem says that

In a way, this is reminiscent

The Mean Value Theorem says that

This theorem says that
under certain hypotheses,
