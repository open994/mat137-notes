In this video, I will introduce the chain
rule with examples.
This is the differentiation rule we need
to take derivatives of
compositions of functions.
For now, I will only present some
examples of calculations.
I won't even write a general formula yet.
In the next video
(link in the description)
I will state the chain rule formally as a
theorem and try to prove it.
For my first example,
I want to compute the derivative of
√(x³ + 1) with respect to x.
If it were just √x, it would be easier.
The derivative would be 1/2√x.
The chain rule says,
that to take the derivative of the square 
root of a function
instead of just the square root of x,
I should first take the derivative the
same way,
as though the argument of the
square root were the variable.
So 1/2√(x³ + 1).
But then, I have to multiply it by
the derivative of the argument.
In this case, the derivative of x³ + 1.
We know the derivative of x³ + 1 is 3x²,
and that is the final answer.
For my second example, I want to take the
derivative of sin(6x) with respect to x.
The derivative of sin(x) with respect to
x is cos(x).
Side comment: at this point in the
sequence of videos,
I have not started derivatives of
trigonometric functions yet.
Perhaps you already knew this identity.
If you didn't, just accept it for now.
I will justify it soon.
The point of the problem is, once we know
the derivative of sine is cosine,
what is the derivative of sin(6x)?
The chain rule says: first take the
derivative as if the argument, 6x,
were the variable.
I get cos(6x).
And then multiply times the derivative
of the argument. The derivative of 6x.
We know the derivative of 6x,
with respect to x, is 6.
So the final answer is 6cos(6x).
For a third example, let's compute the
derivative of (x² + 1)¹⁰⁰,
with respect to x.
I could expand this, write it as a long
polynomial, and take the derivative,
but I don't want to do that.
Instead, I can use chain rule.
The derivative of x¹⁰⁰ with respect to x
is 100x⁹⁹.
The chain rule says, first pretend the
argument x² + 1 is the variable.
I get 100(x² + 1)⁹⁹.
And then multiply times the derivative of
that argument. The derivative of x² + 1.
We know the derivative of x² + 1 is 2x,
so I get 200x(x² + 1)⁹⁹.
And that is the final answer.
