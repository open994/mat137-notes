In English, the word "series"

When we say a series, we
mean an infinite sum,
whereas when we say a sequence,
we mean an infinite list.
It's a simple difference perhaps.
Here is what an infinite sum
may look like.
I have these numbers a₁, a₂, a₃,
and so on, and I want to add them up;
and here are the same
numbers just as a list.
They could be the same numbers,
but when I think of a sequence,
I'm not trying to add them.
I probably want to compute
the limit of this list.
And when I write them as a series,
I want to add them and I don't
want to compute the limit.
I want to compute the
value of the sum.
Of course the problem
is that a priori,
I don't know what it means
to add up infinitely many numbers.
I know what it means
to add 2, or 3, or 10.

To add up infinitely many terms,

Here is the definition in general.
I want to add up the series (the
infinite sum) from n = 1 to ∞, of aₙ.
First, I'm going to construct an
auxiliary sequence:
the sequence of partial sums.
I'm going to call it Sₖ,

S₁ is simply the
first term in the series,
S₂ is the sum of the
first two terms in the series,
S₃ is the sum of the first

Sₖ is the sum of the
first k terms in the series.
And then I look at this now sequence
(S₁, S₂, S₃ is a SEQUENCE, not a series),
and I compute its limit.
And I'm going to say that this sum
from n = 1 to ∞, of aₙ,
is the limit as k → ∞, of Sₖ.
Now the first part,
computing finite sums, is no problem.
That's something we can always do.
But the second part, the limit, may or
may not exist, like all limits.
If this limit exists, then we say
the series is CONVERGENT,
and that means in that case,
the series represents a number.
But if the limit does not exist we say
the series is DIVERGENT,
and that means in that case,
the series is not a number.
Now the limit may not exist for all the
reasons that limits may not exist.
Perhaps it is ∞, perhaps it is -∞,
or something else.
(perhaps it oscillates in some way)
In all of those cases,
the series is not convergent.
In all of those cases, we cannot pretend
that the series represents a number.

An improper integral may have this form:
the integral of a function f,

And the problem with this integral

Whereas the series may be

Again, the problem is that I

The way I define an improper
integral like this one,
is by first computing a regular
proper integral from a to b,

So I define an improper integral

under the limit that

Similarly, the way I define an
infinite sum, is as a finite sum,
then I take the limit as the
number of terms I add → ∞.
So I define an infinite sum
as a limit of finite sums,
because finite sums are the
ones I know how to compute.
