In this video I will obtain an equation
for the derivative of arcsin.
I have already introduced
the function...
...and explained how to compute with it
in the previous video,
which I will link in the description.
I am going to use a standard trick.
I know the derivative of sin, and I want
to obtain the derivative of arcsin.
Since, in the right domains,
they are inverses of each other,
I know that they are related
by this equation.
If I take the derivative of
both sides of the equation,
I will get a new identity that will
relate the derivative of arcsin
to the derivative of sin.
That’s it.
That’s my plan.
The rest is calculations.
Let’s do it then!
The derivative of the left should be
equal to the derivative of the right.
On the left I have the derivative
of sin of a function.
Using the chain rule,
that is cos of the argument multiplied
by the derivative of the argument.
And on the right, I get 1.
From here I can solve,
and the derivative of arcsin(x) is
1/(cos(arcsin(x))).
I could perhaps leave it as is,
but I can re-write it in a nicer way.
What is cos(arcsin(x))?
For simplicity, so as not to write
arcsin(x) all of the time,
I will give it a name.
I will call it θ.
Then, sin(θ) = x,
and I am looking for cos(θ).
Once I put it this way, I see
that this is a problem in trigonometry.
I know sin of the angle θ, and I want
to write cos of the same angle θ.
There are various ways to do this:
with trig identities,
drawing right triangles,
looking at the unit circle...
I am going to use a trig identity.
I know that cos²(θ) = 1 - sin²(θ);
therefore, cos(θ) is ±√(1 - sin²(θ)),
or simply ±√(1 - x²).
But which one is it?
Plus or minus?
This is a well-defined function.
It can only have one value (one sign),
and I cannot choose + for convenience
and pretend to be done.
I need to justify the sign that I choose.
The key is in the definition of arcsin.
We defined arcsin as the inverse of a
SPECIFIC RESTRICTION of sin:
θ must be between -π/2 and π/2;
and in this region,
(in the first and fourth quadrants),
cos(θ) is always positive or zero.
That’s what I needed to know.
I choose the sign +.
Notice that if I had defined arcsin...
...as the inverse of
any other restriction of sin,
then I might be getting
a different sign here.
Now I can go back to
the original calculation,
and I conclude that the derivative of
arcsin(x), with respect to x,
is 1/√(1 - x²).
And that’s the final answer!
