In the previous video, I
presented the Integral Test:
a theorem that gives a relation between
improper integrals and infinite sums.
In this video, I will present
some examples of how to use it.
As a reminder, here's
what the theorem says.
I begin with a function that's
continuous, positive, and decreasing,
between a real number a, and ∞.
Then I can say, that
the improper integral
obtained by integrating this function
all the way to ∞ is convergent,
IF AND ONLY IF the series obtained
from this function is convergent.
And as notation in that case, we
write a tilde between the two of them.
This doesn't mean
their values are equal,
it means that they are both
convergent (both a number),
or that they are both ∞.
I assume you have watched
the previous video,
where I explain where
this theorem came from.
I will add a link in the description.
So now, let's see some examples of
how to use this in practice.
Most of the time, the
examples will look the same.
I want to know whether a certain
series is convergent or divergent,
and it happens to be easier
to check whether the integral
is convergent or divergent.
The first example is probably
the most important one.
It's the commonly called "p-series".
I want to know when the sum of
1/(powers of n) is convergent,
For every value of p
(a positive real number),
I get a different series
(sum of 1/nᵖ).
For some values of p,
this will be convergent,
for some this will be divergent.
Can I use Integral Test here?
Yes I can.
If I fix p, and I consider the
function f defined as:
f(x) = 1/xᵖ, starting at 1,
then this function
is certainly continuous,
it's positive,
and it's decreasing,
so I can use integral test.
By Integral Test, this series behaves
like the corresponding improper integral,
so it's convergent IF AND ONLY IF
the integral is convergent.
And why is this useful?
Because we already know what the
answer is for the improper integral.
We know that this improper integral is
convergent exactly when p > 1,
and ∞ otherwise.
I will add a link in the
description of this video
to the video where I prove that
for improper integrals,
but it's an easy calculation:
just find the antiderivative
and take the limit.
Therefore, by Integral Test, we get
exactly the same result for the series,
and that's it! Now we have
a new large family of series,
that we know to be convergent.
For the second example,
I will look at a very specific series:
the sum from n = 2 to ∞,
of 1/(n⋅ln(n)).
I want to know whether
it is convergent.
Notice I can not start the index at 1,
since ln(1) is 0,
and I cannot divide by 0,
but this series
starting at 2 makes sense.
I want to use the Integral Test,
so I'm going to look at the function
defined by the same equation:
f(x) = 1/(x⋅ln(x)).
Starting at 2, this function is positive,
decreasing, and continuous,
so I can use the Integral Test.
And by Integral Test, the series will
behave like the improper integral,
so one is convergent
IF AND ONLY IF the other is.
Why is this good?
Because there is something else I can do
for the improper integral,
which is to actually
find an antiderivative,
and that's exactly what I'm going to do.
The integral from 2 to ∞
of this function is defined as
the limit as b → ∞,
of the integral from 2 to b.
So, what I need to do is find
an antiderivative for 1/(x⋅ln(x)),
and then I will evaluate that
antiderivative at b - evaluate it at 2,
and then take the limit as b → ∞.
At this moment, perhaps you see
what the antiderivative is right away,
or perhaps you need
to do guess-and-check,
otherwise try the substitution u = ln(x),
because it's derivative is there.
Either way, pause the video if necessary,
and find the antiderivative.
You will end up getting ln(ln(x)).
It's easy to check that it worked.
The derivative of ln(ln(x))
is (1/ln(x))⋅(1/x).
So now, let's evaluate!
I get the limit
as b → ∞, of ln(ln(b)) - ln(ln(2)),
and as b → ∞, ln(ln(b)) also → ∞,
albeit slowly,
so the improper integral diverges to ∞,
and by Integral Test,
the series diverges to ∞ as well.
That's it!
