In this video, I will
illustrate with an example
how to use the definition
of integral
to prove that a function
is integrable.
In the next video,
I will do the same
for a non-integrable function.
I will work with
the function f,
defined as f(0) = 1,
and for all other values
of x, f(x) = 0.
I want to prove
that f is integrable
on the interval
from -1 to 1.
This is the graph of f.
I will use the definition
of integral.
This means I will calculate
the lower integral,
the supremum of
all the lower sums,
and the upper integral,
the infimum of
all the upper sums.
These upper and lower sums
will be taken with
respect to partitions
of the interval [-1, 1].
The function is integrable on [-1, 1]
if and only if these
two quantities are equal.
I assume you already
knew this definition.
If you didn't, or if
you need a refresher,
watch the video where
I first introduced it.
It is linked in the description.
Let's start with
the lower sums.
Let's take ANY partition.
On any sub-interval,
no matter what,
the infimum is always
going to be 0.
It does not matter if
the partition
includes 0 or not.
Therefore, all the lower
rectangles have height 0,
and the lower sum is 0
for every partition.
EVERY lower sum is zero.
We can calculate
the lower integral
with this information.
The lower integral
is the supremum
of the set of all
lower sums.
But, that is just the set
with a single element, 0.
So, the lower integral is 0.
Let's look at
the upper sums.
They are more interesting.
I have an arbitrary
partition here.
As you know, the
upper sum is the area
of the rectangles
above the graph,
pictured in green.
In this case, there is
one rectangle with height 1.
It is the rectangle for
the sub-interval
that contains the value x = 0.
All the other rectangles
have height 0,
and are not contributing.
Notice that 0 is not a point
in this particular partition.
If I choose to add 0
to the partition,
it will just split the
one rectangle in two,
but it won't change
the upper sum.
Notice also that the
extra points in the partition
are not doing anything.
They do not contribute
extra rectangles,
they do not change
the upper sum.
So, I will ignore them.
Okay then.
The upper sum is the area
of this one rectangle.
The area is height,
which is 1, times width.
What is the width?
It can be bigger,
or smaller.
It can be as big as 2,
if I used the simplest partition:
just the points -1 and 1,
and nothing else.
It can get as small as we want,
but always remaining positive.
It can also take
any values in between,
so anything between
2 (inclusive)
and 0 (not inclusive).
Great! We can now calculate
the upper integral.
It is the infimum of
the set of upper sums.
The set of upper sums
is the interval
from 0, not included,
to 2, included.
And the infimum
of that interval is 0.
Excellent.
The lower and upper
integrals are equal.
Thus, the function
f is integrable on [-1, 1],
and the integral is 0.
We're done!
