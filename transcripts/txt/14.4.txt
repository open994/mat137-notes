In the previous video, I wrote
the first definition
of Taylor polynomials.
In this video,
I will translate it into a second,
equivalent definition,
which is more useful
for computations.
Let's recall what we know.
The n-th Taylor polynomial
for the function f
at the real number a 
is a polynomial
which is an approximation
for f near a of order n.
This means that
as x approaches a,
the difference between
the function and the polynomial
approaches 0 faster
than (x – a)^n.
In the previous video,
I explained why
this limit being 0 means
that the polynomial is a good
approximation for the function.
And I also requested that
the degree be at most n.
As I explained,
this definition encapsulates
exactly
what the important property
of Taylor polynomials is,
why we care about them,
and why they are useful.
However, it is quite abstract
and it is not very useful
for computations.
I cannot easily use it
to compute the Taylor
polynomials of a function.
Based on this property alone,
I don't even know when
these polynomials exist.
My goal is to translate
this property
into a second, equivalent
property that is easier to use,
particularly for computations.
Before doing that, I want to
introduce some new notation.
A function is called C^0
when it is continuous.
Sometimes it is also called
"type C^0" or "class C^0".
It is called C^1
when the derivative exists
and is continuous.
It is called C^2 when the first
and second derivatives exist
and are continuous,
and so on.
It is called C^n when the
first n derivatives
all exist and are continuous.
It is called C^infinity when ALL
the derivatives exist
and are continuous.
I could use all these concepts
at a point, or on an interval.
Why do I introduce these names?
We are going to encounter
quite a few results
that require different amounts
of derivatives
to exist and be
continuous.
It is nice to have a name
for these concepts, that's all.
It will make the statement
of theorems a bit shorter.
Okay. Now that I have this
notation, let's move on.
For now, let's assume that
f and g are C^infinity.
They have all their derivatives.
I want to try and rewrite
this condition,
that the limit as x approaches a
of their difference
over (x – a)^n will be 0.
I want to rewrite this condition
into something simpler,
involving the derivatives
of f and g.
As I said, for now I will just
assume that f and g
have all their derivatives.
Once I finish my exploration,
I will check back and see
which assumptions
I actually used.
Let us call this limit L.
I'll admit that this notation
is a bit problematic
because this limit
may not exist.
But for simplicity, I'll refer
to this expression as L
whether the limit exists or not.
I have the limit of a quotient.
The limit of the denominator is 0.
Since f and g are continuous,
the limit of the numerator
is f(a) – g(a).
IF f(a) – g(a) is not 0,
THEN I will have a non-zero
number divided by zero.
The limit L will be plus
or minus infinity.
Possibly the limit on the left
and the limit on the right
will be different, but in any case,
L will not be zero.
So, let's discard that case.
Let's assume that f(a) = g(a).
In that case, I get 0/0,
an indeterminate form,
and I can try to use
L'Hôpital's Rule.
After using L'Hôpital's Rule,
L becomes...
the limit as x approaches a
of f'(x) – g'(x)
over n(x – a)^{n – 1}
I put an asterisk over
the equals sign
because this is only if
the use of L'Hôpital's Rule
ends up being justified:
if this limit ends up
being a number,
or infinity, or minus infinity.
Otherwise, the use of
L'Hôpital's Rule was not legal,
I could not do this, and I know
nothing about the original limit.
Fortunately, that won't happen,
as I will explain later.
Now, we are exactly in the
same situation as before.
Assuming n is large, the
denominator has limit 0.
The numerator has limit
f'(a) – g'(a),
because f' and g' are continuous.
If f'(a) – g'(a) is not 0,
I get a non-zero number over zero.
The limit is plus or
minus infinity,
possibly different on the
left and on the right,
the use of L'Hopital's Rule
was legal, and L is not 0.
So, let's discard that case as well,
and assume that f'(a) = g'(a).
Then, I have an indeterminate
form of type 0/0, again.
I use L'Hôpital's Rule again.
And I keep going.
After using L'Hôpital's Rule n times,
I get that L equals the limit
as x approaches a of
the difference of the n-th
derivatives on the top,
over n factorial on the bottom.
And since the derivatives
are continuous,
I simply evaluate at a.
Great! I think I have found
the conditions
for the limit L to be 0.
I needed all the previous derivatives
of f and g to agree at a
– this is how I kept getting
indeterminate forms
of type 0/0 and I could
use L'Hôpital's Rule –
and I also need the n-th
derivatives to agree at a,
so that the limit is finally 0.
To be clear, this is an if and only if.
These are necessary
AND sufficient conditions.
I have shown that IF
I have all these conditions,
THEN all the uses of
L'Hôpital's Rule
were justified, and
the limit L is 0.
On the other hand:
what happens if any
of these conditions fail?
If the last condition fails,
the limit L is a non-zero number.
If any of the previous
conditions failed,
then at an earlier stage, one
of the limits would have been
plus or minus infinity,
possibly different
on the left and on the right,
and therefore L wasn't 0.
Finally, notice that
in this whole process,
I have used that f and g
were C^n.
I did not need C^infinity after all,
but I did need C^n.
I have proven a theorem
that is worth stating.
The theorem says: let f and g
be C^n functions at a point a.
Then, the following two
conditions are equivalent.
One: as x approaches a,
the difference between
f and g approaches 0
faster than (x – a)^n,
and two: the first few derivatives
of f and g have the same value at a.
So, g is a good approximation
for f near a IF AND ONLY IF
g and f have the same 
first few derivatives at a.
How good an approximation?
It depends on how many
derivatives they agree on.
I need to acknowledge that
the proof I have written
could be made more formal.
I could use induction
instead of saying
"I repeat the process n times."
But, I am just going to
leave it at that.
In any case, this is exactly
what I was looking for.
I can now take the definition 
of Taylor polynomial,
which I had written using
condition one,
and rewrite it using
condition two instead.
Here we go.
Let a be a real number,
n a natural number,
and f a C^n function at a.
The n-th Taylor polynomial for f at a
is a polynomial, which I
will call P_n,
such that the polynomial
and the function
have the same value at a,
and so do their first derivatives,
their second derivatives,
and so on,
all the way to their n-th derivatives.
...And, in addition, the polynomial
has degree at most n.
As I said at the beginning,
this definition is simpler
than the original one.
I am just specifying the value
of the polynomial
and its first few derivatives at a.
For the purpose of constructing
these polynomials,
for the purpose of computations,
this is more useful
than the original definition.
But I think we should always
keep in mind the original definition
as well; the one in terms
of the limit.
It tells us why Taylor polynomials
make good approximations.
Notice that this is not
a completely new idea.
We've encountered it before.
What would the first
Taylor polynomial be,
according to this definition?
It would be a polynomial with
degree at most 1
whose value at a is f(a),
and whose derivative at a is f'(a).
That is how we defined
the tangent line!
Great. This definition
makes sense.

this one step further
and make it even more concrete,
by providing an explicit formula
to construct these polynomials.
Then, from there, I will
be able to compute examples.
