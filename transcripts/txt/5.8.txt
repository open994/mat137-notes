In this video, I will write a proof
for the Mean Value Theorem.
I already explained the statement
in the previous video,
so this one will ONLY contain the proof.
To begin, let's recall the statement.
I start with a function f, defined on
an interval [a, b] (including the endpoints).
IF f is continuous on the full interval,
and differentiable on the interior,
THEN I can conclude that there
must be a point in the interior
where the derivative takes this value.
Before I write the proof,
as preparation,
I will draw a graph and try to explain
the idea of the proof visually.
Once we understand the idea,
writing the proof will be easier.
Here is the graph of a function
defined on the interval [a, b].
I want to use Rolle's Theorem
to prove the Mean Value Theorem.
But Rolle's Theorem only applies
to functions
that take the same value
at a and b.
So I cannot use it directly on f.
My plan is to construct a SECOND
function on which to use Rolle's Theorem.
The second function, which I will call h,
is the following:
Given a generic value of x here,
h(x) is the vertical distance between
the red line and the graph of f.
Notice that h(a) is zero...
...and h(b) is also zero.
So, I will be able to use
Rolle's Theorem on h.
To write the proof algebraically,
I will need an equation for h.
I can write h(x) as this vertical distance
minus this vertical distance.
Since this is x, the y-coordinate
here is f(x).
So, I can write this vertical
distance as f(x) – f(a).
Now, this piece here is x – a...
...and this vertical distance is
m times (x – a),
where m is the slope of the red line.
But the red line is just the line going
through two points
on the graph of f,
with x-coordinates a and b.
So we know that m = ((f(b) – f(a))/(b – a)
Now, I can write h(x) explicitly,
as this quantity,
minus this quantity.
So, h(x) = f(x) – f(a) – m(x – a).
And now, I am ready!
Here is my plan.
I begin with a function f.
I construct a second function h,
by this equation.
I apply Rolle's Theorem to h.
That will give me some conclusion about
its derivative h'.
Whatever that derivative is, I will transform
it into some conclusion about f'.
That's the idea.
Let's do it!
This is just a reminder of
the statement of the theorem.
To begin the proof, I am going to call
m the quantity ((f(b) – f(a))/(b – a)
I did not need to give it a name,
but I did not want to have to
write the whole thing all the time.
Now, I define a new function h
on the interval [a, b] by this equation.
h(x) = f(x) – f(a) – m(x – a).
I want to apply Rolle's Theorem to h,
on [a, b].
Before I can do that, I need to verify
the three hypotheses of Rolle's Theorem.
First: h is continuous on [a, b] (closed),
because f is continuous on [a, b] (closed).
h is simply made of f, constant terms,
and polynomials.
Similarly, h is differentiable on (a, b) (open),
because f is differentiable on (a, b) (open).
And finally, h(a) is...zero.
Just plug x = a into the definition of h.
And h(b) is...
...also zero.
This requires you to see what m is.
Excellent!
I am allowed to use Rolle's Theorem on h.
I conclude, from Rolle's Theorem,
that there is a number c between
a and b such that
the derivative h'(c) = 0.
But look at the definition of h!
For any value of x,
h'(x) is simply f'(x) – m.
Therefore, h'(c) = 0 is the same as
f'(c) = m.
And that is exactly what
I was trying to show.
This completes the proof.
We are done!
