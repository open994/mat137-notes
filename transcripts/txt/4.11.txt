Ln or log:
what’s the controversy?
There are some SPECIAL BASES
for logarithms,
but we can’t agree on what to call them.
If I have a favourite base, call it: "b",
then I can write exponentials 
with any other base,
in terms of exponentials with base b.
And I can write logarithms 
with any other base,
in terms of logarithms with base b.
So why don’t we find the BEST BASE 
and use it always as the only one?
Mathematicians soon realized that
the base "e" was special,
and they gave a special name to
logarithms with base e: "ln".
(Sometimes read as “lawn”)
This is called the natural logarithm,
although I like to think the "n"
stands for John Napier: 
the inventor of logarithms.
What’s special about e?
Exponentials with base e 
appear naturally as limits.
Some limits involving eˣ, or ln(x),
have nice clean values,
whereas the same limits with 
other bases... not so much.
eˣ and ln(x) have SIMPLER derivatives
than exponentials or logarithms
with any other bases.
eˣ and ln(x) also have beautiful
power series expansions.
The evidence is plenty.
Natural logarithm is natural, and it is
the only logarithm you should ever use.
But wait! Some people disagree.
To understand why,
we have to go back to a time before
computers, or even calculators,
back when everything had to
be computed by hand,
with the help of log tables, 
and slide rules.
Since we use the decimal system 
to write numbers,
the logarithm in base 10,
(sometimes called the "common logarithm"
or "decimal logarithm")
was the most useful 
to do calculations by hand.
It became common to write "log", without
specifying the base, to mean base 10.
Fast forward to the present.
Nobody computes by hand anymore,
and slide rules are museum relics.
There is no longer the same need
for logarithms in base 10.
Modern mathematicians say hey!
"log" by itself should clearly be 
reserved for the most important base,
the only one that matters: "e".
But it is hard to change tradition,
and engineers have never allowed...
...mathematicians to tell them
how to use notation,
so they continue using "ln" instead.
And that is the situation today.
Whenever you see ln(x) OR log(x), they 
both probably mean logarithm in base e.
When you read a book, 
or do math with somebody,
it is probably a good idea to find out
which convention they use.
ln(x) is most commonly used in science
and engineering, but also in calculus!
Probably because most calculus students
are in science and engineering,
rather than math.
log(x) is most commonly used in math.
If you take any math courses
beyond calculus,
they will probably use log to
refer to the natural logarithm.
Which notation do YOU prefer?
