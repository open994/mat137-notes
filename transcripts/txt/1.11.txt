When we write a mathematical proof,
we often need
to use the definitions of the concepts
that appear in the statement.
In this video,
I will illustrate how we can prove
that an example satisfies a definition.
I want to prove
that the function defined by the equation
f(x) = 3x + 7
is increasing on the real numbers,
and I want to do it
directly from the definition.
This proof is going
to be short and simple,
and honestly not that exciting.
But I'm using it to make a point.
I don't need to have learned,
in advance, how to prove
that a function is increasing.
I don't need to have memorized
any template or a structure.
If I know the definition of increasing,
that tells me
what the proof should look like.
That tells me the structure,
and that's all I need.
And the same is going to be true
for any other mathematical concept.
I will begin by recalling
the definition of increasing.
It says that for every real numbers
x_1 and x_2,
IF x_1 is less than x_2,
THEN f(x_1) is less than f(x_2).
THIS is what I want to prove.
I don't know if this is true YET,
so I'm going to make sure
to make that CLEAR by writing
that this is what I WANT to show,
which is often abbreviated WTS.
How will this proof go?
I need to prove that for every pair
of real numbers x_1 and x_2,
this implication is true.
Since I need to prove it for EVERY pair
of real numbers x_1 and x_2,
I begin by FIXING arbitrary values
of x_1 and x_2.
When I write this,
"let x_1, x_2 be reals,"
it means I am fixing them,
and they're generic;
I don't control them.
So my proof works
for EVERY pair of values.
And then, I need to verify
this implication.
In order to prove an implication,
I need to assume the "if" part,
and hopefully conclude the "then" part.
And what comes here in the middle
may be short, may be long;
may depend on the case.
In this case,  it's going 
to be just a little bit of algebra,
and that's it.
This is the structure of the proof.
So, how do I go from x_1 is less than x_2
to f(x_1) is less than f(x_2)?
Well in this case,
since this is a form of f(x),
all I have to do
is multiply; times 3, and add 7.
So if I begin with this inequality,
and multiply BOTH sides; times 3,
I get 3(x_1) is less than 3(x_2).
And if I add 7 to both sides,
I get 3(x_1) + 7 is less than 3(x_2) + 7.
And I am done,
because the left-hand side is f(x_1),
and the right-hand side is f(x_2).
That's it; 
I don't need to do anything else.
The important lesson I want to take
away from this, however,
is that I DIDN'T NEED to MEMORIZE
that this is the structure
of a proof that something is increasing;
if I know what the definition
of "increasing" is,
then the structure is
entirely contained in there.
And THE SAME is TRUE
for ANY other definition 
of ANY mathematical concept.
