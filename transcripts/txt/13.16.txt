In this video, I will
write a proof
of the Absolute Convergence
Test for series.
I already wrote the statement
in the previous video,
and I explained how to use it
and why it's useful.
This time, I want to know
why the theorem is true.
As a reminder, here is
the statement again.
I am looking at a series.
IF the same series
with absolute values,
making all the terms positive,
is convergent,
THEN the original series
must be convergent as well.
Before doing anything else,
I will introduce a new
piece of notation.
Given a series,
I will write "P.T."
to represent the positive terms,
and "N.T." to represent
the negative terms.
I'll acknowledge that this
notation is a bit lazy.
To make sense of it,
I need to assume
that we are talking about one
single series at a time,
and that we have
a good idea in advance
of which series
we are talking about.
But for the purpose of this video
it will be enough,
and it is simple, so I am
going to use it anyway.
Let's look at it with an example.
Let's say we are starting
the alternating harmonic series.
1 – 1/2 + 1/3 – 1/4 and so on.
Then, when I write
"sum of P.T.,"
I mean 1 + 1/3 + 1/5 + 1/7
and so on.
I am keeping only
the positive terms,
in the same positions they were
– in the same order –
and I have removed the rest.
Basically I'm replacing the
negative terms with zeros.
Similarly, when I write
"sum of N.T.,"
I mean -1/2 – 1/4 – 1/6
and so on,
just the negative terms.
Okay. Now that I have
this notation,
I want to ask a question that
will be the key to the proof.
When is it okay to
rewrite a series
as first adding
all the positive terms,
and then adding all
the negative terms?
That's a way to re-order the terms.
Is it okay?
It is certainly okay for finite sums.
Finite sums are commutative
and associative.
If I am adding finitely
many numbers,
I can choose to first add
the positive ones
and then add the negative ones.
But a series is an infinite sum.
An infinite sum can we written
as a LIMIT of finite sums.
So, what happens when I take
the limit of both sides?
On the left, I have the very
definition of the series a_n,
and on the right, I have
a limit of a sum,
which I can rewrite as
a sum of two limits.
Look at that!
Those two limits are,
by definition,
the series of the
positive terms only
plus the series of the
negative terms only.
It looks like I have proven
what I was looking for.
But, there is a BIG gap
in the argument.
Can you spot the error?
In order to split the limit
of a sum
into the sum of two limits,
I need to use a limit law.
As you know,
this requires assuming
in advance that each one
of the two individual limits
exists separately.
In this case, this means
I need to require, in advance,
that the series of only
positive terms be convergent,
and that the series of only
negative terms be convergent.
With that assumption,
I can draw the conclusion
I was looking for.
But without it,
I can say nothing about
the original series.
The original series may still be
convergent or not, I cannot tell.
Okay then!
The key observation I was
aiming for is this:
IF the series of positive terms
AND the series of negative terms
are both convergent,
THEN the original series can
can written as a sum of the two.
Great!
This is the best case.
The worst case
is the opposite.
When the sum of the positive
terms is infinity,
and the sum of the negative
terms is minus infinity,
then the original series
may be anything.
It may end up being
divergent or convergent.
It can still be convergent
if I am adding
the positive and negative terms
in a very specific, balanced order
in which most of the terms
cancel out or something.
That is what happens in
conditionally convergent series,
and is the reason why
conditionally convergent series
are NOT COMMUTATIVE.
But that's a tangent.
If this intrigues you, I invite you

For now, let's go back to the
actual goal of the video.
I wanted to prove the
Absolute Convergence Test.
Now that we have made
the previous observation,
the proof will be very short.
Here it is.
Let's assume that the series
of the absolute values of a_n
is convergent.
Notice that the sum of
the absolute values
of the positive terms, and the
sum of the absolute values
of the negative terms
are both BOUNDED
by the sum of all
the absolute values.
Therefore, by the Basic
Comparison Test,
they are both also convergent.
That means that the sum
of the positive terms
and the sum of the negative terms
are themselves convergent,
because they are equal to
plus (in the first case)
and minus (in the second case)
the previous two sums.
Then, by the key observation
we made earlier,
The original series is equal
to the sum of these two
and it is convergent!
That is what I wanted to prove.
We are done.
