The purpose of this video
is to present
three important properties
of lower and upper sums,
including one property I left
pending in the previous video.
These three properties
will make some calculations
and some proofs easier.
Let's start with an easy one.
For any one partition,
the lower sum is smaller than
or equal to the upper sum.
This seems clear geometrically.
If I am fixing a single partition,
then the area of
the lower rectangles
is less than or equal to the
area of the upper rectangles.
Why?
Focus on a single sub-interval.
The lower rectangle is literally
contained in the upper rectangle.
The height of the lower
rectangle is the infimum
of the function on
that sub-interval.
The height of the upper
rectangle is the supremum
of the function on
that sub-interval.
The infimum is always less than
or equal to the supremum,
and therefore the sum
of the areas
of the lower rectangles is
less than or equal to
the sum of the areas
of the upper rectangles,
which is what I wanted to show.
Nothing mysterious here.
Let's try something
more interesting.
Can we compare lower
and upper sums
for DIFFERENT partitions?
And, what makes one partition
"better" than another partition?
There is a sense in which
some partitions
are better than others,
because their lower
and upper sums
will be better estimates
for the integral.
Naïvely, it looks like a partition
that has more points
will be better,
But it not merely about
the number of points.
Here is a new definition.
Given two partitions P and Q,
I am going to say Q
is "finer than" P
when P is a subset of Q.
For example:
This partition Q is finer
than this partition P.
It means that Q has all the
points in P, plus a few more.
It does not mean merely
that Q has more points than P.
How does this help?
Let's see what happens
when I add
a single extra point
to a partition.
Begin with this partition P,
with five points and
four sub-intervals.
I am shading the area
of the lower rectangles
for the lower sum.
Now, let's add one single
extra point, which I will call t.
This extra point breaks one
sub-interval into two,
and creates one extra slice.
What happens to the area
of the lower rectangle?
It increases a bit.
This will always happen
when I add one extra point.
The lower sum will
increase, or stay the same.
If I add a few points,
one at a time,
the same things happens as well.
We have found the second
important property.
Let P and Q be two
partitions of [a, b].
IF Q is finer than P,
THEN Q produces a
better lower sum than P.
A better lower sum is
a larger lower sum.
Also, Q produces a
better upper sum than P.
A better upper sum is
a smaller upper sum.
I have only explained why this
is the case for lower sums,
but I trust you can repeat
the same reasoning
for the upper sums.
This is helpful when one
partition is finer than the other.
But what if I have
two partitions
and neither of them is
finer than the other?
Can I still compare their
lower and upper sums?
In general, without knowing
anything else,
I cannot compare the
two lower sums,
and I cannot compare the
two upper sums,
but I can always say
that EVERY lower sum is
less than or equal
to EVERY upper sum.
This is going to be
the third property.
If I take any two
partitions P and Q,
without knowing anything else,
I can guarantee that
the P-lower sum
is less than or equal
to the Q-upper sum.
In other words,
every lower sum
is less than or equal
to every upper sum.
If you try to prove
this algebraically,
by setting two arbitrary partitions...
...it looks messy.
I wouldn't know
how to start.
But there is a clever trick
to make this
into a very simple proof.
Here it is.
I know how to compare
lower and upper sums
between two partitions when
one is finer than the other.
So let's define a third partition
that is finer than both.
Let us call R the
union of P and Q.
R is a new partition.
R is finer than P, and
R is finer than Q.
Then, look at what happens:
The P-lower sum is less than
or equal to the R-lower sum,
thanks to Property 2,
which is less than or equal
to the R-upper sum,
thanks to Property 1,
which is less than or equal
to the Q-upper sum,
thanks to Property 2.
That completes the proof.
There we have it.
These are the three
important properties
of lower and
upper sums.
They come in handy
in various situations.
In particular, I had
assumed Property 3
when I defined integral,
in order to conclude that
the lower integral of a function
is always less than or equal
to its upper integral.
